Chapter XXII. PARTY POLICY AT THE BALLOT BOX
§1. Organization and Policy
Apart from the rights and the necessity of appearing as a separate and independent party, the party policy also requires further explanation, insofar as it must govern the advance to the ballot box. Here, too, the sharp distinction must be kept in view between the two different natures that come to expression in the appearance of parties at the ballot box. A party in the political field can either be no more than a variation of related parties, or it can stand in principle opposed to a series of parties based on entirely different foundations. If we take no account of any of the parties that have their roots in the revolutionary principles of 1789, and set against them the parties that cling to the old Christian starting point, then there can certainly be variations on both sides. Among the parties of the Revolution, whose year of birth is 1789, there can mainly be three formations, depending on whether one forms the rear guard, the middle corps, or the vanguard, with a certain degree of difference. But an equally pronounced degree of difference can just as well occur among the parties that do not greet their year of birth in 1789, but in the year one of Bethlehem. Although all political parties that owe their origin to the rise of Christianity go back to Bethlehem, this does not at all rule out the often so strongly pronounced difference between those who lead the way, form the middle, or bring up the rear. Upon closer inspection, there then runs a straight line that distinguishes the two triads of groups from each other, so that these move along the two sides, on the one hand the three Christian formations, and on the other hand the three Revolutionary formations. It is precisely this that so often gives occasion for impure mixing in the ballot box policy. The mixing is less pronounced among the parties that form the two sides of the dividing line, but the tendency towards it comes out repeatedly, both in the rear guard and in the vanguard of both, fundamentally different formations. The Conservatives of both groups easily close ranks, and often with such a preference for each other, to defend their backward position. And just as frequently, among the Democrats on both sides of the line, the urge to unite strongly appears, to defend themselves against those who favor a more backward attitude.

§2. Not Always Excluded
More serious attention must be paid to this at the ballot box policy, because it is by no means certain that a political party can and may always unconditionally lend itself to cooperation with a variation in the political field that shares the same proposal with it. It requires no further indication that in itself and as a rule, cooperation with what is closest to you in principle will be recommended. However, it does not at all follow from this that this must and will always be the rule, regardless of all the circumstances. There can even be temporary cases where clinging to what is fundamentally congenial to you would weaken and ultimately disarm you. Take as an example the emergence of the socio-economic question. This question came to the fore as a result of two historical causes. The first lay in the gradually much broader education of the youth, whereby even among the lower classes a development was gradually reached that of itself brought the right to have a say. Now it lay in the nature of the matter that on both sides of the great dividing line, the conservative groups felt this urge of social life less, or perceived it with reluctance. §6. Agreement on equal terms. Now this was in conflict with all sense of fairness. Three factions that profess to possess a unity of political roots can only appear together and go to the ballot box successfully if it is met, and indeed by agreement met, with these three conditions: first, that the mutual strength of the numbers be firmly established through serious electoral organization; second, that the candidates of the three factions remain equal in number with the figure of this investigated and controlled numerical strength; third, that one accepts a program for which each makes a sacrifice, but for which also joint gain is held out in prospect. The first of these three conditions has, as far as possible, been met both by the Anti-Revolutionary and the Roman Catholic side, but not by the Christian Historical side. The Anti-Revolutionary party is able to meet this to a certain extent through its very extensive local organization in 600 electoral associations. The Roman Catholic party largely meets this requirement through its ecclesiastical organization; but the Christian Historical party falls short in this first condition. It goes almost entirely on guesswork, and all too often overestimates its demands. This has also been the reason why the second condition, which we mentioned, could not come to its right from that side. Not knowing its numerical strength, it estimated it far too high, and this was the cause that one constantly sought to trim down the number of its candidates, without being able to devise a means to settle the dispute that constantly arose from this.

§9. Proportional Representation. Since 1870, when the writer became acquainted with Hodge’s ideas on Proportional Representation, he has consistently pointed out how the introduction of this system of proportionality could become the most decisive means to definitively end much of what was and remained improper in the political party struggle. A system of proportionality, as will soon, we may hope, also become our part in the Netherlands, offers the great advantage that it removes the fierce struggle from the ballot box and, if it must still continue, transfers it to the States General. But one is certainly mistaken who may have imagined that, if this new system were to go through, all party struggle would now be over for good. This, of course, cannot be, for the principles remain in their antithesis and the variations retain their color differences, but there is still this truth in the assertion, that the curse of dishonesty, which has burned on it for so long, can now finally be averted from the ballot box, and that the antithesis will continue to hold sway in the political field, but with this difference, that the antithesis will now leave the ballot box to concentrate in the clubs, in order to remain unchangeably in control, especially in our Second Chamber, but in part also in the First Chamber. The writer also considers himself fortunate to have been able to thank that from the outset his eye was opened to the so serious significance of the Proportional system, and that what had been withheld from us for too long by a party struggle that deserved to be condemned, will now finally be obtained. Whether the way in which it is now being introduced to our liking is the most desirable, is left aside here. The scissors must be extremely fine that can bring about the absolutely required and impeccably accurate division in this matter. But this can never condemn the principle. What has remained below perfection can now be elevated to the sphere of the perfect. Therefore, nothing may be deducted from what has now been granted to us. Compulsory voting and women’s suffrage may almost completely ruin it, and the maintenance of the individualistic suffrage makes many clear-sighted people doubt, but so much has now been achieved, that the immorality, which has almost completely ruined our ballot box struggle for so many years, is now being removed from the ballot box.

§11. The current regulation. This newly formulated regulation is based on the fundamental idea that no attack on our independent character as a Coalitional party, with a view to our historical past from the 16th century, and no less with a view to our principle of loyalty to God’s Word, is permitted, under any pretext whatsoever. Is it not undeniable that an accord entered into with a view to a general election can hardly be anything other than a positive accord, determining what one will have to do if one is to support a Cabinet that is of the same spirit, then it follows from this that in the event of a setback at the ballot box, the accord entered into can no longer serve its purpose, and that one will then have to hastily proceed to conclude a new accord, in which the attitude to be taken towards the non-kindred Cabinet is determined. It should even be added that in such a position it would be desirable to appoint a joint standing committee...

DEFECTS IN THE ACCORD - IS ACCORD STILL NECESSARY?
Many expect that, if the Constitution is revised, and the party truce is maintained, as may be assumed, there will already be a next year, after the election campaign, no more talk of alliance or even of electoral consultation under the leadership of the organized parties. Each independent party, it is said, will then operate entirely on its own. Consultation between like-minded or even other parties will then only be possible in the Chamber and in no case during an election campaign. Everyone will go their own way. The voters will receive lists from their own circle or faction. They will fill them in in the most promising manner, and the result will show by itself how many votes, and consequently how many parliamentary seats, they can count on. Even on a superficial view, this is not entirely correct. Suppose that the common vote comes to 20,000, then it is quite conceivable that if there are three parties with mutual interests, party A retains 15,000 votes without benefit, party B 10,000 and party C 5,000. Without further regulation, in this way the allied parties of the Right could lose 30,000 votes. This damage, which would naturally accrue to the benefit of the opposing parties, could be averted if the related parties agreed among themselves as to whom they wish to place on their list as a collector of the other, lost votes. Naturally, the choice here would have to be fixed on a candidate of that party who could dispose of the highest unused figure. A measure, it is true, of small alloy, but as experience has taught, in the Netherlands the majority in the Second Chamber can hang on a single vote. To look sharply at the small things is therefore always advisable with us at the Ballot Box. But even if one disregards this, there can never be a question of any party, in exercising its electoral rights, abstaining from indicating its own tone.

It is therefore completely incorrect to assume that the like-minded parties, as far as the election campaign is concerned, could leave everything blue and could only proceed to political consultation in the Chamber. One may not even for a moment assume the appearance of this. Even after the amendment of the electoral law, it remains inexorably necessary that each party determine for itself what the task will be that the soon-to-be-elected Members of Parliament will have to take upon themselves. Each party must therefore, before the election, give an account of what it deems desirable and necessary in the interest of the country. It will therefore have to design, under this new star, a program of action, just as it does now, and when this program of action has been prepared and regulated by the Deputies’ meeting, then of course each electoral association that has joined will, just as it does now, be obliged to investigate to what extent the candidates it intends to place on the list can be regarded as the men from whom an action in the sense of such a program may be expected. If this is to lead to an effective result, then the like-minded or allied parties, for whatever reason, must be well aware of what is sought in the list candidates as attractive. An accord, not to deceive the voters, but to determine the conditions of the now isolated voters, will therefore remain entirely the same as it is now. The voters must know what they can expect from each other’s support; and in presenting their candidates on the list, they must be sufficiently assured that these candidates, if they become Members of Parliament, will respond in sympathy of purpose to what the voters expect. It is therefore incorrect to assume that the election campaign will be carried on by the various parties entirely separately from each other. Although it is true that the definitive agreement will only be able to be reached in the Chamber itself, yet even at the ballot box, not personnel, but a political accord must set the tone. A party that would neglect this would come out in a loss of a part of its political power.

FRAGMENTATION
§14. Fragmentation. A serious concern arises from the system that is about to be introduced—that, whether we like it or not, a daring “Sonder-bündlerei” could lead to a partial disintegration of the party. A group, however insignificant, could easily, through collaboration, manage to cast enough votes in a certain region of the country to ultimately secure the required quota and obtain a seat. The possibility of this is undeniable. And it is this possibility that so easily leads to attempting such a ballot box isolation. The danger threatens from two sides. In the first place, there may be an individual who would consider his highest ideal achieved if he could become a Member of Parliament. Such a case was always conceivable, but now even more so than before, because the financial position of a Member of Parliament has markedly improved. There was an allowance of 2,000 guilders, now it amounts to 3,000 guilders, and in addition, not only travel expenses, but also a possible pension that can amount to a certain amount, high enough to maintain one’s life in old age. Both the urge to enter Parliament for a special conviction and the desire to improve one’s financial position can thus work together, and so it could be imagined that a political eccentric would work his way into Parliament. This cannot be prevented with a single person, as it is certain that in the long run the chance of success for such eccentrics will become less and less, and the freedom of choice will decline rather than rise. But even if the outcome would soon cut off such isolation by itself, it is very conceivable that in the first onslaught a dozen or so of such individualists would attempt it. And if they then succeed, even if only a very few, they can still cause, by such action, a fragmentation to set in at the very first onset, which will cost us the loss of four or five seats. Not that all four or five would be elected. On the contrary, more than one or two such things rarely succeed. Belgium, right next to us, provides proof of this. But our party would then still lose a significant number of votes in total, and the consequence of this is that these eccentrics themselves will run against us and offer support to the left-wing groups. And now do not say that all parties are initially exposed to this danger, and that the loss of the one will compensate for the loss of the other—never forget that individualism speaks very strongly in our circles, so that comparatively the danger lurking here is greater for us. Later, it will disappear, just as it has almost completely ceased to work in Belgium in the end. Only at the beginning can such damage be done, and that is why it is important to be mindful of it.

§15. District division. Just as every party has an organization, so the Anti-Revolutionary party also has one that is directly related to the district division prescribed by the electoral law. Many have only one division for both the House and the States, but there are also fifteen in double form, one for the House and the other for the States. Now, however, these electoral districts will be abolished, and a much more limited division will take their place. This naturally leads to the question of whether it will be necessary for our party organization to bring it into line with this new legal division. Of course, this would be strictly necessary if the number of votes were rounded off and finalized per electoral district, according to the new division, in such a way that it would not remain inactive in any new district, in an absolute sense. However, this will not be the case in such a strict sense. A completely new district division for the Anti-Revolutionary party is therefore not strictly necessary. Maintaining our existing division would even provide the Anti-Revolutionary party with the not insignificant advantage that the proven leadership, which has been working regularly for about twenty years per district, could remain what it was. Yet it will be advisable for our party to also seriously consider this new emerging issue and consult the district administrations about it. If modification proves desirable and promises us gain, then nothing would prevent us from making the necessary changes. Perhaps it would even be better in any case to live in a division that has lost its legal basis.

DISTRICT DIVISION - THE COMPELLED VOTERS
§16. The Compelled Voters. If the duty to vote, as may be presumed, also goes away, then our Members of Parliament will certainly bear no responsibility for this, for our Party has always strenuously opposed it. But now that this voting compulsion has come, we will also have to reckon with it. For this confronts us with the fact that likely 15 to 20% of the registered voters would rather stay home, since they have no interest in the electoral process, but now must come forward. Partly these are voters who do not care to take the time, and do not want to leave their affairs for a trip to the ballot box. Partly they are persons of weak health, who, if the weather is unfavorable, would rather not venture out. Partly they are the indifferent, who do not concern themselves with politics, even mocking it for years. And finally, there are also those persons who are driven by their own opinion and yet see no chance of succeeding with that opinion. Four categories, to which is finally added the larger quantity of those who feel that they are not up to par, and therefore are embarrassed about how they should vote, a conviction that then leads them to prefer to stay home. Yet all of these must now go to the ballot box. No more fetching is needed. They now come of their own accord. But now the point is that their vote does not slip away in the wrong direction. This now brings a very serious duty for our electoral associations, to make accurate lists of all such stay-at-homes, and not just in the election month, but one or two years in advance, to exert influence on them from afar, to enlighten them, to provide them with the necessary reading material, to visit them, and to win them over, so that at least they will no longer act without some awareness of what they are doing. A very serious task for our electoral associations, but really a task for our organization. It is precisely with a view to this that the necessity has already been pointed out from more than one side, to appoint two permanent agents, something for which a large part of the funds that will have to flow from the entire country to the common war chest will have to be used. Understand well, therefore, that from now on it will not be half the work that we have thus far defended, but on the contrary, a doubling of the task that awaits us.

Especially with a view to the compelled voters, who must now come forward on pain of punishment by the judge, it was excellent that the Propaganda Clubs are now officially recognized in our Statutes as one of the most desirable manifestations in the electoral field. §18. Holy Inspiration. We conclude this explanation, as regards the electoral action, with the prayer that the holy character that our political struggle has always borne may in no way be disrupted, but may, in the seriousness of the times, increasingly inspire our men in higher and lower circles. It has been the misfortune of such a mighty Conservative party that it has always counted politics apart from the Almighty. Hence its inglorious downfall. Insofar as the Anti-Revolutionary Party has also contributed to the collapse of their palaces and the rise of their huts, it thinks of the exactly opposite endeavor, to always begin with the God of our Fathers, and to beg from Him the watchword for our political conduct. Religion first, and politics derived from it! Let this remain our watchword. The Word of God in Holy Scripture remains above all political calculation, ever resounding above us. The “nil desperandum Deo” (never despair in God) was thus the ever-victorious leading power. May this remain so!
