§1. Cleanliness. The honor belongs to Calvinism that it constantly urged for purity and cleanliness. In our country, in Scotland and in the United States of North America, Calvinism exerted the mightiest influence, and in connection with this it is precisely in these three countries that cleanliness has always flourished most in domestic life; and it may even be said that among these three, the Netherlands still enjoys the highest reputation. To a certain extent it must even be acknowledged that the Dutch housewife was not always free from all exaggeration in the matter of cleanliness; something that must be emphasized all the more, since it is undeniable that in our households in the 18th century the care for cleanliness in clothing and table setting, in corridors, stairs and rooms, even up to the attics and lofts, often weighed heavier than the care for the cleanliness of the body. At least the bath came to us only later. But apart from this, it may nevertheless be praised that even domestic cleanliness constantly contributed greatly to maintaining public health at a high level. In this direction, moreover, one has constantly progressed further, especially in the second half of the previous century; the consequence of which was that much illness was prevented or mitigated; that finally the mortality rate among us declined remarkably; much more sharply even than one had previously considered possible. §2. The Duty of the Government.
That the Government has a serious obligation with regard to public health finds its immediate explanation in the contagious character that attaches to many diseases. Everyone is exposed to this contagion who comes into contact with an infected person in any way. Even a letter has more than once been the carrier of infectious matter, as in the case of scarlet fever. It is the living together in the same village or city, and the intercourse resulting from this, from which the danger so often arises. This duty arises, on the one hand, because the danger often comes from society itself, before it can be recognized, and on the other hand, because you lack the power to isolate the evil. From this it directly follows that when the danger arises from society, only the Government, which is entrusted with the formal regulation of this society, can step in to protect you. Private initiative cannot save itself here. The Government must protect what stands in an organic life-relationship against the danger of this relationship. This compels it to impose on physicians the obligation to report every manifestation of contagious disease; to give such public notice of this that everyone can be on their guard against the danger; and, if necessary, to isolate the sick person in a barracks or hospital, if their home is too small to separate them from their housemates. Especially in the outbreak of epidemics, the authority of the Government must sometimes extend very far. Even the “my home is my castle” principle cannot always be so strictly respected, since strict respect for your personal independence could become the cause that either your housemates or your neighbors are placed in mortal danger. A second reason for this duty of the Government arises from the fact that medical and pharmaceutical science is not always self-sufficient. In the cities, there is less fear of this, as there is more to be earned here. In more remote areas, on the other hand, and in small villages, this is not the case, and therefore such urgent supervision and provision is necessary here, so that the necessary help does not fail. Something else to which others must also pay attention is that neither physicians nor pharmacists abuse their function or business. We have already pointed this out in the case of abortion, but the sale of poisons cannot be left unregulated either. In fact, we find ourselves in such a state that if the Government were to withdraw its hand, not only would often be lacking what is indispensable, but the available could also give rise to far-reaching abuse. Here too, of course, it is extremely difficult to draw the exact line between what the Government has to do and what it has to leave. Especially in America, the physician lives in a somewhat freer position; with us the bond is usually drawn very tight. But although we cannot completely avoid a certain exaggeration here, in principle we must also confess that the Government has to watch over the entire field of the health of the People, which is organically connected and forms a unity even in a physical sense.

§3. Consumption’s Cancer. One of the most dangerous diseases with regard to public health is consumption, which is still constantly claiming victims in our country in whole groups. It is so dangerous because it propagates itself in families, not certainly through heredity, but because the disease of consumption, unwittingly and unintentionally, brings about the danger of contagion for our household members as a matter of course. Mere breathing can already infect. When the cough starts to work more strongly, the atmosphere in the household takes in the poisonous elements. And even if one is very careful with the excretions, they disperse in the air and bring the danger where no one suspects it. Now, in well-to-do households, one can isolate a room for such a patient and keep him as much as possible in isolation, but in small households this is already very difficult, and with the poor there is no question of it. Hence the constant efforts that are being made to place consumptive patients in an institution, and thus to put the family in safety from the danger of contagion. Yet mutual aid can only provide very partial and insufficient help here. The annual costs that the stay in such an institution requires are far too high even for the fairly well-to-do bourgeoisie. More well-to-do families must then step in. Philanthropy must provide help. But even so, the masses are not helped. The disability insurance in Germany has succeeded in reducing its expenditures by taking under its care those who were in danger or posed a danger; but even here the Government will not be able to refrain. Its vocation demands more of it. Partly through care for better housing, as we shall see later; but also through supervision and isolation. Fortunately, it may also be said that, thanks to this increased care, the curse that consumption so powerfully attacked our people is gradually losing in intensity. When we see the mortality figures from pulmonary tuberculosis stand at 7,250 in 1909, and already four years later at 6,557, then there is a gain to be noted that is gratifying and gives hope that continued care will be able to push back this national scourge even much further. A decline of almost 700 cases in four years, and that despite an increase in population in those years, is something for which we must thank God.
Alongside consumption stands cancer, as still the strongest endemic evil threatening us. Fortunately, it is reported that the power to exorcise cancer too would have been discovered; but the overall results remain gloomy. If we also make a comparison here of the mortality rates of 1909 and 1913, there is no trace of exorcism of the evil, and even a demonstrable worsening. In 1909 the mortality rate for cancer stood at 6,012, and in 1913 it had already climbed to 6,748 - an increase of 736 in four years’ time. Here too, therefore, the vocation of the Government remains to exorcise this serious national disease, if possible, with God’s help.

§4. Vaccination. The cowpox disease has both an epidemic and an endemic character. In countries and regions where vaccination is not applied as a rule, this evil plague still occurs, though on a smaller scale, while the cowpox disease gains a much greater expansion and can become epidemic. The main issue for us in this disease is the question of direct or indirect compulsion. The character of this compulsion is direct when vaccination is made compulsory by law within a certain period after birth; indirect, on the other hand, as with us, when school attendance is prohibited for the unvaccinated. We have no objection to vaccination itself. The appeal to Job XII:6, where it says, “Those who provoke God have assurances,” is in no way applicable here. What Job cries out here means something quite different. It is Job’s bitter complaint that God has so ordained it that the pious suffer and the godless enjoy the joys of life or seem to possess assurances against all adversity. Whether by vaccination the latent germ of the disease can be awakened and driven out is a purely medical question, and the outcome seems to show that vaccination applied generally reduces the prevention of the disease. But the quite different question remains whether vaccination, while warding off the smallpox, may not have other detrimental effects, and this too the medical science must determine. Likewise, the neglect of repetition cannot be defended against.

§5. Epidemic Diseases. When the country is visited by a serious epidemic disease, the calling of the government takes on an even more comprehensive character. Here one has to reckon with epidemics of a milder and more deadly character. Scarlet fever can locally and temporarily manifest itself much more severely than is commonly the case. In 1911 the deaths from scarlet fever amounted to only 113. Right after that, in 1912, this figure rose to over 200. The same is the case with influenza. From this 298 persons died in 1912, while in 1911 the figure was 767 - more than double. Measles are usually spoken of less, because it seems as if everyone in turn must go through this disease; yet it must not be overlooked that to this affliction no less than 616 children of the male and 580 of the female sex, so together almost 1200 children, fell victim in 1913.

§6. The Law of Moses. Of the dreaded epidemic diseases, cholera alone is still reckoned among us as being of a more serious character, the bitter devastation of which I have twice experienced. If we now read in Leviticus XIII et seq. what was commanded in the Mosaic legislation, under divine authority, for the combating of leprosy and other afflictions, then it becomes most evident that all assertions, as if the conscious and energetic combating of such epidemic diseases were a striving against God, and therefore unlawful, are much rather out of the Evil One than in harmony with Scripture. There is a demonic power which, as a consequence of sin, in all manner of ways threatens human life and well-being, and therefore it rests upon us as a stern duty to avert, as far as lies in our power, all danger to life, both in ourselves and in those entrusted to our care. This duty rests in the first place upon the father of the family, but by implication also upon the patriarch for his whole clan, and upon the government for the entire people. When such an epidemic disease breaks out with any force, the population must be warned immediately, the physicians must be obligated to report all cases forthwith, strict disinfection and removal of feces must be provided for, and where distressing poverty reigns, better nourishment must be supplied, the sick must be isolated as much as possible, and when death follows, it must be seen to that the body is interred as speedily as possible. Especially the poorer, poorly housed population must then be able to count on all-sided assistance, and the government must be vested with the authority, where the carelessness of one citizen could harm another, to intervene for the protection of the endangered. The example of what was permitted, nay commanded, to the government in Israel in such a case, speaks here, for him who says he submits to God’s Word, so strongly that he cannot hesitate. In the face of the outbreak of a serious epidemic disease, the government has not only the right but the duty to take all measures necessary for the protection of the public health, even if these measures should temporarily restrict the freedom of the individual.

§9. Improvement of Housing Conditions. What was discussed in the previous section is directly connected to what awaits the government’s care for the improvement of housing. Fortunately, we are on a better path here, and not the least has the Law that came into being in 1901 for this purpose opened up better paths. Even if we admit that this Law too greatly encroached on the right of ownership and handed over as worthless what still represents real value, it is nevertheless also recognized on our part that it is this Law that has put us on the better track. For it clearly opens the eyes to the very serious danger that the miserable housing conditions pose, both for morality and for health, to the less privileged population. Where father, mother, and children, as is often the case, have to spend the night together in a small room, it cannot be otherwise but that the feeling of shame suffers, and all health is impaired. Of strict and punctual secrecy, for the growing youth, for all that touches the sexual life, there can then be no question, and hardly any thought can be given to the necessary refreshment of the air. The undeniable fact that precisely among that part of the population that has to live in such hovels, the mortality rate, especially among children, climbs to the highest proportions, has also often taken on disquieting dimensions. In all corners and crevices of the tiny dwelling, unhealthy substances and even gases develop; and by no means so rarely is the condition that the Minister of the Word also encounters in such dwellings in his house visits, in a literal sense the very opposite of what it should be according to the requirements of Leviticus XIII et seq. We do not conceal in the least that a solution to the housing problem in itself does not offer the slightest guarantee that the poisonous sting will be drawn from the population problem. Yet it also remains a matter of the highest importance that not only in our large cities, but also thoroughly in the countryside, a steady improvement in housing conditions be strived for. Landowners who have workers living on their land are indebted to their workers to let them live as befits a child of man and as one who must live a healthy life. §21. Unemployment.
The most dreadful evil in the realm of labor is unemployment; can the Government also be held responsible for this wicked evil? In part, undoubtedly. An overly driven system of free trade and free movement can deprive work from those who would otherwise have it in abundance. Free trade can result in countless goods being “made” across the borders, so that there is no more labor to be performed here in the country. One sees this most simply in the case of sawed timber. If the unsawed beams come to us, then the wages for sawing can be earned here. If, on the other hand, the sawed timber is supplied to us, then this wage for our workers is gone. The imports from Germany, France, and England are not offset by our exports to those same countries, at least not in terms of manual labor. Especially for a country that is poor in raw materials, it naturally lends itself to greatly increased imports. It is also beyond doubt that our provision of our needs for food, drink, clothing, household goods, technical aids, etc. comes to a much larger extent from abroad than from our own soil or our own factories. Whoever would go through shop after shop in the Kalverstraat in Amsterdam or the Hoogstraat in Rotterdam and examine which goods and wares are of domestic origin and which are of foreign provenance, would come to a painfully conclusive result for our own national labor. The free traffic works in the same direction. From abroad, many workers come into our country, and also office personnel who are better trained and more capable, and thus take positions that would otherwise be filled by our own countrymen. Above all, the ease with which our cultured public speaks foreign languages contributes to this. Indeed, without exaggeration, it can be said that, in this respect, the situation is now rather the reverse of what it used to be. Formerly, the fact that something was of Dutch manufacture increased its price in London and Cologne; now our fading nationality tends to make many young ladies, and also many gentlemen, prefer anything that comes from Paris or London, and to have an almost unfavorable opinion of anything that is of domestic manufacture.

§24. The wage. The abolition of slavery, which had become an abomination, and also the abolition of serfdom and similar forms of attachment of those who lived and worked on the land to the owner of the land, has raised the lower classes of the people psychologically in an incredible way, but physically has subjected them to an equally incredible risk. §29. Strikes and lockouts.
If this mutual open-hearted trust had governed the situation, the very idea of the indispensable strike that now surrounds us would never have arisen, or at least never have been put into practice. As it now stands, it was not to be avoided. Under the changed conditions of the labor process, abuses often arise on the part of the employers. The English School of Stuart Mill had bewildered the minds. It all came down to a struggle of individuals. Whoever held out the longest won the game. The fittest was the victor, and the less well-equipped succumbed to his fate and perished. This has then led to the disappearance of all mutual trust, and the manufacturers, as the stronger ones, have drawn the workers into the struggle for survival, and according to the rule that the survival of the fittest governs the entire economic situation, have exploited the domestic industry, the woman, the children and finally also the male factory workers in an unpardonable way. Many of them felt this themselves, but it seemed to them that there was no escape from this economic law. Everyone did so, and so did they too. A mighty reaction has then naturally arisen against this. Eminent men who stood outside it took up the cause of the exploited working class. Despite all powerlessness, this one thing always remained, that the employer or manufacturer needed the workers. If one had set out to threaten the mighty capitalist with the loss of his workforce by limiting his needs, by serious saving and by close cohesion, there would have been hope. This, then, was the strike, and already at the Social Congress in 1891 it was clearly stated on our side that the strike is not to be rejected as unlawful. Not that the strike could become the panacea for all evils. Rather, the workers have also increasingly come to see that the strike is by no means the always valid, generally applicable means of relief. It also requires a spirit of cooperation among the workers, which only rarely works through. If this does not succeed, then the one who does not readily join in gives the impression of a “strikebreaker.” In this strikebreaker there is then nothing but betrayal of the sacred cause, which has not infrequently led to a vehmgericht and to murder.

§30. Counter-action of the employers.
And in addition to this, the clearer the insight into the situation on both sides becomes, the more the “lockout” on the part of the employers can thwart any strike almost completely. The strike requires a free, strong development of moral power. One already has not too much of it, and now one would have to set aside from the already meager wages for a long time in order to make the strike possible. In the strike weeks themselves, the family lives in great distress, and the strike leader, who has to keep the troops together, is often in a very difficult position. The lockout, on the other hand, does not require this moral effort, and the employer can simply close his factory and wait until the workers, driven by hunger, give in. This is why the lockout has become a much more effective weapon in the hands of the employers than the strike in the hands of the workers.

§33. The Trade Union Organization
The right way to lasting improvement of fate remains, after all, the trade union organization. That it can also decide to strike, we do not deny; but as soon as the strike emanates from the trade union organization, it stands under more reliable leadership. Precisely because it does not aim at general improvement, but occupies itself solely with a particular trade, the trade union organization has a number of men who enjoy great confidence among their trade colleagues, who are well informed about the state of affairs in the trade, and who possess sufficient knowledge of the state of the trade throughout the country, and even beyond our borders, to judge what can be dared and what should be discouraged. Moreover, the trade union organization has this great advantage, that it enhances the competence and thus the economic value of the personnel, and on that basis may make higher demands. It develops the workers, trains them, and thereby gives them a higher significance in social life. Of course, not all its members are skilled laborers, but the guiding thought in the trade union organization is then to pursue this as a goal. Naturally, the trade union organization is less political in nature. Not what comes up at the meetings or comes to the fore in the press occupies it. For the most part, it confines itself to what concerns the one trade for which it acts. The more this trade union organization understands its vocation, the more at home it will be in everything that pertains to the trade.

§35. The Duration of Labor. Duration of labor and wages are two sides of the same matter, which are also directly connected with each other. If one considers, however strictly incorrect, the labor performed in service as the sale of strength, it follows from this that both the effort shown in the labor and the duration of that effort determine the value of what has been delivered. Suppose the effort in labor is set at one to three energies, as a, b, or c, depending on whether the worker dawdles, works through, or surpasses himself, then this, calculated according to the duration of time, will give a result of time multiplied by energy, and the wage will have to be regulated according to the proportion of the two moments, energy and time. Suppose the price of labor, according to the energy, is 25, 50 or 75 cents, then you only have to multiply these three moments by the duration of time of 1–12 hours to determine the price of the delivered product per day, and thus indicate the amount of the wage. But it also follows from this that the worker must relate the increase in his wage and even the normal calculation of his wage both to the energy he has shown and to the time spent. A first-class worker who worked with three energies could, in eight hours’ time, produce more labor than a slower worker in twelve. In pressing for a reduction in the duration of labor, the rule must therefore always be that the worker must also produce more. This now depends on a proper apprenticeship system, a good trade union, effective hygiene, good housing, sufficient food, etc., but also on moral seriousness, a sense of duty, and the overcoming of our innate laziness. But if one succeeds once in increasing the value of one hour’s labor by, say, 10%, it can be deduced from this either that the wage must rise by 10%, or also, and this is the point at issue here, that the duration of their labor be reduced by 10%. Whoever delivers 10% more in one hour of time can, as far as the duration is concerned, suffice with 10% less. This is of great importance for the working class. The worker must not be completely absorbed in his craft. He also has to take care of his person, enrich his mind, be mindful of higher interests. He must also be able to live for his wife and children. Also the

§37. Exemption.
Exemption from labor demands the Sunday, demands the other holidays, demands the Saturday afternoon, demands domestic events, and demands a small outing in the summer. We combine this, because it all flows from the fact that the worker is not a machine, but a human being. As a human being, he demands Sunday rest, as a human being he has a need to celebrate the holy days, as a human being he may be in marriage, and birth, marriage or death, which form an exception, may occur in him, and also as a human being the worker has a need to get out of it for a while, however short. The free Saturday afternoon is of later invention and is intended to provide some extra rest, which others shift to Sunday, especially when buying clothes, etc. from shops, in order not to disturb the silence of Sunday. But however viewed, whoever is human is not a machine, does not tick day and night like a clock, but knows exceptions, and these exceptions must also be reckoned with in the worker. This certainly poses more than one objection to a business that does not allow any exception; such as the business of the doctor, the pharmacist, the servants, etc. and it also proved not always possible in some large factories to bring the large machines to a standstill. The Minister of the Word, the organist, the sexton, etc. are also to be mentioned here.

§38. The Government should regulate this.
An agreement regulation must then be made about this, just as this has already been done for the pharmacy in places where there are more. And as for the Sunday regulations and the service of the official officials, and those who serve, the Government is the responsible person here. To open a polling station on a Sunday, as is done in France and Belgium, is an annoyance that we fortunately do not yet know; but for the rest, the service of the fire brigade, the water supply, the police, etc., is entirely under the authority of the Government, and a trade union to oppose the Government if necessary does not apply to those who serve the Government.

§39. Night Work. Nature points to the day for work, the night for rest. Even among the animals this natural rule applies, with only predatory animals and venomous insects as exceptions. Man too must take the lead here. His is the light, and the darkness must give him the restoration of strength in sleep. Exceptions naturally exist here as well. With a seriously ill person, one must keep watch. Watching is just as necessary as the fire brigade. And the police have to see to it that thieves and robbers do not abuse the night. Now there are also businesses where it has not yet succeeded in ensuring the night’s rest. Especially the Journalism and the Bakeries still suffer under the misery of it. Telegraph and Telephone could be left to stand still in an emergency, for these means of communication did not exist before, and life went on quietly. We have already spoken of other government services. The Bakery too will sooner or later come to its night’s rest. Here, after all, the question of luxury often applies. The ordinary worker is very satisfied with the bread that was baked the previous evening. Only, in the cities, luxury demands the freshly baked roll. Now this too could have been overcome, if one had respected the freedom of the business. Then it would have won a better custom of its own accord. But unfortunately, one did not confine oneself to this. One wanted to force the matter, and then one lost the game. This was foretold, and it came true. It could not be otherwise. Better planned, the attempt will, however, succeed sooner or later. Not at once, and with one stroke, but gradually. With Journalism, on the other hand, it is different. §40. Anarchy. A deliberate discussion of the Anarchist tendencies in the field of labor is less necessary for our country. We have already pointed out how Nihilism, Syndicalism and so much more found entry in Russia, France and Italy, but could never develop into a power of significance among us. In 1903 this striving still gave a sign of life during the Railway strike, but has then also been suppressed forever, and, as one may hope, above all forever. ANARCHY - CONCLUSION. The anarchist movement has quickly declined. One heard nothing further about it, and even when in 1913 the Socialists were momentarily sixteen members strong in the Second Chamber, not even a rumor went out of an attempt to now finally repeal that fatal Law. This is because it has been recognized here that the transient nature of Anarchy on this point, and now systematically aims at transforming the situation. The Anarchist striving, whether it took the form of Nihilism or Syndicalism, had no patience, took no time for it, to transform the situation, and was always intent on suddenly, all at once, turning the existing order of things upside down. One did not need to wait one’s time, nor even systematically prepare anything. One simply waited for a favorable moment, and then would unexpectedly and all at once unleash the great struggle; a struggle in which victory would of itself accrue to the Workers. The distinction between the Anarchist currents and the more moderate Social Democracy of the Revisionists was not that only the former were revolutionary. Both emanated from the glorification of the revolution idea. But herein they differed, that the entire Anarchist current, and so also the Syndicalists in France, did not take the time and the patience for arming themselves for the struggle, but simply wanted to strike without further deliberation. Hence also, that all such attempts could now and then lead to an uprising, but could not change the situation in any way. The much larger military force at the disposal of the Authorities, cut off every chance of success of the Anarchists once and for all.

§41. Conclusion. Summarizing what has thus been argued, it remains our conviction that as much as possible should be directed towards a government involvement with labor, which ultimately derives the greatest percentage of its energy not from the Government, but from the workers themselves. So in the Insurance system. However, a return to earlier conditions, when labor almost entirely organized itself domestically and regulated, as a result of the expansion of industry and technical gains, is cut off. The Government must then lead to this, by obtaining through a proper educational system with annotation, possession of a complete list of employers and employees, in order to unite these in vocational organization, each independently, but yet in contact with each other, so that all workers are included in it, as well as all employers, and that both may come to that connected organization which should serve the Government for advice, exert influence on legislation, and can lead to the removal of disputes. Entirely in the manner of Martin St. Léon. This complete organization will in the meantime be so arranged that any fundamental difference of conviction can lead to a separate grouping within the one great organization. The workers of Christian conviction will be able to act here entirely freely. As long as this ideal has not yet been achieved, in case of a serious dispute the Court of Arbitration will have to serve, and one will have to learn the art on both sides to fully submit to the pronouncement of such a court of arbitration. Especially we Calvinists should not let ourselves be led astray here. Although Professor Dr. Werner Sombart, in his masterwork “Der Bourgeois,” Munich and Leipzig, 1913, p. 323, has angrily railed against Calvinism, and not without some ground, but as appears from the history of Calvinism, the warmest zeal for the worker has always come forth from our circles. Herein Sombart was right, that Calvinism, as soon as it loosens its religious ideal, tends to turn over into capitalism. One saw it in our own history, how after the brilliant period of our faith-struggle, a grasping for gold and money followed, which ultimately caused all higher ideals to perish. Precisely for this reason we pressed so restlessly for an unchangeable maintenance of our spiritual-religious foundation. Neither the organization, nor the rise in wages; nor the insurance may become the ground of our hope for the future. That ground must for every child of God always and solely lie in his trust in God. Always according to Jesus’ definite statement: “All these things do the Gentiles seek after; but ye rest your soul’s needs solely on what your Father in heaven possesses.”
