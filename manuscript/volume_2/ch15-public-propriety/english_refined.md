Chapter XV THE PUBLIC PROPRIETY.
§1. Shame and propriety.
At the beginning of the first and second period of man’s life on this earth, shame comes to the fore. First with Adam and Eve, the second time with Noah. Of course, both Adam and Eve were created naked, and lived without clothing in Paradise. As long as the sense of absolute innocence suppressed the sinful in them, no feeling of shame could arise in them. But as soon as the loosening from God did not become dominant in their soul life, the feeling of shame arose in them. In Genesis III:6 we are told that they ate of the forbidden tree, and immediately thereafter in vs. 7 we read that they made themselves aprons of fig leaves to cover their nakedness. And that this outburst of shame was pleasing to the Lord is evident from Genesis III:21, where we are told that God made garments of animal skins for Adam and his wife and clothed them. Here, then, there is a twofold thing: on the one hand, the arising of the feeling of shame, and on the other hand, the ordinance of God to cover the shameful parts of the body. In connection with this, it is most remarkable that the second period of human life, which dates from Noah after the Flood, also immediately draws attention to something that had to do with shame. For we read in Genesis IX:20: “Noah began to be a man of the soil, and he planted a vineyard; he drank of the wine and became drunk, and he uncovered himself within his tent.” Not, as is often depicted, in the open field, but in his tent. Ham first pointed this out to his brothers. And then Shem and Japheth “covered the nakedness of their father,” and turned their faces backward so that they did not see their father’s nakedness. Awakened, Noah noticed this, and now the period of Common Grace begins.

The two mighty sinful factors, which come from the lust of intoxication, are thus introduced as all-controlling in significance—the first at the Fall in Paradise, and the second after the Flood. Whether wine or strong drink was drunk even before the Flood, we are now left to conjecture. The fact is that in the period before the Flood, with all the horrors that arose, no word was mentioned of anything that could intoxicate. This therefore presupposes the possibility that they may have drunk grape juice, but that the preparation of wine only came later. But however this may be, it is clear from the observation that at both the beginnings of human life, both in Paradise and after the Flood, shame gives the main signal and shamelessness provokes the wrath of God. Now if the Government is the handmaid of God, it follows that it is called to curb shamelessness in public life, and this now sets the duty upon the Government to watch over propriety in public life.

§2. Private and Public. It is noteworthy here that in Paradise the clothing of Adam and Eve with animal skins already took place when they were still alone. Shame thus extended to the fellowship of man and woman. Even here the animal must be avoided. Now if shame goes so far that it sets a feeling of shame even between man and woman, and thus in the whole family, and even in the most intimate intercourse, it is self-evident that especially in public life, whatever approaches nakedness provokes the sense of shame and calls for covering, and since in the life on the way, on the street, at windows and open doors, this awareness of shame has an absolute dominion, it lies in the nature of the relationships that the Government, as the guardian of public life, must prohibit, punish and suppress all that is shameless. With the Government this is called: watching “over public decency.” Immediately here the profound difference between two spheres of life comes to the fore. There is a private and there is a public human life. The first is lived within chambers and in a closed house, the other on the public domain, which is open to everyone. In connection with this distinction, it must always be kept in mind that the Government has to abstain from the first, but on the other hand has to subject everything to its will on the second. The English expression: “my home is my castle,” speaks sharply, but not at all exaggeratedly, even though there is often talk of shame and propriety. Within the home, the head of the household is responsible, outside the home the Government. Something to which it must also be noted that everything that gives an insight into domestic life from the outside, and thus also every room or house to which everyone who wishes has free access, falls under the public domain.

§3. Immorality. We speak intentionally of Immorality, not only of prostitution, and still less of the shameful nature of the sin to conceal it, as is often done in the French word. What the Government has to prevent from the public domain is any immoral display or action. As for the display, this includes, first of all, the improper clothing, which was previously self-evident among us, but in recent times has taken an increasingly shameless form in our cities, both through the partial exposure of women’s breasts on the street, and through the shortening and splitting of skirts, and to some extent also through the tight tension of women’s attire around the body, with no other purpose than to display the luxuriant forms of the female body as strongly as possible to the eye. It is especially the power of fashion that causes this evil to proliferate so rapidly, and further the dominance of Parisian fashion, which, if it does not actually create the evil, at least promotes it in a very detrimental way. The sense of shame diminishes as a result. The damage done by displays in the field of art will be discussed later.

The Police. - The high character of marriage. The impure understanding between the police and prostitution remains an evil that must be guarded against here above all. The brothel keepers and even the private prostitutes once had an interest in remaining on familiar terms with the police. The police officer would then pretend not to see what was going on, and the brothel keeper did not tighten the purse strings and granted the lustful agent free access to his subjects. As long as the visitation continued and brought in evil money, this wicked practice could not be curbed. Now, on the other hand, this evil must be rooted out with all one’s might. Every commissioner and police agent must know that the slightest improper understanding with this will be punished with loss of office. The personal and strictly domestic in immorality does not fall under the jurisdiction of the government, but insofar as it acts against immorality, its conduct must always be marked by a holy, honorable bearing. An approach that not only leads to not tolerating the evil, but even more to combating it, and that also turns against the immorality itself through discipline. Sinful acts must not only be opposed, but also punished, punished above all by restriction or deprivation of civil rights; a means of chastisement that has been applied all too little among us. The high character of marriage. But apart from this, an equally serious vocation comes to the government in its legal regulation of marriage. This has now been done by the manner in which every married person had the opportunity to compel the judge to dissolve his marriage by means of completely fabricated data. It would be highly desirable if the Law could declare every divorce, whether from table and bed or in the fullest sense, inadmissible. The arrangement borrowed from the Roman Catholic Church from the supposed sacrament of marriage must, as far as the purpose is concerned, be given all honor. §6. Birth Prevention. Among the issues that touch on decency, and over which the government ought to exercise oversight, the Neo-Malthusianism and abortion must also be counted without a doubt. The government cannot simply push these two problems aside, because the population question is of great importance for the country and the nation, now and with a view to the future. There was less attention to this as long as the population growth in our country continued from year to year; but now that a reversal threatens here, more attention is also being paid to the state of the population. Especially the example of France in relation to Germany showed how the entire future of a country can be dominated by the decline of the population. Yet it was especially since 1798 that this question also came more to the fore in the scientific field. For then Robert Malthus published his famous Essay on the Principles of Population, in which he argued that a population, provided no checks, as he called them, stood in its way, has the tendency to expand almost endlessly. The population, he thought he could demonstrate, climbs steadily towards the geometric progression of 1, 2, 4, 8, etc., while the progression in the means of subsistence is only arithmetic and does not rise any further than according to the rule of 2, 3, 4. The consequence of this had to be, therefore, that there is always a lowest class of the population that can no longer find proper nourishment, and consequently perishes in misery, in distress and in hunger. Here too, Darwin’s later struggle for existence would apply, and help had to be sought against this.

To conclude from this that one must therefore resort to the suppression of births did not, of course, hold up. As long as, especially in Russia, in South America, Australia and wherever else there was still vast, fertile but uninhabited land available, there could be no talk of the need to curb population growth. This, however, did not prevent the so-called Neo-Malthusianism from emerging in England in 1877, through the founding of the so-called Malthusian League and the publication of the monthly magazine The Malthusian. This league, you see, sacrificed the principle of morality to the so-called Populationists, by suggesting all kinds of means that could be used effectively to prevent pregnancy, without the slightest restriction in the exercise of sexual intercourse.

The reduction in the number of workers would elicit higher wages. And so, from two sides at once, the social evil of having too many children would be remedied. That such a practice now thrives in private life, and thus in secret, is a matter of great concern.

§8. Abortion. The theory of Abortion stands no better, which consists in this, that one allows the fertilization to proceed unhindered, but then works to expel the approaching fruit, either by means of administered substances, or by perforating the already accessible fetus. In this second system, the assistance of dishonorable midwives and physicians was then invoked, who, mostly for large sums of money, lent themselves to the application of the means of abortion. The temptation at play here consisted in the fact that the physician could once prevent a pregnancy that could become dangerous to the life of the mother, without being able to promise a normal birth of the fetus. In such a case, the system of Abortion had already been applied from ancient times; and this not only in the decay of morals in Greece and Rome, but also out of purely medical consideration. This very fact now seemed to many a physician to be able to provide all kinds of occasion to extend the system of Abortion ever further. Especially in New York, one already counts on the expansion of this system to a tenth of pregnancies. Many, in fact, give preference to Abortion over Neo-Malthusianism, because the first system rarely fails, and the second, if applied early and immediately, only gives an unpleasant sensation for a short time. Whoever confesses the origin of all human life to be in God, of course condemns both of these evil practices, both that of Neo-Malthusianism and that of Abortion, as absolutely criminal and cursed by God. One may therefore trust that the application of neither the first nor the second artificial means will occur in our circles.

§11. Art. The most difficult problem for the government’s care lies ever more in the ever-increasing claim that Art makes upon public life. Art cannot be of a domestic nature. All Art wishes to be seen and heard, and therefore naturally presses towards publicity. This gives rise to difficulties in three different areas in particular. First, in the Theater. The Theater too is a part of public life. Now and then one gives a little domestic piece at a wedding or anniversary celebration, but here the evil does not threaten, for then it is outside its proper sphere. The Theater, on the other hand, stands open to all who wish to pay their ticket. In the Theater as well, then, the government must watch that the play remains elevated and does not degenerate into shamelessness. The sad fact, however, that the more common the piece, the more it attracts, and that high-class performances are scarcely attended, creates here the danger of a very serious violation of morality, and against this the government must be on guard. That it actually does not do this, redounds to its discredit and is an evil that calls for improvement. In the second place, the art museums come into consideration here. Increasingly, one turns, in order not only to display the nude beauty of woman out of a high artistic sense, but also verily to stimulate sensual lust through the representation of immoral scenes and positions. Already now this sometimes goes so far that everyone who thinks feels ashamed. In Naples they have at least gathered the shameful scenes of Pompeii in a separate room, to which access is only granted by special permission. Here too the government must proceed with caution. All that does not legitimize itself as true high art, and merely stimulates sensuality, must remain prohibited. The same applies in part also to literature. There is a French literature of almost bestial nature, which is also devoured by many among us, so that it assaults public decency. Hence, in the third place, the government must also pay attention to the display in our bookstores, to advertisements and posters. What is displayed in the store is of public nature, and all representations of shameful nudities and of immoral nature must be prohibited there. Yet in this area one now thinks that the shameful should not only be permitted, but even recommended. This is connected with the prevailing intellectual direction, which is ever more bent on pushing back religion, if not actually displacing it, and allowing art to take the place of honor that has belonged to religion from of old. The mentality of many is now of such a nature that they feel an inspiration of fanaticism arise in them when art comes into play, while they feel unfree when religion is spoken of. And in this evil the often leading public opinion, alas, also sways the government.

§12. Intoxication. The intoxication resulting from the abuse of wine, strong drink or opium, touches only in part the honor of public life, insofar as it can namely become the cause of scandal on the public way and in public gathering halls or through public sale comes directly into contact with public life. On the other hand, what occurs within the home must not be reckoned to this; insofar, at least, as the protection of the police against drunken violence is not invoked by the household members. Within doors the government must leave the combating of the craving for intoxication to the Church, to the family, to literature or to private associations. This is connected with the fact that the system of total abstainers, insofar as they aim at a legal prohibition of the sale or use of whatever can intoxicate, is not acceptable. That private associations rightly aim at the complete abstinence from whatever can intoxicate, with a view to the harmful abuse, is justifiable and even praiseworthy; in itself, however, it is incorrect that out of conscience all use of wine or alcoholic beverages should be omitted. The wine too comes from God’s creation, and aims, when taken in moderation due to weakness or overstrain, at strengthening and elevating the vital spirits. The question stands quite differently, however, whether the government may not also intervene in private life to prevent abuse, by legally regulating the places of sale and consumption.

§13. All excess must be punished. Yet the government must not stop here. It must not only temper the police excesses of those who intoxicated themselves, but also punish them severely to prevent repetition, and the deprivation of certain civil rights could also be of good service here, as for example in guardianship and otherwise. Also where the government acts as an employer and hires personnel for wages, all use of alcoholic beverages must be excluded from that service. Whether the financial restraint of the evil is imposed on the manufacturer, the seller, or the buyer, is as a rule indifferent. Whichever choice is made, the effect remains the same, for both the manufacturer and the seller try to pass on the burdens imposed on them to the drinker. To such an intervention against the abuse of intoxication, the government is not only entitled by virtue of its vocation to watch over good order and public decency, it is also itself obliged, partly in order to reduce criminality, since statistics repeatedly show what a large percentage of legal violations and crimes flow from the abuse of intoxication, but also partly out of concern for public health, for hardly anything affects the state of health as destructively as this abuse. Even the opening of institutions to wean the drunkards from their sinful habits may lie on the path of the government, even if only as part of its care for the insane. Yet the government cannot succeed in combating intoxication if private initiative does not support it. There is therefore every reason to be thankful that this initiative is constantly growing stronger, as a fruit of which the percentage of alcohol consumption per inhabitant has also visibly diminished among us.
