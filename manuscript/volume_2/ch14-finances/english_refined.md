§1. State Organization and State Enterprise. Regarding the Finances, we can therefore suffice with a shorter discussion in this chapter, because we still defend as correct the main lines regarding this so important question, which were drawn in “Our Program.” Brevity is also possible because the financial question, as far as these public bodies are concerned, has already been discussed more extensively in Chapters VIII and IX. It may even be gratefully acknowledged that since 1878 something has also been recognized in our official State life, which at the time could only be brought forward as a protest. We are here particularly referring to the distinction between the financial management that flows from the organization of the State, and that entirely different management that has no other character than that of a State enterprise. These two categories of management differ not only in form, but in principle, and in such a way that the second category, that of the Enterprise, could if necessary be entirely eliminated, and either be left to the Municipalities or to private individuals; while conversely the entire other financial management, which is required by the character of the State as an organism, can only be entrusted to the rulers of the Realm. This has now been officially recognized, at least in the case of the Postal, Telegraph and Telephone services, the State Printing Office, etc. In our State budget, one has now gone so far that what is purely an enterprise is kept separate and accounted for separately. Yet we cannot be satisfied even with the external acknowledgment that this is about enterprises here. Our protest against the direction that refuses to recognize this as the outflow of the organic character of our State system must also be maintained in the recognition of what is not an enterprise.

It cannot be denied, however, that the view that everything done on the financial terrain by the Government is to be regarded as a transaction between the Authorities and the individual citizens, or independent persons, found defense not only in an earlier period and well into the 19th century, but is even now still advocated. §2. The theory of Montesquieu. The fundamental error in this view was that the Government was regarded as a power that guaranteed the possession and enjoyment of his own property and income to the individual citizen, and therefore demanded rightful payment for this service. Especially Montesquieu pleaded extensively for this system, which was then dubbed the “assurance theory.” The citizens were then in the position of persons who did not know how they would obtain the same protection for their possessions or income. Both were constantly in danger. And now the Government offered itself, or rather, now that many citizens together had established a Government, which could provide the otherwise completely lacking security. Now if the Government proved this important service to everyone who had to secure certain property or certain income, it followed naturally that one had to compensate the Government for the expenses it had incurred for this, and moreover to ensure the Government itself such a salary that its existence would not be endangered by lack of means. The whole representation, however, rests on the false assumption of the Contrat Social, and may therefore be regarded as no longer finding defenders who can claim a hearing.

THE THEORY OF MONTESQUIEU - TWO KINDS OF GOVERNMENTAL FUNCTIONS. This theory, and with it the fundamental idea of the insurance system, though in a modified form, was revived. The difference consisted merely in this, that in this second system the citizens were taken as pure individuals in an even more stark manner. The one has more, the other less profit from the care of the Government. To one who possesses no capital, the Government need not by itself guarantee the safe and unimpaired possession. Imagine two citizens side by side, the one who has nothing but a weekly wage of say 10 guilders, and the other who annually receives the interest on a ton of gold—the State cannot cover that wage laborer, because there is almost nothing to cover with him. The possessor of the ton of gold, on the other hand, needs all his ample coverage, in order not to be disturbed in his quiet possession. From this, they then tried to deduce that therefore nothing was to be claimed from the wage laborer, while on the contrary the man of the ton was under obligation to the State or the Government to reimburse the services rendered to him and the costs, and to offer an honorary reward. This theory of compensation, however, could not maintain the favor it had enjoyed for a short time in public opinion, because it actually rested on the false idea that the State was merely a casual agglomeration of independent persons, and thus completely misunderstood the organic character of the life of the people. Also practically, this theory proved to offer a wrong starting point. It led to the result of making only a certain part of the population liable to pay. And not only this, but it soon became more and more impossible to calculate according to a fixed standard what each individual citizen had actually received from the State, and thus to determine the rightful share of each citizen in the common burdens.

§3. Two Kinds of Governmental Functions. Only an excessively penetrating confusion of concepts kept this theory of compensation in force for a fairly long period, and it is this that should be pointed out here. The Government performs, for the benefit of the subjects, two kinds of activities, and from these two kinds the first flows quite certainly and of itself, but the other only at will and as an incidental result of the task of the Government, in connection with the character of the State. Regulating the import at the borders is something that cannot be left to each border village; this import, and likewise the export, touches the relationship in which our State finds itself towards the neighboring State that lies on our borders. There is therefore no question of the citizens being able to regulate this interest for themselves. Here the one central government authority must act as the power over the whole people. On the one hand, therefore, a series of obligations rests on the Government, which it must fulfill for the benefit and on behalf of the entire population, and which cannot be reduced to the task of the individual citizens. As has already been remarked, this also applies to what concerns the Army and the Navy, to what concerns the care for the payment of interest and redemption of the national debt, to what concerns the colonial possessions in other climes, to what concerns the Department of Foreign Affairs, and so much more. §4. §5. Against fixed payment. Yet all these state services that have the character of a business have this in common, that whoever makes use of them must pay a fixed price each and every time. We pay separately for each piece that goes by post, in the form of a postage stamp. Likewise, we pay directly for each telegram. Also where the railway system has passed into the hands of the government, each traveler pays for his ticket each time, or does so by subscription. The peculiar character of all these business services is therefore that whoever wants to make use of them may do so, but whoever does make use of them must pay the due amount as often as they do so. It is indeed the individual citizen for whom the government opens the opportunity to make use of its good services, and whoever makes use of them pays what it costs the government, and this even to the full for their share, and often even a little more, so that such a business commonly also yields an extra income for the government. This is so with the post, with the telegraph, with the telephone; it is the same in Belgium and in Prussia even with the railway system. The same applies to gas plants, water supply, and so much more, although here it is usually only the municipal authorities that concern themselves with it, and not the state. There is a distinction here, but the character of the business remains. But even if one sets aside everything that has the character of a business, there still remains for the government an ever-expanding task that comes to it from the essence of the state, and it is in this fundamental task that the financial involvement of the state, apart from any business idea, presents itself as a matter of course. Naturally, with the business activities of the government, all kinds of arbitrariness have a place. As is known, there are countries where the government itself has made a monopoly of more than one business, as for example of the sale of tobacco and matches, and this makes a distinction in so far that the government in the monopoly does not take on the business activity in order to accommodate the citizenry, but solely with a view to its own gain. This certainly cannot be recommended, as it hinders the free life activity of the subject; but even so, such a monopoly remains a supporting business, and can never be derived from the essence of the state or from the national existence of the people.

In part, yes, as is the case in particular with the public school, which has also been introduced in part as a business. §7. Concentration on the Realm.
The principle of the French Revolution also led financially to deprive the constituent parts of the State of their relative independence, until finally nothing remained but the all-providing State. So it also went with us under the influence of this revolutionary principle. The finances of the Municipalities and the Provinces thereby came to stand in an increasingly unfavorable relationship to the finances of the Realm. For all the Provinces together, the total of all revenues in 1911 was no more than 11¼ million, while the expenditures amounted to only 10¾ million. With loans excluded, the budget of all Municipalities together in that same year was 161¼ million, and against these two amounts of 173 million stood the Realm’s budget in 1914 with a sum of 208½ million, already rising in 1913, i.e. two years later, to 239½. Set against this now Switzerland, where the Cantons retained such a degree of independence, and one finds that the Realm’s revenues in 1911 amounted to no more than 98 million francs, i.e. about 47½ million guilders, while the population stood at 3¾ million. In the rest, the Cantons provided. §8. Direct and Indirect Taxation.
As for the contrast between direct and indirect taxation, the leading position of the French Revolution has here lost its play. The absolute demand of the newly arisen system actually came down to this, that only one single tax would be levied, and that in this tax all citizens would contribute in proportion to their annual income. It seemed so natural. The community certainly needed a certain amount for state administration and state defense. In the community one found the citizens as members. Whoever had any income at all per year, therefore had to contribute in proportion. Preferably, everyone should pay this contribution voluntarily for their share. And so all the financial needs of the State would be provided for of their own accord. But soon it became apparent that this was a utopia, not at all capable of being realized, and so the indirect levies soon went through again as of old. With the exception of the German Realm’s finances, it can then also be said that now still in only a few German States, in Austria, in England, in France, in Russia and in Italy, not to speak of the smaller States, the indirect taxes deliver the lion’s share in the finances of these States. The indirect taxes have their pros and cons. In their favor speaks that the discharge of them goes as it were of itself and unnoticed, that one has it not largely in one’s power whether one will pay in it or not. This is especially evident with the consumption taxes. What was imported from abroad, fell under it as a matter of course. This was the case with wine, all kinds of distilled liquors, beer, tobacco, coffee, tea and much more. With us one left tobacco largely free, but as a rule it can be said that all such luxury articles, whether imported from abroad or even manufactured in the country itself, were affected either by the Tariff or by the excises.

§12. The World Empire. It would certainly be conceivable in itself that all tariffs were abolished and free trade for all goods was allowed throughout the world, in every land, by the entire human race. Then it would certainly be equal for all lands, and the consequences of it would be felt everywhere. Countries rich in mining, agriculture, and manufacturing would then overflow with trade and become ever richer, while conversely countries with little underneath and little above the ground would also lose more and more of their manufacturing and impoverish. The idea of the world empire would then also be carried out to the utmost in the financial sphere, and the materially favorable or unfavorable conditions would determine the fate of the various peoples and nations. Against this material starting point, the nations have constantly risen up out of a desire for self-preservation; partly to increase their revenues, partly to better secure their own production and manufacturing, they have placed tariffs on the borders and levied import duties. England also did this in the early period of the 19th century, and only when it had gradually, thanks to its overseas possessions and its mighty fleet, secured for itself a dominant world position, did it grant free entry for what it itself produced, at least in its tariff, if not in its excise duties. It owed a temporarily unparalleled success to this. Yet here too a turning point came. The Conservatives turned around and aimed at restoring the earlier tariff, while even the Liberals went along with this during the war years. When England turned around for the first time, the economists here too have also changed their opinion, and have also managed to push through the reduction of the Tariff Act. This has promoted the emigration of the factory population to Germany in a very striking way. Whereas the number of Dutchmen in Germany did not exceed 30,000 before, their figure stood at 144,175 persons in 1910. This placed the Netherlands second on the list for immigration. Austria had accommodated 6 1/4 million of its nationals in Germany, and we immediately followed with almost 145,000. In proportion to the population, the Netherlands therefore had the most of its sons go out to Germany to find work. It thus became the opposite of what it had been before. In the days of the hand-loom weavers, it was the Germans who came here to seek work, and now, on the contrary, it was the embarrassed Dutchmen who went out in search of wage-earning labor to the economically much better-off Germany. Something that deserves all the more emphasis, because it is exactly the same with Belgium. According to the figures for 1910, over 70,000 Dutchmen went from the Netherlands to Belgium, while the number of Belgians coming here was no more than 18,338. Belgium had about 7 1/2 million inhabitants, and yet more than three times as many Dutchmen went from here to Belgium as Belgians came to us.

§13. Any absolute system unthinkable for the time being. It may therefore be stated without reservation that, under the given circumstances, the desire on our part to enforce the free trade system cannot be justified in any way. Even if one admits, as we have always done, that such a system, if universally accepted, can be conceived and accepted—as it now stands, the wish of anyone who aims at it cannot in any way be granted. The state revenues do not flow so abundantly that we can miss the tens and more millions that we sacrifice through our current system. Especially when it was a matter of the Invalids Insurance and Free Trade, it was self-evident that we could not give up such a concrete and strongly speaking interest for the sake of an exception in this. But apart from this, the too free import is the cause—that a very large quantity of what we need in all kinds of ways, and which could very well be supplied here, now comes to us from abroad; the consequence of which is that the foreigner attracts the financial profit, gets various kinds of manufacturing here, and that many workers who would otherwise find plenty of work must now cross the border to manufacture for German manufacturers in Germany what they could normally have prepared in their own country for our own manufacturers.

432 PUBLIC FINANCES. The foreign competition here did not cut prices in a way that excluded all competition. Now it is true that industry here has adapted to the financial-economic regulation of Liberalism, and consequently, especially in Overijssel, a native industry has flourished, which does excellent business, especially in the East. However, this is nothing strange. This always happens. England showed in its tariff reversal how Ireland became the dupe of that reversal, and how not only British India, but also Ireland literally impoverished under England’s economic prosperity. Although our industry has adapted with rare energy to the newly entered situation, yet this was and remained nothing more than a bandage on the bleeding, as they say. It was, and still is, an impossible situation that we are clothed from Germany and provided for in all kinds of necessities of life, and that our men take care of China and Japan. Charity well commanded begins with oneself, and so it is also with industry and trade. The Netherlands should not let other countries dictate the law here, but first of all know how to provide for its own vital needs. As it now stands, almost everything in this area has been neglected with us. Hardly anything was done for the industrial education of our workers. The trade schools themselves could not yield sufficient profit, since the teaching staff was too low. And when at least in 1904 an attempt was made to establish in Haarlem what Germany had had for a long time in many cities, this was even opposed by the friends of Free Trade. Now in Rotterdam at least a first step has been taken with the Commercial School. But for the rest, it is still the pseudo-love for Free Trade that has kept us backward in every field.
