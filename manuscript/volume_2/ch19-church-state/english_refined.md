In the First Part, the discussion of the Principles has already touched on this subject in its historical relations. There is therefore no need to fall into pure repetition. Hence, we will simply refer to the First Part. Chapter XX. NATIONAL DEFENSE
§1. Defense and Offense. The fundamental principle here is that in the Netherlands we have never thought of our Navy or Army in any other way than as instruments for a defensive war. Yet we do not push this so far as if our Sovereign could never draw the sword except to repel an enemy who attacked us with armed force. Even with the most peace-loving disposition, there are very well more than one case imaginable in which we would have to take the offensive, without yet having been attacked. We have the duty to stand up not only for the rights and the people of our land, but very decidedly also for the rights and the honor of our national existence. Without an attack coming upon us from across the borders, a threat may come to our Sovereign, a violation of our Sovereignty, an injury to our honor, and an insult to our Colonies, so grave that we could not refrain from a protest, and if necessary, have to act against the offender with armed force. One knows how there have been moments in the present war where our position already became precarious. But although a state of war on our borders may sometimes force us to endure much that would not be tolerable in normal times, yet it can in no case be asserted that it could ever become our duty to draw the sword ourselves, even if the sword were not yet drawn against us. This does not prevent us, however, from speaking exclusively of National Defense, provided that one never loses sight of the fact that a defense will commonly bear a defensive character, but can equally well, where it concerns the defense of our rights or our honor, assume an offensive character. What is excluded and remains excluded is solely that we may wage war or even threaten war, if the only motive for this were gain in territory or increase of our influence. If we were to take that path of war, our action would acquire an aggressive tendency, and anything that even remotely smacks of this is, in the most absolute sense, excluded for our country. §2. All Attempts at Conciliation Come First. We must even go a step further than this. Not only does everything that even remotely suggests an attack out of a desire for gain lie under a strict prohibition, but also where there is a question of violation of rights, prejudice to interests, or injury to our honor, we may never resort to attack unless all other means to resolve the dispute have first been tried in vain. This applies to every country, in an even stricter sense to the smaller States, and most particularly to the Netherlands as the country where the Peace Palace arose, the Court of Arbitration is seated, and the Peace Conferences have been held. What offends, or injures, or damages, may befall us without there having been any malicious intent involved. There may have been a diplomatic misunderstanding, there may have been unintentional errors in the community of nations, there may have been a clash of interests that no one foresaw—in all such cases we must first exhaust every peaceful means before we can even think of taking up arms.

And in all such cases, it is obvious that, before resorting to arms, one must first try to reach a compromise through a thoughtful exchange of ideas; that if this fails, one must call in the intervention of others; and finally, if nothing else can be done, hand the dispute over to an arbitral tribunal for decision. The most desirable thing here would be that, at a moment of undisturbed peace, we should come to a friendly understanding to the effect that, whatever differences might later arise, it would always and without exception be submitted to arbitral decision.

§3. The permanence has failed. If such a permanent arbitration could have gained ground, a more pacifist future could have unfolded. This, however, was not to be. The conclusion of such a comprehensive Arbitration Treaty was attempted and succeeded on our part with Denmark in 1904, and the credit for this first and only treaty of this kind goes to my then colleagues Melville van Lynden and Loeff. However, a subsequent attempt to achieve the same result with Sweden and Norway failed. And when later, especially with a view to Belgium, it was hoped that we would be just as successful with our nearest neighbors as in Copenhagen, the attempt to do so led to such complete disappointment that even the continuation of the negotiations was not to be thought of. Since then, it has become increasingly clear that the other Empires could never be moved to anything more than a limited Arbitration Treaty, as indeed more than one of this kind has already been concluded; but the exception that was always maintained there, that questions of national honor and higher vital interests would be excluded, ultimately deprived all such half-measures of any decisive value. It could never be determined in advance what would be understood or included under these exceptions; and if ever, from whatever quarter, the evil disposition arose to set us right by force of arms, it would soon become apparent that our half-measure treaty was no more than waste paper. We have therefore never failed to exclude any offensive thought on our part as well. The shifting of the casus positions that could have forced us to take the offensive have not been eliminated altogether.

§4. The former Militarism. Where we have always pressed for the armament of our country from the Anti-Revolutionary side, this has never implied sympathy for Militarism, and it may even be said without exaggeration that the active action of our party contributed to curbing a pseudo-militarism that had crept in here in the past, from which a kind of soldiery had even been born. The writer of this still remembers from his student days how even in the sixties of the last century half-drunk dragoons or hussars did not hesitate to go out on the full Sunday Breestraat in Leiden with drawn sabers to terrorize the citizenry and throw the whole city into turmoil. Drinking, raving, and cursing were then still part of the true soldiery. This was all the more true in a university town, because the students of that time held precisely the same class pride towards the “rabble,” alias citizens. Naturally, the citizenry of Leiden benefited from both the University and the Garrison, but without torn clothes they got off neither with the Students nor with the Dragoons.

§7. Our Navy. More than once there has been talk of abolishing our separate Department of the Navy. The appointment of Mr. Colijn as Minister of both the Department of War and the Department of the Navy already laid the groundwork for this intention. The fall of the Cabinet in 1913 has for the time being thwarted this plan; but still, one will see, it will ultimately result in the dismantling of the Navy Department. As will be set forth in a following chapter, the fleet in the East Indies Archipelago can no longer remain a part of our Navy in the Netherlands. The fleet that will have to guard our Archipelago will for its maintenance and armament have to move entirely from the Navy to the Colonies; and in the end there will be no other interest left here at home for a Department of the Navy than covering our coasts and keeping a small number of cruisers ready for flag display.

Spain was the first to experience this in the most painful way. When in the harbor of Manila in 1898 an entire Spanish squadron was sunk by a few cruisers of the Union, and shortly afterwards off Cuba the entire fleet of newly built Spanish cruisers had to yield to the American fleet, the fate of the fleet of every small State was decided.

§9. Coastal Defense
Leaving the Colonial demands aside for a moment, no other defense can be conducted on our coast in the Netherlands than partly 1° through the army, supported by batteries and forts; partly 2° by laying mines; and partly 3° by submarines and airships. This combination alone makes that almost the entire defense now already falls under the Department of War, except the mines and the submarines; for the airships do their work just as well on land as on the waters. It has already been pointed out long ago that the coastal defense can only very partially come from the Navy. Although a blockade is still conceivable, in which a not too large cruiser, as mentioned above, could harm the enemy by shooting out, yet this is too accidental in nature for the defense of the coast to be controlled by it.

§10. The Small States in Distress
But although we are now on the better path as regards the improved fort construction and as regards the mine system, no less seriousness will always have to be made with the submarines and with the airships, and this poses an almost insoluble problem especially for small states like the Netherlands. In the first place, it will be completely impossible to acquire both from abroad. If anything was clearly shown in the recent war, it was the inexorable necessity of making the construction of both these instruments of war entirely and fully possible in our own country. All orders for them from abroad must be broken. Just as it proved to be the most effective way, it was precisely the war itself that provided the exact data for the construction of these instruments of war from month to month, and it was not before, but during the war, that the vast majority of these instruments were ready and are still being expanded. We too must therefore be able to manufacture them ourselves, and the narrowness of our country imposes on us the obligation to have the construction take place within our own borders.

§12. The Strength of Our Army. For the army that will be entrusted with our national defense, the immediately pressing question that dominates all considerations is in what proportion the number of this army must stand to the number of the population. An absolute number for the army cannot be specified. When I wrote the explanation of Our Program in 1879, our population still barely numbered four million souls; now in 1914, according to the population registers, it consists of 6,339,705 persons. An increase in the number thus in 37 years of more than one-third. Yet in determining the figures for the army, this gigantic increase was by no means proportionately taken into account. There seemed to be no necessity to let both figures increase equally. Now, however, the war that broke out in 1914 has so inextricably linked the connection between the two figures that for calculating the numerical strength of the army, no other basis can be used than the population figure. Only a small detail needs to be noted in this regard. The improved hygienic conditions have brought about that in the population figure there is now proportionately a greater number than before of persons over 60 years of age, who no longer come into consideration for military service. For this reason, a certain percentage would have to be deducted in a precise calculation. Likewise, a certain percentage would have to be deducted for the greater number of women, above that of men. And even if one wanted to proceed with the utmost precision, a certain number would additionally have to be left out of account of persons who, thanks to better medical care, now remain alive, where they would have died earlier.

§13. One-tenth.

We are in the Netherlands at this moment presumably not far from 6 1/2 million souls. As I already indicated, the precise figure in 1914 was 340,000 above the six million. PERMANENT VOLUNTEER CORPS - TWO POSSIBILITIES
§16. Permanent Volunteer Corps. As early as 1878, Our Program pressed for the urgent need, alongside our military army, for a small corps of mercenary troops that would stand entirely on its own and be composed of 4,000 to 6,000 men, enlisted for a fixed period of four, six, or ten years. The endless tinkering that occurred especially after 1900 with the so-called “permanent contingent” repeatedly confirmed the pressing necessity to have such a self-standing mercenary corps of volunteers. With the later introduced regulation, it was in itself a demand that the newly enlisted men, on an equal footing, complete their training during the most suitable months of the year. But the consequence of this was that there would be three or more months when our barracks would be empty, and only the officers with the cadre, cavalry, artillery, and other groups that had been training longer would remain in service. This, of course, cannot be tolerated. A certain number of infantry must steadily remain under arms, even in the winter months. The choice of this, however, can only be made at will; and so we also got that endless fuss over the permanent contingent, which still continues. If now the training period were reduced from 9 to 6 months, this inconvenience would become even greater. The government can never be entirely without troops. §1. Colonies. The name “Colony” is derived from the Latin word colonus, which in itself means nothing more than farmer. The cultivation of the land is called colere terram, and the man who does this bears the name of colonus. To this was then added the secondary concept that a colonist was someone who left his country and went to another State where uncultivated land lay, and who now wanted to reclaim such a region. However, this was not the work of one man alone, but of a group of people who left their country of birth together to begin a new existence on a small scale in the newly to be reclaimed region. This did not exclude the possibility that others who were not farmers also went out with such a company, and at the same time brought a beginning of culture that took on more of an urban character; but the basic concept was and remained that it was a matter of cultivating a land that had not yet been developed. It is therefore not entirely by chance that the word “culture” and the word “colony” are of the same origin and both derive from the Latin colonus.

The higher culture of Central Asia had penetrated into their home cities, and since they were connected through their shipping with the entire Mediterranean Sea up to the Strait of Gibraltar, they gave the Greeks a push in a higher direction, and enriched their influence themselves by founding some settlements on the north coast of Africa. The Greeks themselves soon took over the colonization idea of the Tyrians. One knows the role they played in Sicily. The Greek colonies soon surpassed the mother cities in importance, and the peculiar thing about this was that the Greeks nevertheless understood how to feel one with their colonies. Altogether, this was then called Greater Greece, and both the unity of language and the loyalty to the ancestral customs, in connection with the universal significance of the oracles of Delphi, etc., as well as the Olympic Games above all, gave historical proof that a people can live together in mutual connection, even if there is no overarching Authority that dominates all its parts. Rome became powerful not through settlements with an independent social life, but through military force. For the right understanding of the idea of colonization, one must therefore always go back to the Phoenicians and the Greeks.

§6. Economic System. Atomistic or Organic? Apart from these views on the task of guardianship, the second question now arises of itself, by what economic system we can best fulfill this guardianship for the benefit of the colonies and our own country. In this respect, the revolution in economic life that arose in the 19th century is connected to the school of Adam Smith. From Smith and Cobden, the new school emerged, which undeniably eliminated the last traces of the mercantile-physiocratic system and gave access to the system of free labor, free trade, and free competition. Adam Smith, above all, drew the desire too much away from the treasures of nature, in order to seek the true value that governs life in labor, and in the personal development of man’s powers. Egoism had to be the starting point. Everyone sought the best for themselves. No place could be made for altruism. It was the desire to benefit oneself that demanded the most absolute free competition. The government, therefore, had to withdraw as much as possible from economic life, in order to leave this peculiar life entirely to its own development. Capital was therefore not to be pushed aside at all. Rather, it was the attractiveness of the power of capital that stimulated both selfishness and competition. Every price of any product or commodity whatsoever, therefore, resolved itself spontaneously into ground rent, wages, and capital interest; and since these three factors could work in their natural equilibrium, Smith believed he could predict the highest prosperity for land and people, especially for the lower classes. A gain, to which was added that from his standpoint the national factor spontaneously thrived in the international sphere, so that the rivalry between peoples and peoples lost its sharpness, and a cosmopolitanism could be expected that would soon restore the conditions of paradise. As could not be otherwise, this international-economic idea has now also been introduced into our new colonial policy, especially from the Liberal side. §8. The Kingdom.
The first article of our Constitution underwent a considerable modification in the revision of 1887, in so far as now also mention was made of “the colonies and possessions in other parts of the world.” Even in 1848 this had not yet occurred. Article one then only contained that “the Kingdom of the Netherlands consists of the present provinces.” It was only added: “in Europe.” This already then left room for the idea that the Kingdom also consisted of territory outside of Europe, only it was not yet expressly stated. This addition came only in 1887, and with this peculiarity, that now in Article 2 it was declared: “The Constitution is binding only for the Kingdom in Europe,” while in section 2 it was determined: “Wherever in the following articles the Kingdom is mentioned, only the Kingdom in Europe is meant.” Against this manner of expression there is no overriding objection, provided that the distinction between the Mother Country and its colonies and possessions is clearly kept in view. Also in personal life one can say that a person does not appear only in his person, but as a person also possesses the garment he wears and the household goods that serve him, and if he has possessions, also of that property or of securities and monies. One sees this with the highest placed. This does not take away, however, that between the person and his possessions or property there always remains a principial distinction. He can lose all his goods, yet he as a person remains, and conversely he can die, yet all his goods remain available. And so it is here as well. When one speaks of the Kingdom of the Netherlands as an international appearance, then naturally everything is included that this Kingdom possesses. In the colonies as well as on the palace in the Residence the Dutch tricolor flag waves, as an indication of the authority. Yet always in such a way, however, that something quite different and distinct is meant, if one has our country and people themselves in view, as the subject that possesses, or the colonies in the Far East that are an object of possession for our Kingdom. Imagine that all colonies were taken from us, yet the Kingdom would still remain; but imagine, on the other hand, that our country in Europe disappeared, then our possession in the Indies would not be “The Kingdom of the Netherlands.” Here in Europe the supreme authority is exercised by the grace of God by our Queen, in the colonies, on the other hand, the authority of the Kingdom of the Netherlands applies, and the authority of the Governor-General counts only as granted by the Government of the country. The country in Europe is and remains the actual Kingdom. This Kingdom has possessions in the colonies and conquered territories. The administration of these possessions is entrusted to the Government of the country, in connection with the States-General, and the authority of our Sovereign counts here only in the sense that she is the God-ordained and constitutional Head of the national government. The defect in our Constitution is therefore not included in Article 2. When one speaks of the Kingdom, one also counts our possessions. But it is a mistake that in Art. 1 mention is made of a Kingdom that includes the possessions. In this lurks a confusion of concepts that is not permissible. Properly speaking, the entire expression of Kingdom should have been avoided. Elsewhere one also speaks of the State, and one should have used this word also in Art. 2, so that all difficulty would have fallen away. No part of the Kingdom can be placed outside the Constitution, and therefore the provision in section 2 of Art. 2 already indicates in itself that the Kingdom as such is only the Kingdom in Europe. A Constitution is no Constitution, or it must go over country and people. To say, as is now done: There is a population of 45 million, the Constitution applies only to 6 1/2 of these 45, but not to the remaining 39 million, is a legislative absurdity. Not the Queen, but the Kingdom has colonies, and what one has, that one is not.

§12. Industry. If there is ever to be any talk of a higher social development in the more populous parts of the Outer Possessions, at least in the larger cities, Java cannot remain at the agricultural level.
