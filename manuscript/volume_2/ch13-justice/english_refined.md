§1. The Domain of Jurisprudence.
In the Introduction to this work, on pp. 1–87, the Law in general has been discussed. Here only that law comes up that governs the sphere of Jurisprudence. Now the Government that acts as a judge is by no means the universal or the only and sufficient upholder of the Law. The Law in a universal sense embraces our entire human life, and even partly the life of angels; and this not only for the life they now and then experience here on earth, but for the expression of the self-conscious creaturely life in all spheres. As often as in the self-conscious creature a thought arises, or even a word or an action goes forth, the question immediately arises whether this thought, this word, this deed does or does not correspond to a rule, ordinance or disposition given for it by the Creator. What is in conformity with the will of the Creator is right; what comes into conflict with that will is wrong. But precisely this universal character of the law is the reason why one errs who says that the Sovereign of any country or the judge appointed in that country has to watch over all Law, or would also be the absolute upholder of the Law. CAPITAL PUNISHMENT. The necessity that the psychic order established by God in the right be maintained as much as possible among all peoples through legislation, adjudication, and punishment. This raises the question of capital punishment, which requires deliberate discussion. In 1878, when Our Program saw the light, the view of capital punishment was still almost universal among all peoples in all lands. However, since the last quarter of the previous century, more and more countries have moved towards its abolition. It had already been abolished in Portugal in 1867, in Michigan in 1847, in Rhode Island in 1852, in Wisconsin in 1853, in Venezuela in 1866, and in Romania in 1864. In Switzerland too, it was removed from the Penal Code in 1874, but was reintroduced in 1879, except for political crimes. In Bremen, Oldenburg, Anhalt, and Saxony, capital punishment had also been abolished before 1870. However, the total population of these ten American and European States and Cities was still far too small for one to speak of a general abolition. Since the seventies of the previous century, the movement aimed at abolition has nevertheless gained the upper hand among the circle of legal scholars, and not only among those who do not reckon with a Supreme Judge as the Disposer of all human life, but to a certain extent even in the circles of believing jurists; and this while, conversely, Lombroso, who could see in the murderous instinct nothing but a hereditary phenomenon, peculiar to certain families, precisely urged that the scaffold be used for the extermination of the most dangerous elements in these families. Even a marriage ban in such families was once urged; something that would of course never achieve its purpose, since a marriage ban does not exclude procreation. Leaving these exceptions aside, it can nevertheless be established that the movement against capital punishment has been gaining ground in the scientific-liberal domain; so much so that capital punishment now finds hardly any defenders in legal circles anymore. It is increasingly felt that it does not belong to anyone, and thus not even to the judge, to deprive someone of life; that the severing of life cuts off moral education and improvement; and that the cruelty of public execution rather promotes coarseness of morals than a higher reverence for human life. Emphasis is then placed above all on the fact that it sometimes turned out afterwards how the executed was completely innocent of what was imputed to him. And even more, how bad upbringing, bad company, immoral reading, or lack of the necessary means of subsistence had not merely tempted the unfortunate to his crime, but almost forced him into it, so that our anger should turn much more against the capitalist society that created such deplorable conditions than against the murderer who had become its victim. On the other hand, it was also pointed out from the Methodist side that capital punishment cuts off the possibility of later conversion in the case of an unconverted person.

§11. Looser from the Law. In not so small a circle, the question has arisen at the end of the previous century whether the criminal law did not deprive the judge of too much of his freedom. The law, as it had been from time immemorial, sought to analyze all crimes in detail and then to establish the necessary punishment for each separate and distinct case. This led to difficulties, all the more so as the legal way of thinking, which is increasingly penetrating especially among the younger jurists, cannot boast of the legal insight that had presided over the drafting and promulgation of the criminal law and other threatening laws. To modify all these laws at once was not possible; but it was then thought that a way could be found to ensure that the freer view would penetrate into what was now being established by the legislator, and henceforth be left more to the judge to fix. Thus, over time, a new theory came to stand opposite the previously prevailing one, and the main thrust of this theory was to make the administration of justice more independent of the provisions of the Law. What had thus far been determined in the legislation down to the details regarding criminal threats, it was thought that the judge should now be left more free to determine.

§12. The Subjectivity of the Judge
Thereby the subjectivity of the judge in the administration of justice gains a not inconsiderable preponderance. Not only in the international juridical world have there been schools in every century, yes, in every half-century, leading to very divergent conclusions, but even in our small country one finds, as a rule, a striking difference of insight regarding jurisprudence, as far as the punishment of evil is concerned. Each faculty then tries to imprint its ideas on the students who follow its lessons. The student who regularly attends these lectures, and moreover enriches his study by the acquisition of the products of other juridical schools, and by acquainting himself with other theories, ultimately chooses, with more or less depth of insight, for this or that theory; and armed with this, he then immediately aspires to a judicial seat. A seat on which he has hardly taken his place or his passions and preferences prick him to now officially apply his subjectively calibrated ideas. Considering now how far these juridical theories, both in general views and in detailed application, diverge, so that not a few ultimately almost completely abandon any idea of a punitive justice, in order to aim solely at the protection of society and the improvement of the offender, one sees at once to what pure arbitrariness it can lead, if one leaves it to the judge to decide what would otherwise have been firmly established by the law. This investigation, under good control, should then take on a serious form. It should not be a bandage over the wound, but a real investigation into the relationships as they are. THE OATH QUESTION - DIFFERENCE IN DEGREE
§17. The Oath Question. Regarding the oath question, the Calvinist recognizes no other jurisdiction than that which is conducted in the presence and before the face of God. In itself, no person, whoever they may be, has the right or authority to take the life of another, to temporarily or permanently deprive them of their freedom, or to deny them a portion of their possessions. §29. The Institution of the Oath-Helpers. But while we must commend those more liberal legal scholars who handled the scales of justice with impartiality, it requires no further argument that their objection did not decide the matter. But what did our proposal essentially come down to? We appealed to the Oath-Helpers, who had come into vogue in the Middle Ages. The peculiarity of this legal institution of the “Oath-Helpers” consisted in this, that it was of Germanic origin, and therefore naturally fell into the background as the Roman law began to dominate our legal institutions more and more. This institution from Germanic law had also been incorporated into Canon Law and had gained sympathy there. Both Dr. Gemeiner and Dr. Hildebrand gave the necessary explanation about this institution from the Roman Catholic side in 1841 and 1849. The work of Dr. Gemeiner, which saw the light of day in 1849, bore the title: “Oath-Help and Oath-Helpers”; that of Dr. Hildebrand, “The Purgatio Canonica et Vulgaris.” Officially, these “Oath-Helpers” bore the name of Conjuratores or also Consacramentales, and their action consisted in this, that both in civil proceedings and in criminal cases, they supported by their own oath-taking him who had to take the oath.

—- Page 422 —-

They then testified under oath that they knew the witness or the party personally and sufficiently well, and that his testimony contained no deception. The consideration that applied here was that the judge did not know the witness or the party well enough to judge whether one could rely on his statement, and that they, the Oath-Helpers, on the other hand, knew him very well, and now stood surety for the honesty of his testimony. They were usually seven in number. Since the 13th century, however, this institution of the Oath-Helpers has fallen into disuse, and the more the Roman law, strengthened by Canon Law, gained dominion, the less the Oath-Helpers appeared. Of their own accord, they did not pronounce on the case itself. Their testimony served only to vouch under oath for the credibility of the man. Especially in the purgatio canonica, they gained decisive importance from the 11th century onward. Sometimes their number here rose to twelve, and even now this institution is still valid for Canon Law in matters of impotence, and is then called the juramentum septimae manus, since then seven blood relatives must appear as Oath-Helpers.

We also do not hesitate for a moment to maintain our proposal in principle, regardless of the form, and we still see those Anti-revolutionary Jurists who, without further argument, went along with the Liberal system, also somewhat facing the proof that either my proposal cannot achieve its purpose, or that it would be impractical. It is precisely with a view to this that we have repeatedly insisted that the Oath question should not be dealt with in passing and by surprise, but that the decision on this institution should be postponed until such time as a definitive draft emerges that aims to raise the debates on this weighty issue to that high level that the sacred character of the matter itself demands. Postponement of consideration and decision is therefore all the more permissible, because the Government side continues to insist that it is alien to any intention to banish the Oath from the courtroom, and he who is convicted on a declaration without Oath, continues to raise the objection that full justice has not been done to him. It can therefore not be emphasized enough that in the Oath question one must once and for all cease to see only the difficulty for the summoned witness, and to understand all the duty to also revive the question of how to watch over the condemnation of an innocent person on false testimony. Briefly, to add this, this issue can be reduced to the proposition that those who do not believe in God cannot take an Oath, but that there must be a right of assessment to judge the reality of the refusal of the Oath on that ground. From which it follows that the right of assessment of the judge cannot apply here, since he does not know the person concerned closely, and for this reason, as an organ for this right of assessment, persons must appear who know him personally closely, and are thus able to pronounce on the truth and sincerity of his assertion. This organ is, in our opinion, to be found in the Oath helpers.*) *) On April 26 and 27, 1916, the Oath issue was decided in the First Chamber by the adoption of the amended draft submitted by Minister Ort. The adoption took place with 33 against 10 votes. The Roman Catholics voted almost all in favor, with the exception only of Messrs. Reekers, Haffmans, Van der Biesem and Michiels van Kessenich. Of the Anti-revolutionaries, only Messrs. Lucassen, ‘t Hooft and Franssen voted against. Dr. Kuyper was in Budapest, and was unaware that the decision was about to be made, the more so as the postal correspondence with the Hungarian capital took almost six days. He too would have voted against, just as all Anti-revolutionary members did in the Second Chamber on February 22. It is true that in the previous draft he had pointed out that the position could present itself in such a way that he too would vote in favor, but only if by doing so a concession could be obtained. This, however, was not at all the case here. What was now under consideration was the most unfavorable draft that could be imagined, and even the Minister’s explanation colored it even more unfavorably. It can therefore only be confessed that the adoption of this draft in the First Chamber was one of our most grievous disappointments that could be imagined.
