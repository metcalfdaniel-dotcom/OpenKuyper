Chapter VI. THE GENERAL ACCOUNTING OFFICE.
§1. The Two Interested Parties. The Accounting Office did not attract much attention in our circles. Only since the appointment to that Chamber of one of our most popular Statesmen was attention also more focused on this College among us. A brief discussion of this governing body may therefore find its place here. In an ever-increasing measure, Finance plays a role of high importance on the government terrain. The state budget keeps climbing in figures. In the Provincial States, ever greater amounts are also entrusted to the authorities. And even the Municipal Councils, at least in the larger cities, have gradually come to dispose of such colossal annual resources that at this lowest level of government too, finance is speaking louder and louder. Here, two kinds of questions naturally come to the fore, one of a political, the other of an administrative nature. From a political point of view, the struggle between the Government and the People arises here, and from an administrative consideration one stands before the question whether the country’s monies are being handled with strict honesty. Yet, however much these two questions may diverge, as far as the accounting is concerned, they have been brought under the protection of one and the same college, and this one college is the Accounting Office. Two interested parties are involved here. The Crown-bearer as Head of the Government must obtain certainty that the organs of his government are conducting the financial management in the most punctual manner, and on the other hand the People must be able to control whether the monies of the People, which have been ceded by the Representatives for the Government, are indeed being spent in conformity with the agreement made. The agreement thus intended between Sovereign and People comes about through the parliamentary approval of the Budget Laws, both as regards receipts and expenditures. Hence, to the Accounting Office is entrusted the task of controlling whether the agreement thus made is actually being observed.

THE TWO INTERESTED PARTIES. 181 The States-General must remain in control, to investigate in detail what has been collected, and for what purposes and in what manner the collected funds have been spent. This control also has an administrative character, but it is primarily of a political nature. Suppose for a moment that the Government had at its disposal, from mines, funds, or any other source whatsoever, sufficient means so that there would be no question of levying taxes, then the People would effectively be at the mercy of the Government’s discretion, and their rights would be seen to dwindle and perish. The rights that the people now possess, and in which they are strongest, they have successively conquered to a richer degree by the fact that the administration was impoverished, had no private means, and was therefore almost entirely dependent on the States-General, who were empowered on behalf of the People to grant or refuse the necessary means to the Government. This very right to grant or refuse the necessary means now naturally demands, as the key to the lock, that the States-General can investigate whether the collection and expenditure of the granted means takes place in accordance with the agreements made. The indispensable control thus has a completely different character than the central control the Crown-bearer needs regarding the conduct of his organs or officials. Nominally it is the King who collects, and the King who spends the money. But the piecemeal involvement is much too extensive for him to be able to administer it himself. He is therefore compelled to investigate and examine whether his administrative organs have acted honestly. From history, one knows, unfortunately only too well, how shamefully this has been defrauded, if not here, then elsewhere. In order to be personally responsible, the Bearer of the Crown is therefore obliged to closely monitor his organs or officials in their housekeeping. He must know that he is not being deceived. In itself, it would therefore have been very conceivable that two control colleges had appeared; one to control the officials on behalf of the King, and the other to exercise control over the administration itself on behalf of the Representatives. The first would then have been a purely administrative control college; while the second control college would have had to provide a guarantee that the administration was carried out in accordance with the agreements made.

§2. The two controls united. Nothing, however, prevents these two, principally distinct controls, from being entrusted to the management of one and the same college, as has indeed happened almost everywhere. The means by which this double and in its nature distinct control can nevertheless be entrusted to one and the same college, has been found among us in this, that the Second Chamber would offer the candidates for membership of the college to the Crown, and that the Crown would make the choice from these candidates presented by the Second Chamber. Hereby two goals were simultaneously achieved. If the Second Chamber presented a list of three candidates, and the King was obliged to take one of these three as the new member, then the Chamber was assured that the chosen one would be a proper controller, also in connection with the rights of Parliament, and on the other hand the honor of the Majesty of the Crown was saved, in that the appointment of the new member did not proceed from the Chamber, but from the Crown. This honoring of the dignity of the Crown was further strengthened by the fact that the King appointed the Chairman entirely freely and that the appointment of the lower assistants also remained with the Crown. The King could, without fear that this arrangement would threaten his dignity, acquiesce in this idea, because the control that he himself exercised over his own officials could never be sharp enough, and because, in connection with this, precisely the appointment of the members from the nomination of the Second Chamber provided him with a guarantee that it would not be a control in name only. It did indeed require some formal effort to incorporate the precise formulation of this double control into the Fundamental Law and the statutes, but in substance there was no dispute between the parties on this point. All parties stood for the same control, and the combination once introduced, to have the Second Chamber propose three candidates and the King choose from such a trio, generally satisfied. Of course, it had to be determined at that time that a member of the Audit Office could not hold any other function that would involve him publicly with the expenditure of state funds. The chosen one had to free himself from such ties that bound him financially, and refrain from acting in various functions, but in return he received a lifetime appointment, and could only be dismissed from his office by a judicial decision, even in case of mental weakness or for whatever reason. §13. The Precarious Nature of Verdicts. Now, all these difficulties could not be simply stamped out from the root, but would directly flow from the contradiction that would remain between the character of such a Court and the manner of its establishment. Jurisdiction in a higher sense is obligatory, both for him who gives it and for him who must submit to it. The scoundrel, the villain, does not bind himself by contract to submit, should he commit a murder, to the Criminal Court. Such a contractual criminal procedure would be simply unthinkable. Even what in earlier centuries presented itself as a dispute, whether one would or would not submit to the judge who presented himself, in no way had its roots in a contractual basis. Now, if it concerns international disputes, over which the Court of Justice would have to pronounce judgment, excluding in themselves insignificant, merely formal questions, one might still assume that submission to the verdict would readily follow. But this is by no means the case. Continually serious disputes arise between State and State, and ultimately such international lawsuits may also arise in which one country accuses the other of committed violence and perpetrated injustice, so that a criminal trial would actually come to the fore. Now, such a lawsuit can find no solution except in Sovereignty. There would thus have to appear a sovereign power standing above the two States involved in the dispute. This high Sovereignty would, like every valid Sovereignty, have to be derived from the Sovereignty of God, and both the criminal jurisdiction and the execution of the criminal sentence would have to be derived from that Sovereignty. Thus, it always comes down to the question whether on God’s part a Supreme Authority has been established on this earth, to which all the States of the world have to submit themselves. In the medieval struggle over the Hierarchy of the Bishop of Rome, the solution to this painful question was often sought, and even where the Pope was set aside, it was still the holy “Roman Emperor” who was considered called to fulfill this high office. But whatever may be said about this, the outcome has sufficiently shown that neither hierarchical nor imperial power was to be found as an authority established over all States.
