§1. Connection with the first part. In the First Part, the principles from which we believe political science should proceed have been extensively explained. Now, in this Second Part, we come to the application of these principles, particularly in our country and in the present time. Part I gave the general; in this Part follows the specification. This is not to be understood as if the exposition of the Principles had been carried out entirely apart from the data that present themselves to us in our time. This would not even have been thinkable in a constitutional exposition. The absolutely abstract theory has never done anything but mislead, and Savigny’s historical school has above all cured us of the theoretical one-sidedness of the natural sciences. But even apart from this, it was not to be thought of, in a practical popular handbook like the one offered here, to exclude all specification from the exposition of the Principles. A public, such as I have in mind as readers, and which consists mainly of non-academic leaders of the people, would not easily be able to follow a purely theoretical exposition. Only by specification could the positions I advocate become clear to such people. As far as, in the pleading of these principles, a reference to the particular and to the practical data had to be made, only auxiliary service was sought, and there could be no question of a special application to the Antirevolutionary Party, as it presents itself for the various political issues in our country and in our time.

§4. THE CALVINISTS HERE IN THIS COUNTRY. As far as our country is concerned, the Calvinists in the 16th century formed little more than one-tenth of the population. This figure, indicated by Professor Fruin, was very sharply taken; but when it comes to very decisive principles of politics, one is always safer by staying below the imposing figure than by aiming above it. Now if we stick to this one-tenth, it may be presumed that we still stand in the same proportion to the entire population. Although it is true that the candidates put forward on our side for the Second Chamber have repeatedly reached a much higher proportion, this does not at all lead to the conclusion of a higher proportion figure. Only after the introduction of proportional representation would it be possible to draw a conclusion on this point. Not now, and the less so because the Anti-Revolutionary Party does not dominate a particular part of the country, but is scattered over all the provinces, with only North Brabant and partly Limburg excepted.

The consequence of this is that almost all our candidates are elected only with the help of other parties, but then also in turn give their votes in other districts to the candidates of our electoral allies. The calculation that the population faction of our party now, as in the 16th century, estimates at one-tenth, is therefore not derived from the results of the ballot box, but is the result of entirely different data that stand at our service. From this last, however, it appears that the Reformed Churches alone already count nearly 500,000 members, so that an estimate that adds up all the denominations of the Calvinists certainly does not lead us to an overestimation of our own strength, but rather points to the stubborn group that clings inexorably to the Calvinistic principles, gathered from all the Churches, as in the days of the Gueux, to one-tenth of the entire population. Two things are now conceivable here. The state of affairs will either present itself in such a way that this one-tenth remains entirely by itself, or there will be an interest at stake for the whole country and the whole people that will of itself lead the guidance of this one-tenth. The latter was the case in the 16th century; the consequence of which was that at that time the Calvinists had the decision in their hands; in the first-mentioned case we are now. In the 16th century, the word went out from our group, in which public opinion expressed itself; now public opinion can turn against us with overwhelming force. If the opponents on our side had also not had the imprudence to bring the School Question to the fore in 1857, our influence on the course of the country’s affairs would probably not have led to the Crown entrusting the composition of a Cabinet to one of our statesmen even three times.

§5. Allies. This state of affairs necessarily leads to the consequence that, while maintaining one’s independence as a political party, one must nevertheless be intent on forming alliances with other parties in order to succeed and achieve a common goal. And here, the uncertainty often arises by which motives the choice of your allies should be determined. For this choice will in France lead our kindred spirits to join the radical elements, and conversely here at home, that we seek support from the Roman Catholic part of the population. But that the latter should be our rule everywhere, does not at all follow from this. Just think of Spain and Portugal, and you will immediately perceive the profound difference. In Spain, the Curia has persistently insisted that the Papal Church alone be allowed to appear freely. The infiltration of Protestantism was not even to be tolerated. If there is now at least a milder view prevailing here, it is not the Curia that has conceded this, but rather a radical current that has pushed this through, against the will and intention of the Curia. As for how it went in France, we know. Pure indifference in name, but in fact enmity against all religion has become the leading idea. The consequence of this was that in France too the Réformés joined the Radicalism. For they knew that, if Rome regained its old, lost position, a revival of the spirit of the St. Bartholomew’s Night was to be feared. It is therefore never in an absolute sense to be determined with which tendencies, with which parties we, the Anti-Revolutionaries, should ally ourselves on the political field. This cannot be established equally for all lands and peoples. The choice is necessarily determined by the attitude of the Church of Rome in such a land. If it can live on a footing of equality with the Church of Rome, then it is obvious that we as Calvinists feel much more akin to Rome than to the parties of the Revolution. If, on the other hand, a Roman-clerical regime, as in Spain, even begrudges us the light in our eyes, then we are compelled to seek support where otherwise the principled opposition would forbid us all cooperation.

§6. Kuyper and Dr. Bronsveld. It is from the failure to properly distinguish these two that Dr. Bronsveld, and always only his kindred spirits, have become the victim. Intercourse and association with the collectors of the Églises réformées in France led this otherwise so noble scholar to want to apply the relationship that was found there in France also to our country. However warmly he had initially welcomed my appearance in the political field, I soon lost his sympathy, and he tried to apply the relationship that had to be enforced in France also here at home. His alignment with the Liberals became increasingly decisive; that is to say, with the Conservative-Liberals; and he withdrew his support from me. In Kuyper especially, Dr. Bronsveld found his opponent on this point. This noble fighter too, just like Dr. Bronsveld, always harbored a certain fear that cooperation with Rome promised us bitter fruits, but far from leaning towards Liberal Conservatism or Conservative Liberalism, Kuyper always came out uncompromisingly for the independence of the Anti-Revolutionary principles, and allied himself more faithfully and decisively than any other party member with the Central Committee. Kuyper showed his superiority of spirit in this. Leaders of the second rank like Dr. Bronsveld allow themselves to be determined in their choice by incidental motives; a man of the first rank like Kuyper does not pluck the flower from the stem, but clings firmly to the root. This never letting go of the root could therefore allow much play in the unfolding of the sympathies in practical action. Kuyper had not closely followed the latest development of the Roman political ideas, as they had come to the fore especially under Leo X. The “rather Turkish than Popish” therefore continued to play through his mind.

§8. The Schism of 1894.
In the secession of those who now call themselves the “Christian-Historical Union,” a phenomenon of a very different nature appears. In the elections of 1913, it came to light how all independence fails in this Union. One must be very careful in judging this Union, lest one pass an unjust verdict. In general, it stands firm that those who gather in this Union are fervent believers in the Gospel, mostly inspired by the Revival and the associated Methodism. As a rule, the School Question arouses their warm interest, and they do take a real interest in the ethical-political issues. Questions such as Sunday observance, prostitution, temperance, hygiene, poor relief, the penitentiary system, and many more, tend to inspire particular interest in the men and women of this Union. Often, they also have a love for Missions. Hence, as long as political action revolves around these issues, the Union is truly a Union, and often exerts an influence that we can also applaud. But—and this is what must be particularly noted—when the proper political constitution of the State comes to the fore, then there is no more motley jumble of political ideas imaginable than what comes to the surface in the solidarity of this Union. This appearance with its own Electoral Program has been repeatedly condemned, especially from the Christian-Historical side. Not from the side of the Roman Catholic State Party. §1. The Question of Sovereignty.
In Article 2 of Our Program, the Antirevolutionary Party confesses twofold. Firstly, that the sovereignty over all peoples and all States rests in God; and secondly, that the highest authority derived from this sovereignty resides in the House of Orange in the Netherlands. The first part has been sufficiently dealt with in Part I; therefore, here only a brief word about the lodging of the derived sovereignty in Orange. Since outside of God Himself absolutely no one can be conceived who could dispose of sovereignty, it could only be granted by the Almighty. This could take place immediately or mediately.

—- Page 74 —-

The sovereignty that Adam possessed over Paradise was conferred upon him directly. Without any intermediary, it was directly declared to Adam: “Fill the earth and subdue it, and have dominion over all that is in it.” To a certain extent, this can be placed on the same level as the calling of Moses at the Burning Bush, and in a certain sense also the calling of David, even though Samuel was the intermediary conveying God’s decree. The mandate of the Crown in the Kingdom of God to the Christ also took place, according to Psalm II:6, directly. In the course of history, on the other hand, and generally speaking, the mandate did not come about except indirectly. Providentially, a person, a house, a college, or even the people themselves, can actually be in possession of the supreme authority, without there having been any direct intervention from God’s side. There may have been a conquest of the supreme authority through some act of violence. The supreme authority may have been inherited or transferred. The question could only arise whether regulation of the revision on a more solid footing would not be required, where it concerned a constitutional issue that touched on the relationship between the various political parties or classes; especially in the matter of the franchise. Often it has been presented as if, with too easy a mode of revision, a danger would threaten from the side of the Left.

THE RELATIONSHIP OF THE PARTIES - GENERAL OR PARTIAL.
A temporary majority could too easily abuse its position to twist the entire constitutional order to suit its own ends. That there would be a tendency towards this is undisputable. But one must not lose sight of the fact that, except in revolutionary times, a somewhat significant revision of our Constitution does not just fall from the sky, but generally comes up for discussion years in advance and under public scrutiny, and is often already prepared by the introduction of revision bills by members of the Chamber. Hence, it is soon noticeable at the ballot box whether revision is on the agenda or not. If it is, it is usually already put forward by the parties at the ballot box, and the vote that the people cast in the general election is often already a declaration of their desire. This generally leads, after the Cabinet has taken office, in which the outcome of the ballot box is reflected, to the appointment of a State Commission; as has repeatedly been the case, even after the revision of 1887. In the reports of these commissions, the representatives of the people then naturally obtain a more definite formulation. And in this way, what was otherwise aimed at by the dissolution of the Chamber, can now of itself fall within the parliamentary fold.

§23. General or Partial. The not unimportant question of whether general or partial constitutional revision is preferable is also connected with this. If the revision proceeds too slowly, and too many extraordinary measures are required, one is reluctant to gird oneself for a constitutional revision when only matters of subordinate importance are involved. One then prefers to interpret the letter of the existing articles in a somewhat different way, and to achieve, without revisions, by means of modified interpretation what would require too much effort in revision. Just think of the article on education. This then has the consequence that a certain number of desired revisions accumulate, and that eventually, at a more general revision, a clean slate is made. Groen van Prinsterer foresaw from the outset where this would lead. It then gives the appearance as if, time and again, say after half a century or a quarter century, a completely new order of things is established by the people, just as many still imagine that the constitutional order of things here was only established in 1848. This undermines the popular sense that the Constitution has remained one and the same from the outset until now, and that what has changed were merely modifications made to the one permanent State document. Much healthier is the view that one makes the necessary changes by a simpler way of revision on a few detail points at a time. But then it also goes without saying that the revision must be able to come about more smoothly, on a simpler footing and without too much effort. Otherwise, the partial change is constantly postponed, is even partly played off against the constitutional precept in practice, and this then naturally leads to taking all the postponed matters together after a certain period, and thus seeking salvation again in a general revision. If it were the case that one had only gone slightly beyond the just measure in such a rapid partial constitutional revision, then in this more expeditious handling of matters the means would also be provided to correct a mistake made and to go back the required step. §17. THE ATTITUDE OF THE SOVEREIGN - THE THREAT OF POPULAR UNREST.
If it comes to a revision of the Constitution, and the issue lies not between the people and the Sovereign, but between groups, parties, and classes within the people, then there is a case to be made for justice - that the Sovereign should defend the group that is too severely disadvantaged against the encroachment of competing groups. This could lead to a strengthening of a one-sided preponderance, and thus a partisan choice in the most unhealthy sense; but whereas such intervention by the Sovereign was not uncommon in earlier periods, since the Sovereigns then knew how to make one of the parties subservient to their own interests, there is no longer any fear of this in the current constitutional life. A Sovereign who now steps forward for the rights of the various groups and parties is not diminished in general esteem, but rather gives the impression of defending a higher right. As late as 1878, this was evident in the deputation under Elout van Soeterwoude at Het Loo. King William III then made it clear and unambiguous that he was not insensitive to what was lacking in justice in our Elementary Education Act, and the outcome crowned this expression of a sovereign sentiment by already leading public opinion in a different direction ten years later. And if one finally points to the revolutionary turmoil that can seek salvation in street riots in order to enforce the will of the common people, one must not lose sight of the fact that nothing can better curb this ever-recurring danger than a more flexible revision of the constitutional provisions.

§25. The Threat of Popular Unrest. If the revision of the Constitution is made too difficult, and it is thus postponed from year to year, and there is almost no redress in the ordinary way, this is precisely what so easily sets the popular passions in motion and provokes violence. To hold fast to the two distinct conditions - the one for the maintenance of the accord between people and Sovereign, but which must then also have a character of firmness, and the other for the maintenance of the proper connection between political life and the constitutional order - is the task that the Sovereign has to fulfill.

The constitutional form in which this finds expression determines, in my opinion, the standpoint from which one must approach the revision of the Constitution. If the first requirement is rigidity, and the second flexibility and suppleness, it already follows from this how almost impossible it is to unite both under the same point of view. If for the accord between People and Princes regarding the regulation of the rights of the people the same manner of revision applies, then the fateful consequence is that either the revision of the Constitution is made too difficult in order to nail down the accord, or the accord is too much weakened in order to better carry out the revision of the Constitution. The Conservatives were most guilty of the former evil. This gave rise to an ebb and flow, under which the dignity of the constitutional life was indeed damaged. The walls of the building must be firm, and only the doors and windows movable. Nothing has done as much damage to the constitutional certainty as the fact that accords and detailed regulations have been decided under the same law. In Great Britain, where the written document is lacking, but the mutual right is rooted in the spirit of the people, the constitutional life still stands the firmest and strongest. Small oscillations and shakings have certainly also occurred in England, but at least the building itself has not yet been shaken. On the European continent, on the other hand, and also with us, the general satisfaction over our constitutional coexistence has noticeably declined in the last quarter century. Complaints of an irreparable fiasco have already come from more than one side. Those who could now be blamed for this were not, as was previously very common, the princely abuse of power. Precisely over this one-sided evil, however, complaints have become increasingly rare. From the side of the Crown, a spirit has rather penetrated the land that testified to a warm interest in the fate of the people, to an effort to win the love of the people, and to an ever more scrupulous respect for the rights of the people. The dissatisfaction with the existing order has also turned less and less against the Crown. This was still partly the case in 1848. Not anymore now. Rather, the stains are now increasingly visible on the side of the people in the legal community. There is a tendency towards anarchy, almost every bond begins to hinder. Free as the little bird in the air we want to become. Chaotic phenomena are already emerging. Precisely for this reason, it seemed important to place more emphasis on the character of the Constitution, and at least to bring up a way out that could lead to reconciling and uniting the firmness and suppleness in the revision, which are now constantly damaging each other, in such a way that a healthy relationship between the two can finally discard all crutches. That this is possible is not in doubt, provided that the entire Constitution is stripped of all superfluous detailed regulations, i.e. the side lines are erased with the sponge, and one goes back to the main and fundamental lines, in order to express them in such simple, clear and logically thought-out language that the cacography disappears of itself. In this respect, too, our Constitution does not belong to the most fortunate. Although it can least of all unite us with the political principles from which the Belgian Constitution of 1830 proceeds, yet it may not be denied the praise that it remains a model in its language and style. Only by the way it may be noted here that in a division of the Constitution into two main parts, of which the first would contain the accord between People and Prince, and the second would lay down the details of the Constitution, the dividing line need not necessarily fall at Article 79 of our present Constitution. The distribution of the content over the different parts of our current Constitution would of course have to undergo modification.

HISTORY OF THE COUNCIL. 115
The Council of State, as it exists with us, dates from the Constitution of 1815. It is true that already in the Constitution of the Batavian Republic of 1798 a Council of State was provided for, but this was of a different nature. The Council of State, as it was then conceived, was intended to be a kind of executive committee, which would assist the Executive Directory in the administration of the country. The Constitution of 1815, on the other hand, gave the Council of State a completely different character. It was no longer an executive committee, but a purely advisory body, which had to assist the King in the exercise of his royal prerogatives. The Constitution of 1848 did not change this character of the Council of State, but only expanded its competence somewhat. The Constitution of 1815 had given the Council of State the task of advising the King on all matters of legislation and administration, as well as on the granting of pardons. The Constitution of 1848 added to this the task of advising on the appointment of the highest officials. The Council of State thus became a body that had to assist the King in the exercise of his royal prerogatives, both in the legislative and in the administrative field. This character of the Council of State has remained unchanged to this day.
