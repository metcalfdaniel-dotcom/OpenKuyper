PROVINCIAL STATES AND THE FIRST CHAMBER. A comprehensive discussion of the position now assigned to the Provincial States would not be worth the trouble in the practical part of this work. The Provincial States no longer live in the political heart of our people. For the Provincial Water Management interests in Polder and Dike administrations, there is still zeal on the part of those concerned; and although the involvement of those concerned has also gradually diminished in this area, the old desire to tackle things themselves still partly lives on. For the rest, however, the actions of the Provincial States are so little impactful on life that even most of the eligible voters for these colleges are hardly aware of the provincial Hatred.

The administration of the provinces hardly attracts any attention. However, the composition of the Provincial States is of high significance for the political character of the First Chamber, and without exaggeration it can be said that the electoral zeal which manifests itself in the periodic elections for the Provincial States, even if not exclusively, then at least primarily, is aroused by the struggle over the composition of the First Chamber. A very few issues of a purely provincial nature may also, to a certain extent, pique the interest, and it also happens that in the case of two candidates for the same seat, the personal influence of a large landowner or manufacturer may play a role; - but for the body of voters as a whole, the party choice regarding the First Chamber now stands so prominently in the foreground at every provincial election that is released, that at least in the wavering provinces, the political struggle of the parties now dominates the outcome of each ballot box. That this was not the case earlier, and only became so towards the end of the previous century, was due to the fact that in the preceding period the liberal government had filled almost all mayoral positions with men of its own color; so in South Holland, for example, 170 out of the even 180 municipalities. And since, moreover, the preponderance of the liberal members in the First Chamber was so oppressive that not a single one of our representatives, for example, sat there, there was often a lack of any interest in the choice for the Provincial States. And so the relationship remained as it was. Since, however, our party organization had succeeded in finally breaching this political wall, and people began to believe that the transformation of the First Chamber too would ultimately not belong to the impossibilities, there has been an awakened zeal in province after province, which has steadily increased, and has finally succeeded in completely transforming the composition of the First Chamber. A turning point in affairs, which did not, like a mirage, rise up and disappear again in the mists, but which has continued to dominate a considerable part of our political situation. With this, however, the provincial character of the choice for the Provincial States had almost entirely given way to national politics.

§2. No Heart for Provincial Housekeeping. Even among not a few members of the States, interest and zeal in their own provincial housekeeping had gradually diminished. They left the housekeeping to the Provincial Executive Council. After all, this would be well cared for under the supervision and cooperation of the Royal Commissioner. The reports of the meetings of the Provincial States also show how little warm participation their minutes testify to. Increasingly, the handling of provincial affairs was left in a narrower sense to the Commissioners and the Provincial Executive Council. And even if the Perser reported on it, it did not captivate the public. Even the narrower principled organs of the Press could not stimulate interest in it. This might occasionally be the case in matters of agriculture or public works, but the provincial public as such often did not get involved in it. In this result, which has emerged since 1815 as the fruit of a century-long duration, lies partly even a judgment on the regulation that has been given to our provincial affairs. Van Hogendorp had certainly intended it differently, and Professor Buys saw precisely, when he drew the conclusion from the unchanged preservation of what was written in 1815 in Art. 1 of the fourth chapter of the Constitution, Part 1 and 2, that one had not wanted to kill the provincial life, but rather to awaken it. But the outcome, beyond dispute, has not answered that nobler intention. The fear is now that the Provinces would renew their ancient claims to sovereignty. This had to be prevented in every way, yes, the possibility of it had to be cut off. Hence the appointment of the Commissioner, even without nomination, by the King, the concentration of almost all provincial action and authority in the Provincial Executive Council under his leadership, and what did not less tighten the screw, a constant, ever-working magic of the central authority, partly through an ever-expanding legislative power over provincial regulations, partly through the ever-increasing power of the Minister to submit to the Crown the annulment of what the Provincial States had decided. No less than fourteen articles of our Constitution had the tendency to consolidate certain rights and powers to the Provincial States, and this remained so even in 1887. But what Thorbecke also did to breathe new life into the States from the side, their significance never got the so much desired exponent. And since the main weight of the provincial ballot box in the choice of the First Chamber was felt, the shadow that fell on what the States did below it became increasingly gloomy.

§3. Centralization and Decentralization. It is the systems of Centralization and Decentralization that seek the right balance here. Leaving aside Monaco and the other three Lilliput states, Europe has only two Commonwealths, France and Switzerland; but now see the difference in both. In France, they have simply made all the provinces disappear from the national inheritance and thus fragmented the freed inheritance into more than 80 Departments. At the head of each of these 87 Departments, a Prefect has been set up from Paris almost as the sole ruler, and to this Prefect they have added a Departmental “Conseil” that has no other task than to say yes and amen to what the Prefect desires, or at least what the Prefect commands. This decentralization of power has in fact gone so far that the uniformity, as a curse of the modern direction, extends over the entire interior of the French State, and at least after 1870 a certain tendency began to emerge to bring some relaxation into the decentralizing embrace. In this strict centralization, the life-killing principle of the French Revolution expressed itself. Originally, the Kings of France had been almost unlimited rulers as feudal lords. Against their preponderance, the one-man had gradually risen up, so that in the end the King became a roi fainéant. This prompted the Princes to break the mighty estates, so that Louis XIV could finally declare: L’état c’est moi. Against this monarchical preponderance, the tiers état then rose up in revolt, and this brought us the French Revolution. The individualized nation now melted into a centralistic unity, and this unity was then further strengthened by the Napoleonic regime. In Switzerland, on the other hand, the cantons have maintained their ancient sovereignty to this day, and the Confederation has only gradually taken over certain tasks from them. The Confederation has no Prefects, no Departmental Councils, no centralized legislation, no centralized administration. Each canton has its own constitution, its own government, its own legislation, its own administration. The Confederation only takes care of what the cantons have voluntarily entrusted to it. This decentralization has given the Swiss Confederation a vitality and a power of resistance that the centralized French State has never known.

CENTRALIZATION AND DECENTRALIZATION. - SWITZERLAND. The unity was the people, the People. There was now no God, nor Master anymore. The people themselves had become God and Master. Naturally, there could no longer be any talk of an autonomy of the provinces. France became a map, a mosaic of fragments, and all the peculiarities and distinct character of the various historical population agglomerations fell away.

§4. Switzerland. But let us now look at the course of events in Switzerland. Switzerland has never been anything other than a Republic. And yet the preamble to the Constitution of May 20, 1874, even after the amendments of 1908, still reads: “In the name of Almighty God!” Article 1 emphasizes that Switzerland is a Federation, and indeed a Federation of “Völkerschafte,” which politically organized themselves into 22 cantons, and in such a way that each of these 22 cantons acted as sovereign in its own right. They are also called Eidgenossen, because they have bound themselves under oath before God. All the States together exercise their power through a large Federal Assembly (Art. 7) and this Federal Assembly is divided into two sections. One is called “Le Conseil National,” the other “Le Conseil des États.” And now Article 72 stipulates that the National Council is elected by the entire Swiss people, one member for every 20,000 inhabitants, and that the members of this Council are directly elected by the Swiss citizens of 20 years and older. This Council sits for three years and then retires in its entirety. The “Conseil des États,” on the other hand, forms a Senate. In this second Chamber, each canton elects two members. Besides these two representative Bodies, there is now a Council of Administration, the “Conseil fédéral.” This consists of seven members. They are elected as such by the two parliamentary Chambers in joint session; but only for three years. And finally, at the head of the entire Federation stands a President, who is elected for only one year by the federal assembly. There are no Ministers. The President and the members of the “Conseil fédéral” manage the various activities, we would say the ministerial Departments, among themselves. Its members have a seat and the right to speak in the parliamentary assembly,

§5. The Solution in America. What was found as the final solution in America borders very closely on the Swiss system. What the Cantons are in Switzerland, the States are here, whose number is still constantly increasing. Here too, each State is sovereign, although of course this sovereignty can only be nominal. For here too, the Congress stands above each State by itself. This Congress also has its two parts: “The House of Representatives” and the “Senate” (see Art. 1 in the first chapter) approximately what we would call a Second and a First Chamber. The House of Representatives is elected here by the combined population of all the States, and the Senate stands next to it, consisting of two members, each to be appointed by the legislative Body in each State. The members of the legislative Body sit for two years, those of the Senate for six years. Completely outside of this Parliament or Congress, composed of these two parts, stands the President as the executive power. He holds his office for four years. He is elected by the electors who each State appoints for this purpose, in a number equal to the total number of seats it occupies in the Representative Body and in the Senate. Effectively, therefore, by all the voters together, since they elect the Committee members. This President chooses his Ministers at his own discretion, as assistants. But not more than that. §6. Pennsylvania
However, this is not enough. The organization of the individual states of the Union must also be examined more closely, at least with regard to their governing power. As an example, I have chosen Pennsylvania, which promulgated its latest Constitution on December 16, 1873. Pennsylvania had already had three Constitutions. The first came into being on September 28, 1776. The second was proclaimed on September 2, 1790. This later Constitution underwent more or less important changes in 1838, 1850, 1857 and 1864. And it was this Constitution, revised four times, that finally gave way in 1873 to the Constitution to which we now refer. I prefer to point to Pennsylvania among the states of the Union, because this state was originally populated not only by Swedes, but mostly by Dutchmen, even though it was Penn who first gave it a firm form and further development. As I remarked elsewhere earlier, the Dutch element was so strongly represented here from the outset that in the Philadelphia directory there are now no less than five columns of names with “Van” - names that had not been placed, as with us, on the letter of the surname, but that were all included under the prefix “Van”; something that is connected with the almost noble meaning that one attaches to the prefix “Van.” All the more did this large colony of descendants from our country impress me in Philadelphia, because not Philadelphia, but Harrisburg is the capital of Pennsylvania. §7. Failed imitation of our past.
Now, one should keep in mind here that the present regulation of the United States represents the decentralization of power in the Congress. What was first tried in 1876 was an imitation of what had existed among us under the Republic; but as early as 1787, this had been put to an end, in fact illegally, and only from then on has the now still too centralized institution come into being. I have already explained this in my First Part. Here, however, it must be emphasized in particular that provincial autonomy in the United States has come to its full right precisely because it remained connected with the centralized organization of the common Government. What had existed among us under the Commonwealth had made provincial autonomy excessive at the expense of central administration. This erroneous system, which began with the Union of Utrecht, had been judged by history. It had been initially imitated in America, but it did not work; and what the Netherlands owes to the Prince of Orange never comes out better than when one historically examines what has nevertheless been achieved here, in spite of our untenable state organization. But conversely, it also now teaches us from America what a rich provincial autonomy can still very well exist, even if one cuts off what would doom the central power to impotence or helplessness. If we had here what, as I indicated, Pennsylvania possesses, there would be no more complaint about the restriction of provincial life among us.

IN 1815 PROVINCIAL AUTONOMY WAS NEGLECTED. To point this out would now serve no purpose, since all our seven united Provinces before the Union of Utrecht were never anything other than parts of a larger whole. This, however, was certainly not the case in 1581; and if one now compares the so insignificant autonomy that has been left to our Provinces after 1815 with what has been left to the Provincial States in Switzerland and both in South and North America, then the irresistible judgment imposes itself that the autonomy of the individual Provinces has been swallowed up by the then formed national government. In Switzerland too, in later years, more and more thought has been given to encroaching on the autonomy of the Cantons, but a reaction soon set in against this, and during the upheavals that the Great War of 1914 also brought to Switzerland as a border country, even in the French Cantons a not insignificant movement has arisen to strengthen the autonomy of the Cantons again. Now it should be immediately noted that both in Switzerland and with us the smallness of the Cantons and the Provinces worked in favor of this solution of their autonomy in the state unity or in the Empire. In America, on the other hand (not counting the small states on the Atlantic Ocean), one had to deal with states that were almost all much larger than our entire country. Only Holland could once boast of its extent, but this province has also been divided into two parts. And if one now objects that in Switzerland and America republics prevailed, and with us after 1813 a monarchy, we too gladly emphasize this distinction, but cannot concede that this had to lead to such a fatal destruction of autonomy. What has worked so disastrously here is twofold. Firstly, the example of the French Revolution, which simply cut up the French provinces and sought salvation in the Departments; but then also no less the condition that has prevailed in England since William the Conqueror. In England, you see, the autonomy of the ancient provinces has been almost completely abolished, the central government has become the sole legislator and power holder, and what in England alone gave the necessary counterweight, was the self-government that had been so powerfully carried out here as well. Something of this was also intended here; but the fact is that it was the French and English influences that have largely dominated the disposition of our leading statesmen since 1815; and this explains why from the very harsh autonomy of the days of the Commonwealth, after 1815 almost nothing remained, even if it was intended otherwise by Van Hogendorp. And although it may be said that Thorbecke in 1848 succeeded in at least somewhat loosening the oppressive bond of unity, Thorbecke too did not restore autonomy to our Provinces in any significant sense.

§11. Autonomy and Self-Government. Yet again, the distinction that Buys pointed out comes to the fore: not autonomy, but self-government. That is indeed the actual state of affairs. Especially when it comes to the provinces, the law comes from above, and the province is accountable to the one who gave the law; and even insofar as they retained some own initiative in matters of roads, canals, water management affairs, and now presumably also in the matter of electricity connections, the central action from above dominates the entire provincial life. On the other hand, the multifaceted activity entrusted to the Provincial Administration in the execution of the laws, especially also in relation to the Municipalities, the polders, and the peat bogs, is under pressure; but even in this multifaceted domain, one is increasingly moving in the direction of also intervening from higher up by law in these matters. Thus, the free initiative is steadily diminishing in the provincial domain. Much, very much, remains the meddling, but it is increasingly taking on the character of subordinate labor performed at the behest of the Central power. The complaint that has just now, as we indicated, again arisen in Switzerland against this ever-increasing centralization power would therefore also be fully justified here. Ever less remains of the ancient autonomy of the Municipalities. Just as the Mayors and Municipal Administrations are also increasingly overburdened with work flowing from the law, so too the Provincial States are increasingly reduced to mere execution, and for a not insignificant part under the leadership of an outsider appointed as Chairman, the so-called King’s Commissioner.
§12. Our Commissioners. Also a brief word about this highly placed official. In Switzerland and in the American Republics, the Canton or State elects its own highest dignitary. With us, on the other hand, he is appointed by the Crown, entirely from the outside. There is no nomination, there is no consultation. From the Government Gazette, the Province learns of the appointment of the King’s Commissioner, who then takes up his position.

AUTONOMY AND SELF-GOVERNANCE - OUR COMMISSIONERS The provincial commissioner, whoever he may be, lacks all initiative, and even the cooperation that could come from the Province is absent. Indeed, the very title of “Commissioner” has something about it that repels rather than attracts. The old title of “Stadtholder” at least had this in its favor, that it drew a connection to the Sovereign, however tenuous. The title “Governor” chosen in 1813 at least aimed at rule and administration. But “Commissioner” is a mere agent, and conveys no other impression than that of a higher power exercising control over you. We cannot but consider this title as not particularly well-chosen. And this is all the more true, since differences of opinion arise between the Commissioner and the Provincial States, the former having the right to suspend execution and seek support from the Ministry of the Interior. Even his vote in the meetings of the Provincial States is questionable. The Provincial States and the Provincial Executive are bodies emerging from the electorate. In such a body, where the majority, as in the Provincial Executive, already consists of a few persons, to give the Commissioner who has come from outside not merely the right of advice, but the full right to vote, means that it is the Commissioner who so often casts the deciding vote. If there are five or seven Provincial Executive members, this easily splits into two and three, or four and three, and depending on how the Commissioner votes, the outcome is determined. To this is added that as the network of legal provisions enveloping our entire system of government becomes more extensive, multifaceted and intricate, fewer members of those States are capable of feeling at home in this web, and more than one member of the Provincial Executive sometimes loses their way in this web. §11. The Municipality Autonomous

The mayor’s authority is a delegated power. §14. Self-government. It seems that the need to limit the power of the so-called autonomous Municipalities has arisen primarily because an overly one-sided right of interference was granted to the Council. For according to our Municipal Law, the Municipal Council is not only called upon to promote the general interests and to draw up and establish the necessary ordinances, but is also called upon to cooperate in the execution of what has been ordered from a higher authority or regulated and ordained by the Council itself. As far as autonomy has remained with the local circles of the people, it must be exercised by the Council. More difficult, because more secret in nature, is the struggle against brutal immorality; for here one has to contend with immoral notions which, instead of gradually disappearing, are rather gaining ground. Immorality is naturally connected with the question of marriage, and one knows how many families in almost all circles are, where the bond of marriage is becoming ever looser. §11. Public Poor Relief. The issue of public poor relief is much more complex. In Christian lands, the most desirable state of affairs is that the civil municipality can entrust the care of the poor entirely to those powers who do what they do for the poor out of the impulse of love. First and foremost, the Church comes into consideration here, with its Diaconate so rich in potential. In earlier centuries, when Church and State were one, this flowed naturally. One did not feel like two, but like one, even under that duality. The care of the sick would only very rarely fall to the civil administration. An epidemic or a state of war might call forth an extraordinary need in life that demanded care, but in ordinary life it was the Church, it was the monastery, it was the private association that provided for all the needs of the poor. This could happen earlier because the civil municipality declined what wanted to establish itself without proper means of subsistence. But since then, the state of affairs in this respect has been thoroughly changed. Especially the rather too large Reformed Church excludes a very large part of the population that only belongs to it in name, but neither in faith nor in ecclesiastical service, lives with it and gives to it. Hence, it is often simply impossible for this Church to provide for the full need of its poor, as regards the care of the sick and the distribution of alms. §21. In the Service of the Municipality.
A Municipal Administration cannot fulfill its task, if it cannot count on the help of the serving personnel, whether officials, civil servants or laborers. §23. Justices of the Peace. The withholding from the Municipality of all legal competence does injustice to its character of self-reliance. More strongly than ever before, it is felt in all the land that a distinction must be made between what is a transgression against the common law and what are civil disputes and disturbances of order. As long as one had to deal here exclusively with rural hamlets and villages, under a predominantly male population of only a few hundred persons, it was still very well possible to assemble together, deliberate together and decide together. But when, on the contrary, the population in a place began to produce some thousands of persons entitled to speak, this was no longer possible, and then the obvious custom naturally came into use, that the too many let themselves be represented by a smaller number; and so one then spontaneously obtained representative bodies in Council or State. And this was all the more necessary because of what was stated in the second place, namely, that one no longer had to reckon only with the village housing, but also with the common interests of a group of villages or hamlets in the same district or province, and furthermore had a share in the care of the interests of an entire People. This goal could not be achieved in large popular assemblies of 50 to 60,000 persons, and therefore one had to resort to elections to appoint a much smaller number of representatives, who would take care of all common interests. It is true that we read in the history of Israel that either a prophet like Samuel or shortly afterwards a Prince summoned the whole People together, but this may not now be understood as if all persons from all tribes flocked together. Such gatherings were popular assemblies, just as the Germans later also knew them, so that one no longer came together with all and sundry, but that the persons entitled to vote in a certain district or province elected from among themselves a smaller number of representatives.

§31. Historical Development. This transition now from the original popular assemblies to the representative system did not everywhere come about by choice. §46. The Calvinistic standpoint. The Calvinist is therefore already offended by the very idea of compulsory voting.
