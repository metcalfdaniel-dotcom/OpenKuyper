THE COUNCIL OF STATE AND THE MINISTERS OF STATE. A republic in 1805, and even already under Charles V, had a nearly identically named body established, but these previously existing colleges had a different character, because they saw themselves as partly entrusted with the task that has since fallen to the Departmental Ministers. In 1805, therefore, alongside the Council of State, not ministers but Secretaries of State were appointed. See Articles 47 and 48. Article 47 provided for the appointment of a General Secretary of State, and Article 48 the functions of five Secretaries of State, for Foreign Affairs, for the Navy, for War, for the Interior, and for Finance. The Council of State, on the other hand, which was regulated by Articles 44, 45 and 46, seemed more like the Conseil d’État of the Burgundian Princes and the creation of similar colleges by Napoleon. In Articles 71—74 of the Constitution of 1815, a Council of State of a different character emerged, which was appointed by the King, would not have more than twenty-four members, preferably from the different Provinces, whose members the King could appoint and dismiss at will. The King himself would be the chairman of this Council, the Prince of Orange a member by right, and the other Princes of the Royal House could also be granted a seat in this high State body by the Sovereign. All legislative proposals came up for consideration in this Council, and the King sought the advice of this Council on all measures of domestic administration, both here and for the Colonies of the Kingdom. Furthermore, the King could ask this Council for advice on all State affairs that he considered desirable to have such advice on. However, the decisions were made by the King alone. The Council merely received notification of this decision. Only after this had been so regulated in Articles 71—74, did the establishment of the Ministerial Departments follow in Article 75; and in the second paragraph here it was stipulated that the King could call one or more Ministers into the Council of State. The three articles for the more detailed regulation of the ministerial powers and obligations were only inserted in 1840. It cannot be denied, therefore, that in 1815 the Council of State was very much superior to the Departmental Ministers. Even in the Constitution of 1848, the establishment of the Council of State in Section VII of Chapter II still preceded the establishment of the Departmental Ministers. Two articles (Articles 71 and 72) were even devoted to the establishment of the Council of State here, and only one to the establishment of the Ministers. The Princes now fell out, and only the Prince of Orange remained. The task of the college was now to advise on all proposals for laws and general administrative measures, both here and in the Colonies and possessions. The King could also elicit advice on other State affairs. It remained the case that the King alone decided and merely communicated his decision to the Council. Just as in 1815, it was stipulated that the “having heard the Council of State” should appear in the title of every law. Furthermore, the law was also to regulate the entire constitution of the Council of State. In 1887 all this remained the same, but now a new addition was made in Article 76, that a law could entrust the decision on disputes to the Council of State or to a particular section of this Body. The more detailed regulation of one and the other has been effected by the Acts of 21 December 1861, 28 June 1881, 11 July 1884, and 8 December 1906, and by the Royal Decrees of 4 September 1862, 29 March 1875, 8 December 1877, 16 November 1881, 13 October 1905 and 28 March 1907 pursuant to these Acts. A separate Section was now established for Administrative Disputes, so that in the end the Administrative Jurisdiction also found its regulation.

§2. The three extra cases. The entirely separate task assigned to the Council of State for the three cases described in Article 45 lies entirely outside of this and is included in Chapter II, Section 4, dealing with the Regency. Article 38 regulates what must be done by the heads of the Ministerial Departments in the matter of the Regency; but when the exercise of the Royal Authority becomes unavoidable in the absence of the Bearer, it is not the Cabinet, but the Council of State that steps in, and it is not the least of this assignment that has stamped a seal of very high quality on the college. Just as throughout the past, so also from this assignment to the eventual exercise of the Royal Authority, it appears that the Council of State stands in very close connection with the Crown. Something that also applies elsewhere.

THE THREE EXTRA CASES. 117 Professor Bluntschli in his Allgemeines Staatsrecht rightly points out that the Council of State in the Netherlands has a special position compared to the Councils of State in other countries. This special position is mainly due to the three extra cases mentioned in Article 45, in which the Council of State is called upon to act. These three cases are: 1. When the King is a minor; 2. When the King is incapacitated; 3. When the throne is vacant. In these three cases, the Council of State does not merely advise, but actually exercises the Royal Authority. This is a very special task, which gives the Council of State a completely different character than the usual advisory body. It is not just a consultative body, but in these three cases it is the governing body. This is why the Council of State in the Netherlands has a much more prominent position than the Councils of State in other countries. The fact that the Council of State is called upon to exercise the Royal Authority in these three cases shows how closely it is connected to the Crown. This close connection with the Crown is also evident from the fact that the King himself is the chairman of the Council of State, and that the Princes of the Royal House have a seat in it by right. All this gives the Council of State in the Netherlands a very special character, which sets it apart from the Councils of State in other countries.

§3. Weakening of significance. The fact that from a more revolutionary side, one was always intent on removing the Council of State, as having become altogether superfluous, leads to the same conclusion. In Belgium, this has already come about in 1830, and also among us, from the liberal side, attempt after attempt has been made to make the Council of State disappear from our institutions. And although this has not yet been achieved, it cannot be denied that the legal regulation given to this Council has not enhanced its prestige in the life of the State. What Article 45 assigns to the Council, in the event of the immediate disturbance of the Crown Bearer, or also what is assigned to the Council in its Administrative Division, is undoubtedly of very high importance, but these two tasks are of an incidental nature, and the main task of the Council of State remains always to lie in the advice it provides to the King, either according to the law, or at the King’s special request; and precisely as regards these opinions, the significance of the Council of State is no longer rated very highly. The reason for this is not far to seek. It lies in its composition and in its authority so severely limited by law. Its composition, it is true, proceeds from the King, but is in fact entirely dominated by the Minister of the Interior, under the control of the Cabinet. The time also lies so far behind us that the successive liberal Cabinets almost never presented to the King for membership anyone other than kindred spirits. Often, it is true, a limited number of Roman Catholic statesmen were also given a seat, but previously, at least, the choice for this was easily made from among statesmen who still felt entirely homogeneous with the Liberals.

WEAKENING OF SIGNIFICANCE - CABINET AND COUNCIL. Only in a later period did this high college also include statesmen from Anti-Revolutionary or non-liberal Roman Catholic backgrounds. The nature of the college entailed that it possessed a more lasting character than could be attributed to most Cabinets, and that it thereby represented more the general course of political life. The ministers came and went, continually varying in their efforts and intentions. In contrast to this variability in Cabinet life, the Crown Bearer represented the permanent and as much as possible unchanging, and only in this permanent could the Council of State provide support. Now, on the other hand, as the composition of the Council of State began to suffer under ministerial pressure along with the constant change inseparable from the spirit of the Cabinets, the Council increasingly ceased to possess a character of its own, and, though at a certain distance, continually followed the ebb and flow in Cabinet life. The original intention behind the Council’s actions, to uphold the permanent, characteristic nature of the royal authority against the ever-changing in Cabinet life, was thus increasingly lost.

§4. Cabinet and Council.*
The original independence diminished. And since the advice of the Council was not made public, nor the response of the Ministers to the Council’s criticism, it could not but happen that in the ministerial bureaus less and less attention was paid to the advice received from the Council. By virtue of its one-sided composition, the Council was generally unable to issue other than broadly liberal advice, with the peculiar mark that almost always, when the drafts tended in a more radical direction, the criticism of the Council pushed them back towards a more conservative line. When such a tempering opinion came from the King’s side to the Department, the Department’s immediate effort was to set it aside as very little to the point and as insignificant in a briefly written counter-opinion.

As a college of advice on matters of state, the Council of State has hardly ever been of service; its influence on legislation has become increasingly insignificant; and undoubtedly administrative jurisdiction and the temporary bearing of royal authority under Article 45 are now its main significance.

§6. Administrative Jurisdiction. Now it is granted that this last, entirely exceptional function fits in a very peculiar way with a Council such as the one dealt with here. Under normal circumstances, the Council would never be called upon to act in this matter. If there is a successor to the throne, if it is necessary to entrust a Regent, and if at the moment of the Prince’s demise, in the first case the Prince or Princess, and in the other case the Regent or Regentess, are present on the spot, the Council of State sees itself called to no act or deed. Only in abnormal circumstances, if there is no successor to the throne, or if such a Regent is absent, would the Council of State have to intervene. Such intervention now forms an essential part of the task that has been laid upon the President of the Council, but in the ordinary course of events the cases to which this task refers hardly ever occur. It would never have been thought of to call such a college into being solely for this purpose. Elsewhere, in more than one country where no Council of State has been established, the possibility has nevertheless been reckoned with that such a case of embarrassment might arise, and it has then been entrusted to the Council of Ministers what with us fell to the Council of State. Now this is not quite the same. It must be admitted that the Council of State, which from the outset had the purpose of raising a shield for the Crown, leans more peculiarly towards exercising the royal authority than the Council of Ministers; but even if we are willing to emphasize this, it would not do to maintain such a costly college mainly with a view to such a case, which is almost never likely to occur. It calls for much more definite and extensive work; but for such a certainly highly necessary jurisdiction, a Council of State such as we possess does not seem to be the most peculiar and self-evident college.

§7. How it is organized elsewhere
In Switzerland, a state even smaller than the Netherlands, a separate college has been established for this jurisdiction in the Tribunal fédéral (see Articles 106–114 of the Constitution). This Tribunal rules on disputes between the Confederation and the Cantons, between the Cantons themselves, and also on disputes between the Confederation or Cantons with corporations or individuals. Furthermore, this Tribunal can also adjudicate in any other type of dispute if both parties request it to do so. It is also entrusted with jurisdiction over “conflicts of competence” between national and cantonal authorities; over public-law disputes between Cantons; and over complaints by individuals regarding the violation of their public and constitutional rights. Even the federal legislation, by virtue of Articles 110 and 113 of the Constitution, is empowered to assign other conflicts to this Tribunal for adjudication. Although the scope of this task does not quite match what falls under our administrative jurisdiction, in principle and in its starting point one finds here as well a regulation of administrative jurisdiction, but one that, departing from an independent college specifically established for this purpose, is added to a college of a completely different origin and character; and even then, not assigned to the entire college, but only to a specific department of it. So in other countries as well, recourse has been sought in the establishment of an independent college. Consider, for example, the Reichsgericht in Austria, which was called into being by the Constitutional Law of December 21, 1867 and established in Vienna. Article 2 of this Law stipulates that the jurisdiction of this Reichsgericht includes competence disputes between judicial and administrative authorities, between the representation of the People and the magistrates in the various provinces, between the local authorities, and also over disputes between municipalities, corporations, or individuals with the authorities of various ranks. §7. Administrative Jurisdiction and Autonomy. What speaks even more strongly in this regard is that the Council of State, by virtue of its origin, can least of all be regarded as the designated college for administrative jurisdiction. Between the authorities of higher and lower rank, between those authorities and both public and private associations and private individuals, disputes and conflicts constantly arise, in which the question always arises as to what extent the municipalities, provinces, polders and so many others have or do not have a certain independent position. The autonomy of local and regional character is a matter of the utmost delicacy, and in the care of which two different opinions have long stood opposed to each other here at home. On the one hand, the excessive fragmentation of the state, and on the other hand, a strong centralization spirit. The former has been at home here for a long time, the latter gained the upper hand in France, especially during the Revolution, and has also come to us from Paris and gained dominance in the days of the Batavian Republic. And although this all-solving spirit of centralization was thwarted here after 1814, it cannot be denied that our Liberals have always shown a poorly concealed preference for that centralization, and that the autonomous life, even in our polder administrations, has always been curtailed by them. That the professor Oppenheim took up the cause of municipal autonomy again with love and faithfulness may elicit our gratitude, but it can hardly be denied that, to avoid difficulties, one has always been more inclined here to let the autonomous character of municipality and province suffer, if that could facilitate the orderly course of administration.

§9. The Council is unfit for this. One might therefore have expected that the administration of justice in conflicts of this nature would be entrusted either to a college dominated by the autonomous, or to a college in which both the centralizing and the autonomy-minded element were equally represented. But the current regulation among us does not meet this requirement. The inherent character of the Council of State, by virtue of its origins and past, has always leaned one-sidedly towards the side of the Crown, whose shield is its vocation. And although the later regulation of the Constitution and the law has not entirely disowned this original character, but has considerably weakened it, in no case can it be claimed that the Council of State is a college in which attachment to the autonomous has the same weight as attachment to the centralizing.

THESE COUNCILS ARE UNSUITABLE.127 In our local, provincial, and corporate life, they were naturally represented. The modern State idea, which does not aim to allow a national life that has arisen from within to ripen in a more organic connection, but rather imagines that the power lies within it to create a political form for the national life, is increasingly gliding over the earlier antithesis between autonomous local life and its central integration. A separate administrative and legal college, established specifically for this purpose, even if it came into being with the cooperation of the autonomous life, would at least have intended to provide a certain counterweight. As it is now regulated with us, however, it cannot be otherwise—the preference for the spirit of centralization will rather continue to push back the autonomous life even further. In fact, there are now only a very small number of gentlemen and jurists, who are often deliberately appointed with a view to the Division of Disputes, who have the entire swell of the autonomous against the central principle in their power. The purpose for which the Council of State was originally called into being among us, the further regulation that this Council received in 1861, 1881 and 1884, gave the Council a composition and a character that would have provided full guarantee if the autonomy were to run wild and try to do damage to the central character of the supreme authority, but in which, under the present regulation, almost every guarantee is lacking for maintaining in our state law what the principle of autonomy demands. §10. The second point demands no less. The second task assigned to the Council of State, to oversee the legislation and the executive power, leads to an equally unsatisfactory conclusion. The regulations for this task are given in Articles 20–26 of the Act of 1861, with the later amendments made therein. Art. 20 merely states the commission given to the Council by Art. 45 of the Constitution, and the subsequent articles stipulate: 1° that all legislative proposals, whether originating from the Crown or the States-General, are to be submitted to the Council for consideration; 2° that all general measures of domestic administration for the State and its Colonies and possessions in other parts of the world are to be submitted to the Council; 3° that the Council is to be heard by the Crown regarding the annulment of decisions of the Provincial and Deputed States, as well as of local ordinances; 4° that the Council is to be heard by the Crown in the cases where the Law commands it; 5° that the Council is to be heard on all matters of general or particular importance which the Crown deems necessary; 6° that the Council can make recommendations to the Crown regarding subjects of legislation or administration, insofar as it deems the issuance of general measures of domestic administration desirable; and 7° that the Sections of the Council are to provide information, when requested, regarding administration and legislation to the Ministers. In short, this amounts to a certain control over the departmental activities, both in what they accomplish and in what they are deemed to devise. That this is intended to fulfill a very real need cannot be disputed. There must be, in legislation and administration, a certain connection between what is accomplished in successive periods of government. Now that the Cabinets change and not infrequently one appears to undo the work of its predecessor, the danger undoubtedly arises that the Cabinet changes disrupt the coherence of the administration. Since a Minister can take office who is less familiar with the secrets of administration, what emanates from the Ministers can become unmistakably lacking in solidity and coherence.

COUNCIL OF MINISTERS. The influence of the Ministers has steadily increased in significance, and yet they are often regarded in the Departments as lodges that come and go without often leaving many traces of their ministerial tenure. More than once it has been pointed out how this has sometimes even given some departmental officials an influence on the course of the country’s affairs that does not rightfully belong to them. Since such officials often stand at the head of an important division for a long series of years, their gradual accumulation of knowledge of the matters handled, at least in the details, far exceeds the familiarity of the Ministers with this subject matter. §12. Before documents are issued. Yet, in practical terms, a serious control over what will be issued, before it is issued, is not only desirable but absolutely necessary. Such control is indispensable, not only regarding the substance, but even the form. Before any document reaches the States-General or is published in the Official Gazette, it ought to have been examined by the government to see whether it is in harmony with the entire body of legislation, the Constitution foremost, and further whether it fits the existing modes of execution and administration. It is also important to consider whether the forms and language fit the whole of our existing legislative and administrative style. One can give a very different form to a draft law. The English, German, and French ways of framing a law of a certain scope are by no means the same. It often happens that in one and the same Cabinet the various Departments proceed according to different methods. This must not be. The Netherlands, too, must have its own conception of the theory of law-making, and it makes a very undesirable impression when one sees law after law appear, of which the one follows the German, the other the English method, to the utter absence of a national form of our own. This even extends to the language; something that deserves special mention, because it is especially the language in our legislation that has always left so much to be desired—a defect that is even aggravated if the language in one official document speaks so differently from another. Even the Constitution suffers from this, and anyone who takes the trouble to compare, for example, the Belgian Constitution with ours, immediately feels how in the French a perfection of style, and even of linguistic form, has been achieved that puts us to shame. Even the choice of words in our legislation often leaves much to be desired.

§13. Waste of time without purpose.
One is therefore confronted with the undeniable fact that a high college of state has often spent too much time on investigating and issuing an opinion that hardly yields any positive gain, and that, on the other hand, the careful control that should have come, fails to materialize. In England, they have followed this rule from of old, and they have found it good. To come out for or against this or that political tendency would of course have to lie entirely outside the vocation of such a College. §8. This struggle is now over.
One may say that we have now moved beyond the theory of the opposite. The forced maneuvers of the old Mr. Heemskerk have made us definitively abandon all resistance to the ordinary rule. The ballot box, not the Crown, determines the political direction in which the country will be governed; and not on the Crown that appoints, but on the Statesmen who follow the call to take a seat in the new Cabinet, rests the responsibility for what will then come to pass. Especially from the circle of Da Costa were ideas propagated among us that were connected with his incorrect conception of the authority of the Constitution; but Groen van Prinsterer soon saw through this and was quick to attack it. Only a coup d’état can make a breach here, but for a coup d’état no argument can be made in theory. Whoever resorts to such an attack on the state order acknowledges for himself that he is resorting to a completely arbitrary act. Not until afterwards can the necessity of such an attack be argued, and this argument must then show that the country was indeed in political danger of life. This threat can come from two sides. There can be a danger to the life of the country in the international relations, which, if there is to be any rescue, necessitates immediate and unconditional decisive action. Or also, there can be a threat to the established order of things by Jacobin unrest and by an unscrupulous Fronde. But it cannot be claimed that a coup d’état would always in itself be justified; as a rule, this is too dangerous an undertaking to be resorted to. In the theory of constitutional law, there is no place for such an attack on the established order of things. The rule must remain that the Ministers are the intermediaries who connect the Crown and the People with each other, and the less theorized is made of their mutual relationship, and the more calmly and quietly the Crown allows the course of electoral life to take its course, the more firmly the Majesty of the Crown will take root. Just as one knows from a child who constantly pulls up a young plant to examine the just emerging roots, how he thereby ruins the regular growth of the plant and ultimately causes it to die completely. And so also of the monarchical plant it can be said, that it thrives all the better the more quietly and normally one allows its roots to work through. Nothing weakens its vital force as much as precisely this constant meddling with the roots of the monarchical being. Only letting it grow quietly promises bloom here as well.

The Ministers are not the representatives of the people, but the confidants of the Crown. They are not the leaders of the people, but the intermediaries between Crown and People. Their task is not to carry out the will of the people, but to advise the Crown on how to govern the country. The Crown remains the sovereign, the Ministers are merely the servants of the Crown. This is the theory, but in practice it has turned out quite differently. In practice, the Ministers have become the leaders of the people, and the Crown has become the servant of the Ministers. The people no longer look to the Crown, but to the Ministers, for the direction of the country’s policy. And the Crown, in order to maintain its position, has had to submit to the will of the Ministers. This is the reality of the situation, and it is a reality that cannot be ignored.

The seventh division of the Second Chapter of the Constitution has the title: “Of the Council of State and the Ministerial Departments.” It does not say: “Of the Council of State and the Council of Ministers,” but: “and of the Ministerial Departments”—each on its own. Only in Article 38 does the Constitution discuss the case where the King is unable to govern, and then, it states, “the heads of the Ministerial Departments shall act in consultation.” This somewhat peculiar expression shows that whoever formulated it did not think of an official body called the Cabinet. §10. A Fixed Chairman? The only thing that remained limping was the question whether the Cabinet Formateur would be the permanent fixed Chairman, or whether the various Ministers, in turn, would be entrusted with the leadership of the whole. Initially, they did not want to allow a fixed presidium. In a fixed sequence, each Minister then received the gavel in turn, and only the duration of each period still differed. The presidium could change by the month, by the two months, and so on. This was relatively indifferent, but a continuous chairmanship was in any case excluded. And this is precisely what has now been changed in the 1901 Regulations, so that the Queen appoints the Chairman, and the Minister chosen by her presides as long as it seems desirable to her. If one asks what preference was given earlier to a presidium that alternated, this is to be sought in two things. On the one hand, each Minister wished to remain free, to be on an equal footing with his colleagues, not to be under collegial pressure, and, as far as some leadership was indispensable, to be able to share in giving that leadership with his fellow officials. It should not be a Council that came together; it had to be a loose gathering of equal officials. And it was precisely this equality that was threatened if one of them held the gavel throughout the entire four-year period. It was precisely against the appearance and the corps that objections were raised. The system should not become a college. The Ministers had to be completely independent persons who only came together now and then as having a common interest, to discuss and decide on that common interest.

§11. Homogeneity. One knows how Groen van Prinsterer constantly insisted on this homogeneous character of the administration. A Cabinet composed of Ministers each pursuing their own path and each seeking to advance their own political direction would soon become the plaything of the States-General. No “unified” force could emanate from it, because it was not one. Even in the ancient name of “Cabinet” lay the idea of a unity of firm cohesion. The more the involvement of the Government in the life of the people expanded, the more urgent the need to ensure that one Minister did not work against another. Even the Tinhoven-Tak Cabinet, may its memory be cursed, has taught us how fatal a lack of homogeneity can ultimately become for the entire Cabinet. But it follows from this that it was not enough to secure this homogeneity in the Cabinet’s program at the time of its formation; it was equally necessary, indeed even more necessary, to maintain that homogeneity throughout the further conduct of the administration. Even the order in which subjects were to be addressed had to be firmly assured, lest one Minister cause another to stumble. But all of this was absolutely unattainable if first one, then another Minister in the college presided. Whoever presides not only holds the gavel, but also always wields no small influence over the course of affairs. It is therefore of the utmost importance for all the Ministers together that this influence emanating from the leadership should always move in a uniform direction, remain in conformity with the program, and proceed without resistance or delay.

§13. An Equal Voice for All. No less important a question is whether all Ministers in the Cabinet should have an equal voice in voting. At least when it comes to making serious political decisions, this can be a weighty matter. Without falling into one-sidedness, it may be asserted that the technical ministers as a rule were not the leading predecessors in the political arena.
