Fourthly, we are dealing with a legal ordinance in the prohibition against eating from the Tree of the Knowledge of Good and Evil. For this involved a limitation of the right that had been granted in general. That generally granted right is here repeated and confirmed in the words: “of every tree of this Garden you may freely eat.” This expresses that they have a right of disposal over another’s goods, and therefore must be reckoned with the will of the owner. If someone allows me into their house, it by no means follows that I have the right to use everything found in that house. That right must first be granted to me by the owner, and this granting of the right—emphasis is again placed on this—takes place in the words: “of every tree of this Garden you may freely eat,” words which are all the more emphatically a legal ordinance because the prohibition immediately follows, not to touch in any case that one tree in the middle of the Garden, which was designated as the Tree of the Knowledge of Good and Evil. The first change that now takes place concerns the relationship in which man will henceforth stand towards the animals. For not only does man retain dominion over the animals, as he previously possessed in greater measure, but now “the animal world is given into his hand” - that is, man may now, according to divine ordinance, dispose not only over tree-fruits and grain, but henceforth “over everything that moves and lives, that shall be food for you.” On the other hand, God takes the protection of human life under His care, both against the fury of wild beasts and against the raging of the elements. As for the former, it is declared: “Surely I will require your lifeblood from the hand of every beast.” And as for the latter, the promise is given that the Flood will not be repeated. The execution of judgment as vengeance for the sin of the first generation is terrifying. All that remains outside the ark is condemned to death and drowns in the waves. And finally, to avert the repetition of the sin of Cain, there is yet another highly important change in the right established by God, namely this addition, that “whoever sheds the blood of man, by man shall his blood be shed.” For God has thus ordained it: Cain was not to be killed.
