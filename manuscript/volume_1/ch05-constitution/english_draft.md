Chapter II THE NAME “STATE”
§1. In Antiquity. The name “State” is now in general use for a political community, though comparatively only since the rise of the modern era. Antiquity did not know this thing, and had no word for it. Nor did the Middle Ages hear of the State. Even in the 16th century, the word appeared only with a very few writers, and it was not until the 18th century that its use became more general. As for antiquity, every Bible scholar knows that the name “State” is not found at all in Holy Scripture. There is talk here of “peoples” and, in distinction from these Goyim or Ethnê, of Israel as Ha-Am or Laos, but a general name for the political community is lacking.

The Bible knows the expression “kingdom,” both in the sense of the Kingdom of God and in the sense of lands ruled by an earthly sovereign. It even knows of a “world empire” (see Daniel 7:13), but neither in the Old nor in the New Testament does the word “state” or any equivalent expression occur. Nor is the general term “state” known to ancient Greek thought. They organized themselves not as states, but as cities. Hence the word “polis,” meaning “city,” gave its name to all political life, all the more so as the concept of “polis” also included the territory surrounding the city or its colonial possessions. The Romans, whose domain eventually encompassed the entire known world at the time, used expressions such as civitas, urbs, and municipium, and alongside these the terms regnum and imperium, or Senatus populusque, or even the more general term respublica. But originally, these did not at all denote the anti-monarchical state form in the sense of our common existence, nor exclusively the popular association, but rather any highest political association, and thus in principle even the city, just as with the Greeks it was with the polis. For what lay outside the Roman Empire, they spoke of gentes, or peoples, just as in Scripture these gentes or peoples are considered less worthy under the name of Goyim or Leummim.

§2. In the Middle Ages. When in the Middle Ages the “Holy Roman Empire” sought to introduce a new imperial unity for the entire civilized world, the tendency naturally arose to regard the various Christianized lands as dependent on this Imperial Empire. But soon resistance arose, especially from the side of France and England, and the centuries-long struggle between the Guelfs and Ghibellines began to strengthen this resistance, although there was as yet no question of a fully equal standing alongside the Holy Roman Empire. The idea of the unity of Christendom could not yet be abandoned, even after the defection of the Eastern Church. There was as yet no thought of the idea that Europe would consist of a broader series of equal kingdoms, and above all, the pressing advance of Islam, and especially of the Turks, was the reason why one refused to dissolve the unity into a multiplicity of kingdoms. Not least the Crusades upheld the idea of unity, so that the need for a common term for the political association of the various independent peoples was still lacking. It was only in Italy that the unity gradually crumbled as a result of the conflict between Emperor and Pope, and no less did the rise of urban city-states under the influence of the Renaissance favor this new state of affairs. Pisa, Genoa, Venice, and many other cities sought to realize a condition similar to that which had once existed in Athens and elsewhere, with the city as the center of a kind of dependent political territory. In Latin, this was then called, as of old, a respublica, or also a civitas, and only when Latin began to lose its monopoly as the official language and the language of scholarship did one take recourse, in order to express what was common to all these Italian republics, to the word état.

§3. Transition to the modern era.
In the sense in which we now speak of “the States of Europe,” and even of “the States of the world,” the name “State” was hardly used at all among us, except under the direct influence of Italian or French writers. Even Kiliaen does not yet know it in this sense in his Etymologicum. In Verdam’s Middle Dutch Dictionary, it occurs as little under the word “Staet” as under the word “State” in the sense of respublica. Even now, the use of the word “State” is at least among our people limited to groups with an independent political bond. Just as in the days of the Commonwealth, we still always speak of the States General, but now in a modified meaning. The States General was then the expression of the central governing power in the Seven Provinces, so much so that Littré in his Dictionary gives the word “États généraux” as the designation of the government of the Netherlands; now it is the name for our Parliament. The name Council of State, note well, with State in the singular, has undoubtedly been an imitation of the French Conseil d’état, but the name has nevertheless remained to remind us of the old Council of State, as it was established under the monarchical rule of Emperor Charles in 1531. Alongside the States General, the name of the Provincial States still lives among us, and the Provincial States are again distinguished from the Deputed States as a moderamen. These now characterized expressions all point back to the ancient meaning of the word State, which was connected with status. It then referred to the distinct groups in the population that held an independent position of power, and the name of the state passed over to the person who represented the position of power of the group. The person was thus not a State in his own right, but by virtue of a right of representation or mandate. Something that must always be kept in mind is that the Latin Status, from which our state is derived in all these distinct meanings, by no means meant a territory or estate, but originally nothing other than condition. One still speaks of the state of death, of a defective state, of the state of marriage, etc., in exactly the same sense. These groups in the population that held a higher and more or less independent state within the whole of the people, then formed a distinct or separate state, and this expression of state passed over by itself to him who represented this position, this higher state, this state and the relative power of his group. There was certainly a concept of partial independence expressed, almost always in relation to equal powers of other estates or groups. The power arose from the partial independence of the distinct groups, estates, cities or provinces, but could only be exercised in the mutual bond of the joint States. Now that later the unity of Christendom, as it had existed and flourished under the Popes and the Emperor, had fallen apart, it was quite natural that the distinct peoples of Europe were each intent on acquiring their own national position, and that in this name of State the bond was expressed in which these “states” as forming the European population belonged together and were designated for international mutual relations. So there were States of the Province, States General and finally States of Europe, as they also appeared in this capacity at the Congress of Vienna. As for their own country itself, one spoke then of Commonwealth or Realm, but in relation to the other countries one spoke of the states of Europe. The German state scholars have also always been accustomed to discuss the States connection separately in the general theory of the state.

§4. In our Constitution. Our Constitution uses, not without a certain confusion, the following distinct expressions to designate the political unity of our nation: first, the Land; second, the People; third, the Realm; and 4th, the State. Naturally, there are certain distinct meanings and implications underlying this differentiated usage, without it being possible to say that the framers of our Constitution had formed a clear conception of these distinctions and meanings. The confusion between the Realm Treasury, the Land Treasury, and the State Treasury already shows this. Mention is made of the State in twelve articles, namely in Articles 24, 58, 59, 67, 94, 103, 113, 114, 118, 172, 176, and 181, and here most often in the expression: “interests of the State.” The People is only spoken of three times, namely in Articles 25, 52, and 78. The Land is mentioned eight times, namely in Articles 24, 25, 28, 30, 96, 171, 181, and 186. But for the rest, the constant expression for the unity, the previously unused term: Realm, which one encounters in Articles 1, 2, 3, 4, 43, 52, 59, 61, 63, 75, 80, 81, 90, 122, 123, 126, 129, 141, 149, 164, 166, 168, 177, 180, 184, 186, 187, 189, and 192—more than thirty times. It stands out so clearly which leading idea the framers wanted to place in the foreground, not that of the Land, not that of the People, and equally not that of the State, but very decidedly that of the Realm, aiming at the regnum, i.e., the kingdom. In connection with this, it draws attention that the official government gazette received the name not of Realm Gazette, but of State Gazette, and that one speaks in the same way of State Service. Although there is undoubtedly an attempt underlying the differentiated use of the four terms in the Constitution to find a modified expression each time for the fourfold sense in which the national unity can be conceived, yet it cannot be said that this has been consistently maintained in our Constitution. For while Article 176 states: “The obligations of the State towards its creditors are guaranteed,” this can be understood. The use of the expression “interests of the State” also commends itself sufficiently. But from the rest it is clear that in the differentiated use of the four terms mentioned in 1814–1848, one was anything but settled.

Chapter III THE ESSENCE OF THE STATE
§1. Uncertainty in meaning. Not without reason, Professor Dr. Rehm of Strasbourg remarks in his Allgemeine Staatslehre (p. 17) that it is extremely difficult to bring the fundamental concept of what we understand by a State to full clarity. He even goes so far as to declare: “A unity of insight into the question of what a State is will probably never be achieved.” What we remarked on pp. 88 ff. about the name “State” confirms this feeling. That France is a State, no one doubts, but in France lies Monaco. Is now Monaco also similar to France? It has a constitutional monarchy, a princely House, ministers, a State Council, a kind of parliament, and boasts 29 consulates of foreign powers; only it still has less than 2,000 inhabitants and is only 1¼ square kilometers in size. Can such a miniature phenomenon now claim the name of State? The Principality of Liechtenstein is somewhat larger and has nearly 10,000 inhabitants, yet it is still much smaller than our Hilversum. If one now places such small countries alongside Russia and England, do they fall into the same category? From a legal point of view, certainly, but does this not precisely show that the legal conception here does not decide? This comes out even stronger when one places countries like Bavaria, Saxony, Württemberg, even Prussia, alongside Monaco, which gave up part of their independence in the formation of the German Empire. Was Egypt a state? Legally, no, for the Sultan of Turkey was the nominal ruler, and in fact the King of England ruled. The colonial status of British India deprives the country of British India, which has almost as large a population as all of Europe, of the quality of a state. In connection with this, Professor Redslob in his work Abhängige Länder (Leipzig 1914) only recently dealt with the peculiar position in which an ever wider group of countries has come, through the fact that they have lost their statehood.
