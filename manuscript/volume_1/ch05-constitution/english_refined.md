Chapter VI THE LAND
Constantin Franck has, through his work published in 1870, Die Naturlehre des Staates als Grundlage aller Staatswissenschaften, on which we have already commented, registered objections to the then increasingly common habit of weaving the entire science of the state out of mere concepts. One-sided exaggeration may have been attached to his work, but the core of his objection was correct. The state is not a mere concept, but a living organism, and the science of the state must therefore be based on the study of the living organism, not on the construction of concepts. This applies not only to the state, but also to the people. The people is not a mere concept, but a living organism, and the science of the people must therefore be based on the study of the living organism, not on the construction of concepts. This is what we want to do in this chapter. We want to study the people as a living organism, and in doing so we will come across the land as the physical basis of the people’s existence.
THE NAME OF OUR NETHERLANDS. As we have already remarked, this attempt has not remained foreign to us, but here he has actually attacked an ever-widening evil, against which he protested, not only insufficiently with history, but above all with reality. In this respect, he can even be considered a kindred spirit of Buckle, in his History of the Civilization of England (Leipzig 1865). Since then, political aviation, if we may so call the theorizing in concepts, has appeared in a more moderate form, and especially Bluntschli in his Allgemeine Staatslehre (Stuttgart, 6th ed. 1886), pp. 255–299, has better done justice to what he calls “the basis of the state in its external nature,” both for the soil, the climate, and the fertility, as well as for the division of the land and the relationship of state and private property. Ahrens had already drawn attention to the neglect so far in his Organische Staatslehre (1850), pp. 201 ff., but it was especially Bluntschli who has further introduced the reckoning with the real data. In Stahl’s Staatslehre, Berlin 1910, p. 99, there is also a short chapter of five pages on Volk and Land, but in 1856, when Stahl wrote his Staatslehre, this real element in the theory of the state was still too much of a side issue. For us Dutchmen in particular, this reckoning with the land we inhabit has a special attraction. When you think about it, we are the only people who are not only named after our own language, but also in other languages, after our country. For us, in our name, it is not the people, but the land that seems to be the main thing. We are called Nederlanders, that is, inhabitants of the low countries, a name in which no ethnic designation is included. Everywhere else the land is named after the people, as in England, Scotland, Germany, etc., but in our name no ethnic name appears, and without any further indication, one names us after the low country in which we have settled. And this is also the reason why the French call our country Pays-Bas, the English the Netherlands, the Germans Niederlande, the Italians Paesi Bassi, etc. For the rest, the use of the word land in the name of a people is an exception. In North and South Europe, in America, in Asia and Africa, the word land is hardly included in any people’s or state’s name. THE LAND. The word “land” does not at all occur in the name of the people. And in Asia, Christianity speaks of the Holy Land, but the word “land” occurs neither in Hebrew nor in the derived name. In common usage, the word “land” to designate a people is found exclusively in the middle of Western Europe. In Northern Europe, one speaks of Denmark, Norway, and Sweden; in the South, of Italy, Spain, and Portugal, etc. France also falls outside, but the central group, to which we also belong, points to Germany, the Netherlands, England, Scotland, and Ireland. We also sometimes speak of Russia, but it is not called that in Russian, French, or English. Upon closer inspection, one would almost have to exclude England, Scotland, and Ireland, since these states as a whole are called Great Britain, a name in which the word “land” occurs just as little, but in Angleterre the word “land” has nevertheless been adopted even in the French translation. One could also point to Greece, but it does not call itself that, nor is it called that in French or English. Only in German and Dutch does this realm have the addition of “land” in its name. And there is still talk of Greenland and Tierra del Fuego, but for entirely different reasons. For there, in Greenland as well as in Tierra del Fuego, one found a land but no people. Hence the word “land” was used there without a people’s name, merely to point to the land as land. Thus it stands firm that the addition of “land” in the name of a people or state has remained a high exception, and that this high exception has produced this peculiarity among us, that in our generally recognized name the people is entirely silent and only the land is spoken of. This forms a sharp contrast especially with Belgium, which is so closely related to us in its western part. The Belgians descend in the eastern part from the Celts, and the provinces in Belgium show no trace of the addition “land” in their names. Flanders, Brabant, Hainaut, or whatever one wants, it does not give the word “land.” With us, on the other hand, the word “land” even appears in the foreground, as it occurs in the name of no less than five provinces, namely South Holland, North Holland, Friesland, Zeeland, and Gelderland, a phenomenon that does not occur in any other country in the world. It is therefore well established that we are dealing not only with the ethnological realities of the people, but also with the realities of the land, especially in the Netherlands. This is readily explained by the fact that our country presents a quite peculiar form, and to a certain extent can be said to have been shaped by the people who inhabited it. Our polder system was demanded by the nature of the land, and it is the people who, through reclamation, have made from the nature of the land what was to be made of it. With any people, it is therefore on our way, not only in ethnological but also in political consideration, to take into account this prominence of the land as land.

THE FATHERLAND. Yet this is not to be understood as if the significance of the holy land as such would be of subordinate importance to other peoples. One need only call the word “fatherland” a composition of “land” to receive a completely opposite impression. Even in the Marseillaise, the mightiest battle song of the French Revolution, which actually aimed at the international abolition of all national life, the “Allons, enfants de la patrie” has been the expression of the sweeping inspiration that made the stream of the people surge high. For us, too, there lies in the name of the people, nor in what reminds us of our descent, the tone, the sound that grips us whenever the “fatherland” is spoken of. The “Wien Neêrlands bloed,” which called to the people, has never been able to equal the tone of “the faithful to the fatherland” in the Wilhelmus. “Hollander” speaks to only a part of our people. “Netherlands” leaves us cold. The fatherland, on the other hand, is a sound that penetrates to the heart. The reason for this is easy to understand. What the nest is for the nobleman, what the hollow is for the lion, that is the home and the fatherland for our people. The population is physically so insignificant compared to the land. As you rush half-darkly through the country by train, you see only here and there a few people at work or on a walk. Broad, on the other hand, the land stretches out in all directions with its villages and meadows, its rivers and canals before your eyes. Nature has...

That land has boundaries. On the other side of the border, a foreign power reigns, but within those boundaries, our people are lord and master. That land is ours. It belongs to us collectively. It was the land of our fathers in previous centuries, now it is ours, and soon it will belong to our children. We have a stake in it, we live from it. It is the place assigned to us under this sun to live. And that is why our heart clings to that fatherland. Not the heart of the consistent social democrat, who obliterates the international and national character too much, but the heart of one who has a historical sense, to understand what this land, what this soil, what these seas have been for our fathers, and therefore must still be for us as their posterity. At the same time, in that sonorous sound of the fatherland, the contrast between the enduring and the passing is expressed. Tollens sang, “The place where our cradle once stood, there too our grave will stand.” Here rings out that childish superficiality that characterized the period then, as if it didn’t matter to us where our cradle was rocked. Certainly, we also speak of “native land” and “native soil,” but then not locally, but in connection with what that local place means to us. We are transient; and long before a century has passed, hardly anyone from our present generation will still be alive. A people is like a stream that comes down clear in the individual drops of water from above, and flows through the riverbed, to soon throw itself into the ocean. But in contrast to this constantly changing, exchanging and disappearing in the persons who form the people in each period, the land, the fatherland, and the fatherland’s soil now show that fixed and self-identical, which is inhabited from generation to generation, on which it labors, and by which it is nourished. The image of the enduring and abiding inheritance that all successive generations have borne, made their life possible, and soon, when we too will have gone to rest, will await a new generation to be inhabited by it. Thus the land shows the unity of the people in the succession of generations. That is why it is the land of our fathers, which lifts us out of our personal, individualistic existence into that communal life that connects us with our entire people in all regions, but also with our entire people back into the gray past.

The testimony of all peoples, who with wonderful talent have defended and protected their land as created and arranged, and in glorious enthusiasm as the soil of the people against every enemy, is that name of fatherland, as indicating the land bequeathed to us by our fathers as an inheritance, of such a gripping significance. We feel so clearly how the land itself, as every connoisseur testifies, contributed to our formation as a people. We see it before our eyes how, had we settled in any other land, we would never have become what we now are. Not only have our fathers raised their descendants, but the land itself, in which we have lived as a people, contains a peculiarity that has influenced the formation of our national character. And it is for this reason that in this precious nature of our fatherland two things speak to us. On the one hand, that it is the land of our fathers, whose lofty spirit passed into us, and on the other hand that it is the land of our fathers, which also powerfully helped to shape our peculiarity. We felt all the more urged to emphasize this, as far as our land is concerned, with a certain special emphasis, because no people and no land lends itself as little as ours to being reformed according to a state model woven from concepts, something that makes so palpably clear the defect of so many state scholars among us, who in the construction of his state doctrine, constitutional law and political economy have so nearly not reckoned with the condition of our land as land.
§3. The Zone. In no case, however, should it be inferred from this that only for our people the land we inhabit has such significant meaning. Rather, it cannot be emphasized enough that in general, for all peoples, the nature and condition of the land they inhabit has exerted influence on the formation of the national character and on the historical course of national life. It is therefore necessary to go into the general significance of the territorial, something in which one must then distinctly consider the zone in which the land lies; the condition of its soil, both in terms of formation and wealth;

So the land itself, what is under the land, what is above the land, and what surrounds the land, all demand our attention. If we first consider the zone, it requires no further explanation that the influence of nature is distinctly felt here. There are lands situated in the torrid zone, there are lands situated in the Arctic sphere, and there are lands situated in the temperate zone. Naturally, transitional states occur, and these three zones cannot be demarcated with fixed gradations, but in general this distinction of zones asserts itself with predominant decisiveness. Java, the Netherlands, and Greenland scarcely offer any unity of life conditions. In Greenland, human life has almost disappeared, at least shrunken. In Java, it is luxuriant and overwhelming, but without backbone. And in contrast, in the Netherlands itself, all the conditions for a rich and vigorous development are given. Geographically and historically, it is firmly established that the temperate zone has always offered the richest promise for human culture in general. Also, the Church of Christ has not been able to take root except in a very limited way in the hot zones, and has not thrived except miserably in the cold North, while conversely, in the temperate zone, the Church of Christ has always flourished best. To a certain extent, it can therefore be said that the zone governs the life of peoples. Immediately it follows from the difference in temperature degrees in the three distinct zones that the inhabitants of the cold North are inclined to enjoy their life mainly within the home, and scarcely move about in the open air, while conversely in the too warm South the idea of the dwelling is poorly developed, and life is mostly spent under the shade of trees in the open air. Only in the temperate zone is the daily life fairly evenly distributed between the house and the surrounding grounds. The long nights in the North and the short nights in the South also lead to a similar distinction, while in this respect too the temperate zone, if one counts the length and shortness of the days over an entire year, is the best endowed. The division of the seasons leads to a similar result. Only in the temperate zone does this division govern life with proportionate alternation. In the

The zone. 173 The North has the milder season in short supply, to counteract the capricious cold of the icy season, and in the South the cooler season scarcely brings relief. In this respect, too, the middle zone is therefore the most fortunate state of affairs. Here the winter lasts long enough to give the soil rest, to strengthen the domestic life, and to bring the human being to full power, both physically and psychologically, while at the same time the summer is of sufficient significance to introduce a pause in the bustle and to move to much sojourn in the warmer atmosphere, with spring and autumn then forming the two transitions that in the best-situated lands of the middle zone almost take on an ideal character. This difference in zone has also had a clearly noticeable influence on the character of the people. To some extent, the races even diverge according to zone differences. The dark skin tone is found as a rule in the South, and hardly at all in the Northern zone, while in the middle zone the tint does not show white to the South, but the fair complexion remains the basic tone. Also, the ratio between the predominance of muscle life, nerve life, or blood life regulates itself more or less according to the zone in which a people lives. Hence, human nature presents itself in the different zonal regions in a strikingly distinct manner. In the not too cold, but also not too warm middle region, human nature is characterized by a certain sobriety, self-control, and a strongly emerging willpower; in the hot regions, on the other hand, laxity soon sets in or one has to deal with an overstimulated passion that causes one to flare up one moment and sink into helplessness the next. The influence of this on public law and even more on civil law and criminal law requires no further elaboration. The entire state order and legal order is thus governed to no small extent by the nature of the people. And this all the more so as a people by no means always manages to settle in the region that suits its nature best. From Asia to Europe and from Europe’s eastern border deep into the West, there has been a shifting of peoples for century after century, leading to the newer, more powerful people either subjugating or overwhelming the earlier settlers in a certain region, so that they were first subjugated and then assimilated, or forced to move further westward.

Thus more than one people were driven out of a region where they felt at home and had settled out of affection, and now forced into a region less suited to their nature. Here too, the providential ordering of God is not lost sight of. Already on the Areopagus at Athens the Apostle Paul has emphatically declared: God has “made of one blood all nations of men for to dwell on all the face of the earth, and hath determined the times before appointed, and the bounds of their habitation” (Acts 17:26). Animals are not infrequently bound to a particular region or zone, but this does not hold for man, something connected with the common origin of all men from one and the same ancestral father, and with the all-sided variability of human nature. The fundamental principle must therefore always be held fast, as sung in one of the Evangelical hymns: “Thou, O Sovereign Ruler, hast appointed the place for every one’s abode, the circle wherein he shall work.” The influence of such a region on our life, and on the development of our own character, is therefore not accidental, but a revelation of God’s ordinance, and calls above all for appreciation and reverence. To the international tendency toward community with the whole of our race, the national character trait must never be sacrificed. In the variations speaks the richer life, and the one-sided craving for the international impoverishes.

§4. The Soil. Even stronger than in the zone does the influence of the land on the formation of the people come out, if one descends from the atmosphere to the soil itself. Here a distinction must be made between the dimensions of the land, the subsoil of the land, the nature of the land, and the condition of the land. A land can be too small and too large for the people; it can be rich or poor in mines; it can be fertile or thorny and stony; it can be flat or have elevations. Four variations that are of the highest significance for the people inhabiting the land. A population of well over six million souls, as we now have here, in a territory of barely 34,000 square kilometers, presents a very different ratio than the ten times larger area of Norway for not even 2 1/2 million who inhabit that land. The difference is even stronger between Belgium and Russia. Belgium has 7 1/2 million inhabitants on only 29,000 square kilometers, while in Russia a territory extends of 227 million square kilometers with a population of 170 million souls. In Belgium and here one can hardly find room, while conversely in Russia entire regions cry out for inhabitants. This difference naturally exerts influence on the entire social life, and no less on the form of government. In a small country the government can oversee and control the whole life down to the smallest details, while in large empires the power of police and administration is constantly deficient. §5. A people who own their own mines, from which they extract coal, iron, and petroleum, are not only rich in those mineral resources, but are also less dependent on other peoples. A population that for a large part has to spend the best hours of the day underground, and is thereby deprived of the healthy influence of daylight and the refreshing purification of the wind, is easily inclined to melancholy, lacks the cheerfulness, and is only too easily given to a brooding disposition that can strain the spirit to the utmost and for that very reason so easily slips into rebellious movements. A strike has an attraction in the mining country that is unknown in a land like ours. And so it appears again here how, no less than the size or smallness of a country, the treasure that lies hidden under the ground also impresses its stamp on the mood and character of the people, and in connection with this also calls the government to a peculiar fulfillment of its governmental task.

FERTILITY.177 The eye is directed to the people who inhabit it, according to the greater or lesser fertility of the soil. Even the simplest knows this from the parable of the Sower. There are stony places where there is no depth of earth, there are overgrown regions where the thorns choke the crop, and there is “good soil”; but even in that good soil there is still a difference between thirtyfold, sixtyfold and a hundredfold. Yet these are still only very small differences, since our country as a whole can be counted among the fertile lands. Not yet equal to Egypt and the land of the Tigris and Euphrates, or as the land of Canaan was once fertile, but yet fertile in the sense that the harvest from pasture and field, from forest and orchard, and to some extent even from the hunting grounds and inland waters, divided over 34,000 square kilometers and over six million inhabitants, gives us the right to speak of a rich country. It is quite different, on the other hand, in regions where the rocky soil almost always disappoints every desire for a harvest; where the barrenness of the soil turns the scarcity of vegetation into a rule; or also where the sandy nature of the soil becomes so predominant that there is hardly any question of choosing fixed places of residence. That in regions of Arabia, Persia, Asiatic Russia, and on islands such as Borneo, and even partly on New Guinea, the population remains so sparse and does not progress, is mainly to be explained by the fact that the soil of the land cannot produce enough in the harvest. Not infrequently one has also seen how guano fertilization, and elsewhere even chemical-artificial fertilization, has completely revived a previously withered land. But whatever remedy is applied here, the significance that the fertility or barrenness of its soil has for a people remains to assert itself.

§7. Flatland and mountain land. A third difference between lands and lands comes to light in the flat or mountainous nature of the region in which the land lies. The mountain dweller is more exposed to the adversity of the weather than the dweller of the plain. He lives in a much smaller circle. The movement of life does not penetrate to him as much. By nature he is generally stronger, because he is more a child of nature, but his general development tends to be on a lower level. The luxuriance of life is much more enjoyed in the valley than in the highlands. From boyhood on, the mountain dweller handles the gun, and hunting gives something heroic to his character. Often lacking a doctor or pharmacist, he tends to take better care of his health himself. When it comes to fighting, he usually also wins against the dweller of the plain. And even if he does not live in a very high mountain region, so that the plateaus of the mountains offer him a kind of elevated plain, by which the community becomes greater and the social togetherness gains in enjoyment, yet as a rule the population living at an altitude of 500 to 1000 meters is more muscular and healthier than the people living in the valleys and on the plains. The course of history also shows that it was always the mountain people who ultimately invaded, robbed and subjugated the prosperous population of the plain. What the Armenians still have to suffer from the Kurds today was the rule of old. Even if it is no longer to the same extent, yet the same distinction must also be made for those regions that stretch out as high and low lands over a wide expanse. We in our extremely flat country understand this; and where the traveling through Germany, Switzerland and Tyrol is very common among us, many of us already understand from our own experience what a completely different picture the life of a people presents, depending on whether it is overlooking in all directions, like our fatherland here, or is enclosed on all sides by high and low mountains as if by walls. Depending on whether the land is flat or mountainous, the people are different, live differently, and develop differently, and it is especially at this point of difference that it becomes so clear how the way of governing and ruling the land is also largely determined by this flat or undulating nature of the land. §8. In a region where nature repeatedly seizes him with its power of beauty and the thrill of change, he easily loses his self-confidence, feels the constantly gripping power of nature, trembles and shudders as that power awakens and comes storming in, and turns himself to a life of constant disturbances, a life of fear and terror. The impact of this is most noticeable in the religious life of the valley. In the far North, there was an ancient paganism in which powerful human persons, incorporated into the life of the gods, played the leading roles, and the main content was sick tableaux and the doctrine of the gods. In the East, on the other hand, especially in tropical lands, there was an anxious animism, a spirit-weakening mysticism, and a complete domination of the life by questions about what might come upon the person torn from his moorings by an unseen world. Here too, the extremes are at work. The Means of Communication. Of a completely different nature is the influence that the land in which a people dwells exerts on that people through the means of communication that provide access to and passage through the land and intersect the land. Regarding the land routes, we can be brief on this point. §11. The Land by the Sea.
And yet even stronger than the blessing that the rivers and streams bring to the people is the heightened vitality that enters the people when they may dwell by the sea. For lands that are surrounded on all sides, as is our world within, by the sea, the sea becomes an even more dominant factor than the rivers. PROVINCIAL DIFFERENCES.199 Finally, the significance that “the land” has for the people once again confirms the objection we raised from the outset against the notion that the state has a being of its own. Therefore, we insist that in the state we see little more than the mechanical form that a people has assumed within certain boundaries on an identifiable piece of land. For this form is but a consequence, not the cause of the existing condition in which the people presents itself. If the nature of a people is nomadic, like the Gypsies or the much larger nomadic hordes, then no state can consolidate itself. On the other hand, as we have already remarked, a similar phenomenon occurs with the Jews. The Arabs, too, were already mentioned in this connection. To want to see a sovereign state in tiny enclaves like Monaco or Liechtenstein is an irony that makes a laughingstock of itself. The state form only becomes necessary and possible where the fixed settlement of a certain significant number of people, for whatever reason, proves to be necessary. Always, the group of people with whom one is dealing must be bound together internally in order to be able to act as a people. Always, this group must have ground under its feet in order to rise, stand, and work. And the state bond is only an additional means of connection that comes afterward. Organically and of necessity arising as an integral part of the life of the people—that is what the state bond would be, if all groups of people spontaneously took action and all the land on the globe adapted to them. But since the condition of the globe in its various parts is quite divergent, no people can make or occupy a land as it wishes, one people imposes the other people on regions where it then takes refuge out of necessity, and once a migration has taken place, the people imprints its peculiar stamp on the land and the land on the people—now the essence cannot be sought in the idea of the state, now a state possesses the organic nature of its appearance not from itself, but owes it to the people, and can only be found in the people as such, to which the state form attaches itself. This state form can then almost infinitely change, and yet the people as people remains the same. Even the state bond can disappear entirely without the people as such undergoing any change. Austria is an undoubtedly powerful appearance both as a Great Power and as a state. Yet the anxious fear constantly arises in the Austrian monarchy that the death of the Emperor, who held it all together through his personal excellence, could suddenly cause the entire empire to fall apart, especially after the atrocity in Sarajevo. Now, suppose it came to this, then the Czechs in Bohemia, the Germans in the West, and the Poles in the North would naturally continue their existence as a people undisturbed and would continue to inhabit the same piece of the globe, but the state bond that held them together would have fallen away. Of the three constituent elements that are indispensable to the concept of the state, the people is given by itself, the land is found by the people, and only the Authority is the third element that comes afterward. Eliminate the people, and nothing remains but uninhabited land, and the Authority disappears of itself. Imagine that in an elementary upheaval a part of our world sank away, so that neither people nor authority would be left. But imagine that only the Authority disappeared for a moment, then land and people would remain, and the state could immediately be restored by the appointment of a new Authority. People, land, and Authority never stand on an equal footing. People and land are always the first present and fixed elements, and it is from these two elements that a state emerges through the intervention of an Authority. The mistake in Napoleon’s (III) pushing through the idea of nationality was therefore not that he demanded one people for the existence of the entire state, but that he tried to forcibly separate what had wrongly been mixed.
