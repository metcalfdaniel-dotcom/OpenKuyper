### Chapter I
### §1. Further Elucidation Necessary.

The elucidation of the Program of Principles of the Antirevolutionary Party, which appeared in March 1879 under the title: “OUR PROGRAM,” published in Amsterdam by J.H. Kruyt, was perhaps not altogether unsuitable at that time for meeting a need that had arisen spontaneously, though it was then still very limited. This need had arisen from the demise, on May 19, 1876, of the Founder of our party, Master Guillaume Groen van Prinsterer. As long as Groen van Prinsterer remained with us, he constituted, as the very incarnation of the Antirevolutionary Party's political existence and intention, the natural and life-giving center for our entire group. But when he was taken from us, that group, lacking a cohesive bond, would have all too readily disintegrated; and thus, to preserve its unity, Groen’s seminal thoughts necessarily had to be transmuted into a programmatic formula and thereby expounded for a wider circle. For me, a non-Jurist, the undertaking of serving as a guide to our Calvinistic people in this matter was—I readily confess—no small venture. Nevertheless, perceiving that no more capable hand was extended at that juncture, and having been enabled, after a protracted illness, to resume the editorship of *De Standaard* in 1878, I felt myself called to this task, however arduous. ### FURTHER ELUCIDATION NECESSARY.

I must not shrink from this task. The now fifth edition of this work, numbering nearly five hundred pages, in which I presented this so urgently required elucidation, suggests that, however deficiently, it has nonetheless rendered some salutary service toward the intended goal; and this, not merely within the fortress, but also beyond its walls. At least, even so eminent a statesman-theologian as Professor Buys evinced appreciation for my labor, insofar as it clarified our Antirevolutionary intention. Yet it was clear to me from the outset that, given a conceivable further development of our party's significance within Dutch political life, a more complete and systematic exposition of what the Antirevolutionary Party professed and aimed for in this land could not be foregone. Should we once emerge from our isolation into alliance, and thereby advance more prominently into the foreground; should we, sooner or later, temporarily shift from the sphere of opposition into the realm of governance; and should the moment approach when we might succeed in partially bending the entire trajectory of the Netherlands’ political course: then the elucidation provided in 1878 would, of course, no longer suffice. A deeper penetration into that which had hitherto been merely touched upon at the surface would then brook no further delay. Only an absolute dearth of time has thus far prevented me from discharging this subsequent task. Now, in proceeding to this task, an appeal to the indulgent judgment of Jurists would savor of presumption, as if competition with that which is and remains their proper task were even remotely my intention. A Jurist I am not, neither by disposition nor by study; and the double doctorate in Political Science and in Law, which was conferred upon me *honoris causa*—the first from Princeton and the second from Louvain—has not metamorphosed me into such. I remain a practical statesman of theological provenance. Any other designation would be misplaced upon the study which I now offer solely to kindred spirits, just as it was formerly misplaced upon Our Program. Yet it by no means follows from this that what I offer is therefore entirely worthless, and would have been better left unwritten. ### §2. The Concept of Law.

Our starting point must be the establishment of Law. Not as if, since Rudolph Gneist advanced the concept of the “Rechtstaat,” the life of the state could be confined within the legal sphere; but even though critique of this conception remains a duty, one who, for his part, desires to give direction to the political life of his own people cannot escape the demand to declare himself concerning the meaning and significance of Law. The etymology of the word offers no definitive elucidation here. What Ahrens cites in his *Droit naturel* I, p. 107, from the Digests No. 2: “*Juri operam daturum nosse oportet, unde nomen Juris descendit*,” that is: For sound legal knowledge it is necessary to understand the origin of the word: Law, holds no validity. Even Rudolph von Jhering’s attempt in his *Zweck des Rechts*, II, pp. 12–74, to derive the concept of “morality” from the word, however acute, cannot be regarded as conclusive. And this applies even less to the word *Recht*, inasmuch as the Germanic stem *recht*, the Greek *dikē*, the Latin *jus*, the Scandinavian *pravo*, the Gothic *to//zsas*, and the Hebrew *mishpat* diverge too widely, not only in form but also in meaning, to permit a conclusion regarding a unity of thought in the formation or selection of the word for Law. Ahrens’ well-known conception, as if our word for *Recht* were derived from the fact that a straight line is the shortest distance between two points, has found favor with scarcely anyone.

### THE CONCEPT OF LAW.

Linguistically, “*Recht*” is rather to be understood from its antithesis to *scheef* (crooked) and *krom* (bent). Dr. K. F. L. Weigand, in his *Deutsches Lexicon* II, p. 447, brings us much nearer to the truth by pointing out that our *Recht* is connected with the Latin *Rectus*, from *regere*, and that it thus contains the idea of governing, i.e., ordering, regulating, dominating; an interpretation confirmed by the word “right hand,” as an antithesis to that which is “left,” a concept which likewise refers to dominion, e.g., in the saying that Christ is seated “at the right hand of His Father.” The derivation of the word “Judge” likewise points to a similar signification. Law is the emanation of a power that establishes or restores a certain order, and its antithesis lies in that which is crooked, that which is left, that which is confused, that which is chaotic. Yet the concept of Law did not arise as an antithesis. It is in its origin entirely autonomous, and by virtue of this autonomous origin, it created its own antithesis. The concept of Law is situated similarly to the equally autonomous concepts of the Good, the Beautiful, and the True. The Good, as a concept, denotes the character of the moral sphere; the Beautiful, the character of the aesthetic sphere; and the True, that of conformity with the real; and thus, in the concept of Law, is expressed the existence of that which *ought to be*, as opposed to that which *ought not to be*. Law, like the Good, the Beautiful, and the True, is a distinct category of being. Just as the category of the Good itself creates the antithesis of the non-good, that is, of Evil; just as the category of the Beautiful calls forth the antithesis of the Ugly into life; and just as the category of the True is inconceivable without the antithesis of Falsehood; so also does the category of that which is Law, of its own accord, posit Injustice as the negation to be annihilated. The Good aims at intention, the Beautiful at form, the True at the identity between appearance and essence; Law, on the other hand, derives its significance from the relations, both between God and the creature, and among creatures themselves. If such relations do not exist, then there is nothing but chaos; but if they do exist, then the question naturally arises whether they are the play of chance, or whether they have been instituted and ordered by a power standing above all creatures. And if the latter, then the problem arises whether the thought that ordered these relations, supported by the will that established them, merely leaves them at that establishment, or rather counteracts every disturbance thereof, whether by punishment or by restoration. Since it is now empirically established that such fixed relations exist; that these manifest relations do not originate from the creature; that they rather owe their genesis and subsistence to a will transcending the creature; and that this will possesses and exercises the power to maintain that which flowed from it: thus, Law cannot otherwise be understood than as an order instituted by God over us, which not only regulates our relation to Him and the mutual relations among creatures, but also maintains them, where they have been violated, through punishment and restoration.

### §3. Legal Sensation.

All this, however, would transpire beyond our consciousness, were there not given within our mentality the spiritual apprehension of the antithesis between Law and Injustice. Just as we have received a sense for taste, for smell, for sight, for hearing, etc., so also is there within our mentality a sensation, an apprehension, that reacts to the distinction between Truth and Falsehood, to that between Beauty and Ugliness, and to that which, in a moral sense, affects us beneficently or repulsively. And in an entirely analogous sense, there is now, apart from the question of what is objectively right or wrong, a spiritual faculty within our mentality that reacts to the Category of Law or Injustice. This is our inborn or implanted apprehension of Law or Injustice, which, of all the four mentioned, manifests itself most strongly in its reaction to our perceptions. To that which is good or bad, the apprehension of many reacts sometimes very weakly; to that which is truth or falsehood, already much more strongly; but strongest of all is the reaction when one perceives that injustice is taking place. This cannot be tolerated. Against this, almost everyone rises spontaneously, and often with tempestuous fervor. Thus it appears that, entirely apart from any specific content of Law, we are susceptible to a very strong sensation of law or injustice. This sensation, as such, is an inborn characteristic of our human nature. It is peculiar to man, irrespective of all content, and thus also of the question whether we are in the right. One who is entirely mistaken in his conception of his right, and regards as injustice that which rightfully befalls him, often reacts to this false sensation of injustice just as strongly, sometimes even more strongly, than one who is in the right. If it is now thus established that there is within us an apprehension of Law or Injustice, then the question arises as to how we come to know the content of Law.
