§6. Actually advanced. Even more must be said. Not only is the fact undeniable that the victory of the Law and the thirst for Peace have both gained enormously in importance, but it must no less be recognized that in the movement of the Rule of Law as well as in Pacifism, a drive of life found expression that has a claim on our cordial assent. Machiavelli’s system may have long borne the brand of political infamy, and on closer inspection it may appear that the so-called “reason of state” is nothing but the naked selfishness of the ruling power, yet the fact remains that the movement of the Rule of Law and Pacifism has brought to light a moral force that deserves our respect. The more the sense of privilege and the practice of violence recede, the more the sense of law and the thirst for peace come to the fore. And this is a gain for humanity that cannot be valued highly enough.

The essence of the state has proven to be more bearable than it seemed for a long time; yet this does not negate the fact that: Salus reipublicae suprema lex esto [The welfare of the state is the supreme law] has found entry for far too long in the higher circles of the state. I have already explained the part of truth that lies in this. But it must also be acknowledged that the whimsical truth of “necessity knows no law” cannot be erased, yet it is no less certain that European diplomacy has acted century after century as if no demand of truth and justice applied to it. What has only just been seen, when Austria-Hungary suddenly annexed and incorporated the regions of Bosnia and Herzegovina, which the Treaty of Berlin had only entrusted to the Emperor in Vienna as an administration, and Germany as the Habsburg’s ally covered this act with its shield, and the other great powers ended up recognizing this incorporation without protest—this took place in accordance with Machiavellian morality. The same can be said of Finland’s incorporation into Russia, where this was likewise severed by treaty. These two transgressions against public morality took place in the 20th century, and anyone who now consults the history of past centuries to give an architectural account of the construction of the great states, can come to no other result than the sad recognition that power went before right almost as a rule. If it was so in international relations, it could not be otherwise, or in civil life there was a constant recurrence of similar violations of private law in knowledge and jurisprudence, and precisely because of this it could not be otherwise, or in private life the cunning considered it his right to make the innocent pay the reckoning. Insofar as now the belief in the rule of law has at least tempered this evil and wants to temper it increasingly, the idea is undoubtedly to be welcomed. To let the sense of justice work more intensively on consciousness and practice is a step forward on the long road that must lead to the triumph of the truly human in our society over the all too bestial. The verdict of the judge wins in humanity over the clenched fist. In so far, we also fully understand that it was not the least Stahl who has cheered the rule of law, and no anti-revolutionary wishes to be inferior to anyone in appreciating this rich thought.

The same can be said of Pacifism. Whoever professes Christ shudders at the misery that war has brought upon land and sea over the course of centuries, and even now again. Aversion to war is in the Christian’s blood. The horrors of war fill us with consternation. As far as it is now historically established that war has repeatedly been sought as holy, not to resolve mighty international problems, whether in blood or otherwise, but to satisfy personal ambition, vent vindictiveness, and gratify unjust desires—to make what was great even mightier by the suppression of the small—our curse turns against this. The slogan: “Peace through Law” is not the highest for us, nor is it entirely true, but it does express a higher standpoint for us than the immediate grasping for the sword. In the international sphere too, the human must ever more rise above our bestial power, and how does this differ from the practices of the wild beast of yore, that, if only it had the power, would cross the borders of a neighboring state, and, once arrived on that territory, plunder, pillage, and murder at will? A bestial passion that also completely cast off respect for women and made one think of an invasion of tigers and hyenas. This is no exaggeration. Such was the military frenzy. Woerden and Naarden still remember, not to speak now of much more heaven-defying scenes. Against this, human self-awareness has finally risen in revolt. The ambulance service of the Red Cross has raised the banner of a higher humanity against this raging of bestial passion. Walled cities may no longer be attacked. Unarmed country folk are no longer shot down. The idea of law has taken its stand even at the battery with the sharpest guns. And as for the matter of war, the seeking of war is everywhere condemned. What remains is a harsh necessity, to which one finally submits. The international relations have been improved in all kinds of ways by the traffic routes. What was formerly unknown became unloved, but now greater familiarity has led to better appreciation. There is a gain that we wholeheartedly applaud. §7. Overly Optimistic Expectations. What we could not and should not have conceded was the assertion that the Rule of Law would gradually free us from all injustice, and that Pacifism would liberate us from all violence, and that, with gradual progress, the long-awaited moment would soon arrive when the gates of Paradise would reopen for us. We could not and should not have shared in this expectation, because it was based on impure premises, against which we must set the only correct premises of God’s Revelation. It is not true that the misery in which humanity has struggled through all these centuries is to be explained solely by “not yet being perfect,” so that with steady improvement in perfection, this misery would be replaced by untroubled happiness. The Revelation of Holy Scripture protests against this, and experience has just recently set its painful seal on this testimony of Holy Scripture. If there is anything that stands firm against this, it is this: that higher culture has indeed refined sin, but there is no question of the sinful passions in the hearts of the people being exorcised. Whoever examines the state of society in higher and lower circles, as it now presents itself to us, in the light of the Ten Commandments, will certainly not be less disappointed than in earlier centuries.
