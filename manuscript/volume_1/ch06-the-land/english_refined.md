Chapter VII THE SUPREME AUTHORITY
§1. The Title. Now in the title of this chapter, we must speak of the Supreme Authority, even though for convenience’s sake we omit the addition: “high authorities” in the further discussion. In the Frisian Placquaet book A°. 1583, 4, 381b, we read: “That no one shall venture to send any letters... to the enemies... unless they first show such to their proper authority... and obtain consent thereto from the same their authority.” From this it appears that Authority was the translation of magistrate, and was applied both to the local and municipal, as well as to the national magistracy. Only in the last-mentioned sense, however, is there mention of Popular Authority. The lower authorities recognize an authority above them. Now since in our argument at this place only that power comes into consideration which exercises authority without itself being subject to a higher authority, the term Supreme Authority alone is the appropriate one. Our argument should therefore be understood in that indicated sense, even though the addition is omitted for brevity’s sake. §2. The Starting Point in the Family. Now, since, as we have already noted, the Reformed have always professed (see Art. 36 of the Confession A°. 1618) that “God has ordained the authorities, in order that the licentiousness of men may be restrained and all things may proceed among men with good order,” a goal that can only be attained if the world is governed by laws and polities, i.e., established administrations, the question naturally arises as to what condition would have historically presented itself, if no sin had entered and the entire human race, as an organism in its operation unimpeded, had followed the course willed by God. To answer this question, we must go back to the Family, as was also customary in earlier times; but since, under the pressure of revolutionary principles, which always put the individual first, this had fallen into disuse; yet now it has again come into vogue. Already Bluntschli in his Allgemeine Staatslehre, 6th ed. p. 15, expressed the view “that the State is only then called for when the circle of a mere Family is exceeded. A Family, a clan, like that of the Jewish patriarch Jacob, can become the nucleus around which a larger number of people gather, but only when this has happened, only when the individual Family... has expanded into a people, is a real state formation possible.” Dr. H. Ahrens already gave in 1850 in his Organische Staatslehre bl. 95 to Dahlmann: “The primal Family is the primal State, every Family, independently constituted, is a State”; of course, always with the proviso that this Family can never be anything other than “the first and lowest stage of state formation.” And Conrad Bornhak in his Allgemeine Staatslehre second ed. Berlin 1909 goes even further; he rejects (p. 18) in Aristotle that he lets the idea of the State precede that of the Family; and he states decisively: “The State is not a legal event, but a generational fact. From the outset, the State is based on the Family. Just as this gradually expands in several generations into a clan, so the connection of several clans of the same stock forms the original state organism, the clan-state. This has thus naturally grown out of the Family.” That this last even goes so far as to stamp the State as the normal form of association, is explained by the fact that Bornhak does not reckon with the breach in our human life through sin. Apart from this, however, he too goes back to the Family and does so almost entirely in the old manner. With J. F. Stahl, Staatslehre Berlin 1910, p. 45, it stands even more decisively: “The first State is the patriarchal, ordered Family. Here the relationships that later unfold into the healthiest existence - Family, Clan, State, Church - are as it were still compressed into the small.” Now, if we go back to the Family, we naturally go back to the Home. Only here it should also be noted that Stahl wrongly derives the concept of the State from the Creation itself. It was only sin that called the State into being.

MARRIAGE.203 And so we return to the unavoidable question: If the breach had not occurred, what bond would have normally and naturally formed among us humans, given that the Family was once established. For it was in the Family that God Himself laid the first bond as the starting point for all further bonds. Man and woman formed in this bond a physical unity. “These two,” said God, “shall be one.”

§3. Marriage. Three kinds of marriage have historically presented themselves here. The first marriage, in Adam and Eve, was a becoming one of two newly created persons, without any prior bond with any family whatsoever. The second marriage was a becoming one of brother and sister born from the same parental pair. And the third marriage bond, under the prohibition of incest, united two persons born of different parental pairs. Finally, there already lay in the becoming one of man and woman an element of binding together of the distinct parts, by which our human race would gradually spread out. Personality presses toward the individual, and splits and divides, but the Family, on the other hand, already forms through marriage itself a counterweight to this. Personality separates, the becoming one of man and woman performs the service of binding together. From this binding together of two families or lineages, then, a new social cell emerges in a new household, thanks to the drive that God Himself has placed in the human being through the division of our human nature into two sexes. All of this is not the product of human invention or human power display, or of human willful striving. Even one who does not believe in an Almighty God as the Cause of all creatures is nevertheless compelled to acknowledge that an external and superhuman power was not only active here, but was the sole agent at work, and that, even if one then speaks of Nature where we speak of God’s Almightiness, in this Creation of the human being into two sexes there lay a gigantic plan that would govern the entire history and call forth the first factor of binding together and interconnection in life. One could object that the human being has it in their power to make sexual bonds ineffective, but first of all history of all ages shows that the vast majority either did not intend this or were unable to accomplish it; and secondly, that same history brings us a devastating judgment over all such attempts. Even now again one sees before one’s eyes how all resistance to this divine will finds its inescapable punishment in demoralization and ruin. Humanity is not a mere agglomeration of atoms, but exists as an organic body, and one of the main bonds for the binding together of this body in its organic connection is the seeking of the sexes for each other. Such a bond would therefore lie in marriage even if one could think away the birth of one human being from another. The sexual difference that God has built into our nature always binds. But naturally, the binding power emanating from marriage becomes infinitely stronger through the birth of the child from father and mother. This, too, was not an absolute necessity. Given the fact that the first man had neither father nor mother, and that the woman he received also appeared without parents, the possibility stands firm that in the same way all other humans could have come into being, and that there would have been no talk whatsoever of any sexual act of the man, nor of any carrying of the child by the woman. How this would have impoverished our human life, hardly needs demonstration, but that it could have been so is undeniable. And now the fact is that it has been established otherwise, and that on the contrary the bond “that binds the heart, from mother to child” is interwoven with life, was neither our provision, nor our invention, nor even our desire or lust, but God’s sovereign decree, His will’s expression and His act. This also connects with the fact that we humans begin as helpless infants, something that was not the case with Adam and Eve, and would not have been the case with our entire humanity if it had come into being in the manner of Adam. So we now have three binding factors. The first was the sexual difference between man and woman. The second was that every new human being is born from a human pair that already exists. And now a third factor is added, namely that the human being does not come into the world as an adult, but as a helpless infant, and for this reason is once and for all dependent on the care of father and mother in its early life.

ORDER IN THE FAMILY. That the family is the fundamental unit of society, on which the well-being of the nation depends for 205 years, is undisputed. Now, that sin has disrupted this order—through divorce, deliberate avoidance of pregnancy, and neglect of the care and upbringing of children—does not detract from the high command that lies ordered for us in all of this. Despite all the corruption that has crept in and still thrives, the family as God’s creation remains the great link that holds our human race together; and this in such a way that the entire plan of these bonds, the constant production of what is required for the multitude to remain bound together, proceeds from God and is upheld by Him. Whatever countervailing forces there may be from polygamy, polyandry, matriarchy, eunuchism, and all unnatural and perverted sexual sins, and even now from Neo-Malthusianism, it remains established that the monogamous order, the possibility of family creation, the desire for family propagation, paternal, maternal, and filial love, offer us the highest and noblest form of community, and that all that emerges as beautiful and noble in this is from God, and all that brings corruption in this is from man. Apart from sin, family life would always have realized its highest ideal.

§4. Order in the Family. In the family as such, an order now arises of itself. With or without sin, there is a certain order in all that is composed. This is so even in chemical compounds, and neither in the animal nor in the plant world is organic connection conceivable without a rule, a law, an order to which existence is subject. Breach of this order can lead to destruction. So it is also in the human body, so it is also in the human spirit, and so it is also in all human community. This order did not first come into consideration when sin entered and disrupted it; this order is always there, for it belongs to the very essence, and this order can have no other origin than in the ordinance of Him who created the essence. Now since marriage and the procreation of offspring are implanted by God Himself in the nature of things, it follows that the order in family life also does not originate from us, but from God. §8. World Empire, Pacifism, and Parousia. From this standpoint, two different conceptions emerge of the process that could lead from this fragmentation to the recovery of unity. The one is revealed to us in Holy Scripture, and the second emerges in various forms from the world’s current dissatisfaction with its lot. Continually the idea of unity has sought to press forward and has manifested itself outwardly in the gigantic ancient empires of world conquerors, and in the mighty conception of the world empire. We need only mention Alexander the Great and Napoleon to conjure up the image of the founders of a world empire, and of such a world empire not only the Roman and Imperial dominions and the “Holy Roman Empire” give us the memory, as we have seen, but Great Britain also seems ever anew to want to present us with the image. With its territory of over thirty million square kilometers and its population of over four hundred million, Great Britain now already far surpasses what in ancient times, in the Middle Ages, and after the Middle Ages in the world power of Spain, was contemplated as a world empire; while alongside Great Britain, Russia too continually attains more and more gigantic proportions and an ever stronger population, and now already rules over a hundred and seventy million souls on a territory of about 23 million square kilometers. And alongside this impressive reality runs Cosmic Pacifism, which not only wants to ban war and guarantee eternal world peace, but in highly excited fantasy reaches much further, and now already on Peace Conferences, through the Court of Arbitration, and so much more, thinks to encompass the whole world and dreams of being able to call into being a world-encompassing world confederation or league of states, and has even already devised plans to abolish all fleets and armies and to leave only a small, central military force at the disposal of the world government, in order, if necessary, to compel a rebellious group or a quarrelsome people by force to submit. All evolutionary ideas, which are to be welcomed insofar as they aim at peace, but for the rest, insofar as they want to design a new state structure, are to be rejected as mere chimeras. The contrast is then also remarkable. In the world of fantasies, never anything other than grandiose creations of a world empire and a global federation, and on the other hand in reality, never anything other than ever more powerful fleets and ever more gigantic armies armed from kingdom to kingdom, as if to shame the ideals of Pacifism. Yet the cause of this painful miscalculation is obvious. The men of Evolution know no creation of God and no wandering of man from God. They therefore do not see and cannot see that a fracture in the organism of humanity has cut off all chance of normal and regular development. The idea of the Pacifists is in itself also ours. We too believe in a world empire, and we too believe in eternal peace, but never otherwise than as the fruit of the Cross. §13. By the Grace of God. Any hesitation on this point is absolutely impermissible. Only where an extraordinary, direct calling or revelation has taken place, as in Israel, would the phrase “By the Grace of God” signify that a supernatural divine disposition had invested a person or House with the highest authority. Insofar as, except in Israel, no single Prince or Royal House has ever had such a bestowal of the highest authority in an extraordinary manner, it follows that in the ordinary royal title the expression “By the Grace of God” can never be understood in such an exceptional sense. One could rather say of a father and mother in their household that they were placed at the head of their family “by the grace of God,” and then in a direct sense, for the bestowal of a child upon the parental couple presupposes a direct creative act of God. On the other hand, any commission of the highest authority to a Prince in person or to his House is always bound to a human action and can never be derived from a divine act that took place outside the ordinary order of things, since, outside of Israel, such a personal, direct divine appointment has never occurred anywhere. Yet it is equally inadmissible to place the commission of authority on par with any other providential disposition in the economic, scientific, or aesthetic realm. The opponents of the formula “By the Grace of God” were always ready with the remark that here there was not only no special appointment, but that even the providential action had here lost all special value, since it stood on the same line as every higher calling that fell to the lot of any single person, in whatever domain. Whoever possessed a large estate and had to manage it, had by that very fact the authority to assign persons to labor, to determine the rules to which the labor to be delivered had to correspond, and to deal with the fruits of labor as he pleased. Now, however, the entirely different question arises, namely, who possesses the right to act as the government. In the abstract, the necessity of a governmental authority by the grace of God has now been demonstrated, but who is the person, or who are the persons, to whom the right belongs to assert themselves as the organ or instrument of this divine authority. This second question now does not touch the world order instituted by God by virtue of His right of creation, but His providential appointment and administration. It is from among the children of men that persons come forward who either take the authority upon themselves personally, or personally for themselves and their heirs, or to whom it is entrusted. This may happen in good order, but it may also come about through all kinds of abuse of force and power, even through cunning deception. Even a generally recognized government can abuse its authority and act in direct conflict with the purpose for which all governmental authority has been instituted. Just as sin can ruin the family without thereby discrediting the marital order of the family itself, so a similar sin can ruin the life of peoples and states without thereby branding the governmental authority itself, in the spirit of the Anabaptists and Anarchists, as criminal. The sin that seeks to corrupt all human endeavor has also provided the most shameful examples in the domain of government, but nevertheless the governmental authority remains holy in character, and God’s providential appointment ultimately leads to a blessed solution. For just as there can be a degenerate mother who lets her child starve to death, the institution of motherhood is not thereby discredited.

§24. Need for Authority. It is only to a very limited extent that we can trace how this process has unfolded in all its particulars; but in general, it can be stated with a fair degree of certainty that need and violence were the two main factors through which the establishment of governmental authority succeeded in a wider circle. If the body of humanity had remained physically and psychologically healthy, then this need for an externally imposed bond of authority would not have been felt, because there would have been no question of a pulling apart of the parts. When a bone or arm is broken, the need for a plaster cast is felt, as long as the arms and legs do not interfere with their normal functioning; but as soon as something would hinder the free movement of the arms and legs, one would rather do without a foot than put up with it. Where the need for governmental authority comes into play, we must therefore think of two different needs: on the one hand, the need that arises from our nature, as God has ordained it, and on the other hand, the need that did not exist at the Creation and has only arisen through the dominion of sin. The need that arises from our human nature manifests itself as a longing for sociability and community life. To be sure, this longing can be reduced to a minimum and even turn into misanthropy, but this works against sin and goes against the nature given to us by God. The individual tries to assert itself against the masses. True personality only emerges where good harmony arises between the drive for independence of the individual and the drive for community in sociability. If this harmony is disrupted, a state of emergency can arise in two directions. Either the individual can underestimate the community, or the masses can prevent the independent right of the individual from emerging. Apart from this, however, all of history, and even now, provides conclusive proof that the need for community and for cohesion is not to be thought away from our nature. Solitary confinement has proved to be one of the harshest punishments. Even children seek other children, young people seek each other, everything seeks company. And where kinship, friendship, community of interests and unity of life have once established bonds, these bonds are generally seen to draw ever closer. Villagers feel a common interest, colleagues seek each other out. Everything groups itself—the solitary wanderer is almost repelled by everyone. From this it is self-evident that, when the common bond could no longer perform its original service for man, a certain need arose for another bond that could unite larger groups into a unity in its own way. This bond had to be able to encompass the whole life, the whole existence of a group to a certain extent, and so it was quite natural that the need for a governmental authority, which enclosed this circle in itself and separated it from other circles, had to gradually emerge. This would not have been the case if the drive towards sociability could have found complete satisfaction in itself, and if it had not been threatened by disturbing elements. For then everything would have gone of its own accord. Now, however, since what drew, drew deficiently, and the counteraction that threatened disturbance could not fail to occur, it was self-evident that one thought of establishing an external bond, and from this, in whatever deficient form at first, the governmental authority nevertheless emerged in principle. §25. A Second Need. No less powerfully works the second need, which did not arise from our original nature, but from fear of the consequences of sin, which threatened human life from all sides. The image of Abel therefore remains so poignant, because his blood cried out to heaven against the violence of Cain, and because the first murder immediately revealed the abyss of evil that had opened up in the human heart. When the genealogical bond was broken, the need for a power that could curb the outbreak of sin and violence became immediately apparent. The first need was for community, the second need was for protection against the consequences of sin. The first need was positive, the second need was negative. The first need arose from the original nature of man, the second need arose from the dominion of sin. The first need was for the restoration of the original unity, the second need was for the establishment of a new order. The first need was for the realization of the ideal, the second need was for the prevention of the worst. And just as the first need led to the establishment of the family, the second need led to the establishment of government.
