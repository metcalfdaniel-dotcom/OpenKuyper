§1. Essence and Mode of Being. Already since Aristotle, so many different things have been understood under the Ousia or the essence of a thing, that in the end almost everything, even down to a tool or a product of the imagination, was attributed a “essence.” Essence and Existence finally flowed together as concepts of equal content. Leibniz emphasized to prevent this confusion that the essence carries an eternal character. (Nouv. Ess. III.3.§19). Wolff repeated this, and sharpened it further, when he argued that the essence is not only eternal, but also necessary and unchangeable (Vern. Ged. I§40—42.) Kant calls the essence the primary basic concept of all necessary characteristics of a thing. Hegel sees in the essence of a thing only that which is absolutely, absolutely its own. However much these conceptions may diverge, the essence is still understood more and more as that which, by nature, from the outset, according to the first creative thought, constitutes the thing. In this sense, there is indeed an essence in the Family, and likewise an essence in a Race and in a People, but not in what makes a People into a State. For the State, too, the essence lies in the people, but that which makes a people into a State is neither eternal, nor necessary, nor unchangeable, nor inherent in the nature of the people. The State is not an organic unfolding of a creative thought, but a surgically necessary bandage, in which not the inventive genius of the political philosophers, but the Common Grace of the merciful God reveals itself, and which is destined to be completely overthrown with the last day, in order to make the ideal of the world empire and the pax aeterna possible through the kingly appearance of the Christ.

The Concept of the State
The State is not a unity, but a combination—it is a people plus an authority placed over that people, not an authority that has arisen from the people itself. It follows from this that the answer to the question of whether a being may be ascribed to the State depends on whether one currently, as is often the case, sees in the State a form for the existence of a people that necessarily arises from the nature of our human association, or whether the State is considered a form brought in from the outside, which intervened to prevent the complete disintegration of humanity after a breach in the original bond of life. Whoever adheres to the former feeling sees in the State a necessary being that organically arises from the nature of the human race. Those, on the other hand, who can only see in the state-form a surgical apparatus to partially replace the original bond of humanity that was lost through sin, can indeed ascribe a being to the people, but not to the State. The State is to them nothing more than a particular, not eternal and unchangeable form in which the being of the people manifests itself. Dr. R. Redslob rightly says on page 3 of his Dependent States (Leipzig 1914): “To a State belong a people and a land. Further, an authority over that people within the borders of that land is indispensable.” But here we encounter the cardinal concept of the idea of the State. “Here we encounter the phenomenon in which the innermost vital force of the State is contained.” More and more, one is now coming to see in the idea of the State a composite concept and to recognize that the three components of a State are: a people, as possessing a designated portion of the earth’s surface, and governed by an authority. A people with its own land and its own authority lives in a State, and this combination is now generally called a State. One speaks of the State of the Netherlands, the Belgian State, the Danish State, while for very large countries, especially large monarchies, the word “Realm” is preferred. It is therefore on the people in its own land, with its own Authority, that it depends, and in this concept the people appears as the main concept, and only to the people is a being in the proper sense ascribed. It is not a land, with a people under an Authority on that land. Nor is it an Authority in a land with a people under it. Always the people remains the starting point, and its land and its Authority.

§3. The Historical School.
With all appreciation for what now refreshes us in the history of the past and holds us back too quickly in our forward movement, it is still a serious duty to watch that we do not slip back into a past that has had its time and is now out of service. Even the lasting fruit of the past must take on a form for our time. It must always be investigated what in the past ultimately disappointed and caused us to miss the goal we were reaching for. There must be no colorless glorification of history, but never anything other than a warming of ourselves by what from the past can still cherish us, provided that it is always with the sieve of criticism. And this is precisely why it is so absolutely indispensable that we move from the temporary in the general, from the national in the generally human, and from the special in our own sphere of life, and reckon with other experiences. The failure to do this caused so many scholars to run into a dead end in the course of their theories. It is from this that the false Conservatism has arisen, which has caused so many a political party to wither and die after a brief bloom. It is this self-enclosure in the present and in one’s own house that has made so many an otherwise pleasant family provincial. Such an exaggerated love for the historical-special causes one to lose one’s influence on future development, for which, after a period of turmoil, a people that has not extinguished its life still cries out. For this reason, what is called “Allgemeine Staatslehre” must now ripen us, and the comparative science of the state must continually make us receptive to it. Healthy, conclusive criticism is only possible if one climbs from the particular to the general, understands the leading thought in that general, and discovers the goal at which the state life must be aimed.

We remain historical, both because the breach with the past weakens one’s own vital force, and because of the inspiration that still goes out for every Calvinist from the 16th century.
