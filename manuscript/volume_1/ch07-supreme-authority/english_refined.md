Chapter VIII THE SOVEREIGNTY
§1. The word Sovereignty. The concept of Sovereignty is increasingly limited to the governmental authority in a magisterial sense, so that it then becomes merely a modified expression for the authority of the Supreme Authority. We also took the word from the French, and Souveraineté in French was no different than the Latin Superanitas, an ugly, medieval word for Supra-potestas. This expressed that the bearer of sovereignty on earth had no power or authority above it. On the other hand, there was nothing in it of what has later been attempted to put into it, namely, that to this supreme power on earth all things were subject, so that everything had to give way before the sovereignty and the sovereignty of the government never had to yield to anything outside of it. The word would then indicate an authority that remained freely mighty in itself, and had the power to dispose freely of everything that fell within its reach. Against this representation, one cannot protest seriously enough. For this is to transfer to a man what belongs only to God and is never conceivable in any other way than in the Almighty. §2. Two domains of the Sovereignty of God.
In God is the absolute Sovereignty over all that exists, and this for the conclusive reason that all that exists owes its existence solely to God and in its continued existence is just as absolutely dependent on the Almighty power of God.

For power, bound to a rule, law, or order, to be the true Sovereignty, the Creator who has established these and alone possesses the power to maintain what has been instituted and ordained must be the one. If this is the only true Sovereignty, it follows that God alone is Sovereign, and that no human being can ever be sovereign. God may use a human being or a group of humans as an organ for the exercise of His Sovereignty, but whoever acts in this capacity as an organ is never sovereign in any other sense than “by delegation.” However, precisely because God’s Sovereignty is all-encompassing and extends not only over humans but over the entire Creation, and in the human being controls not only their public actions but their entire being, inward and outward, in all their relationships, it is in the nature of things that there can never be a transfer of Sovereign authority from God to any creature, except in a very limited sense. The starting point must therefore be that there is a Sovereign power in God that can never be exercised by anyone other than God Himself. No father or mother has the power to impart to a child that is yet to be born high gifts or talents, or even a special nature. Sometimes one even sees that the father or mother of a child who later reveals itself as a mighty genius were very ordinary, simple citizens. This also applies to the physical realm. A beautiful voice can represent a capital for the singer, but however much a human being may exercise, develop, and bring out what is in it, they cannot create that voice. Even with the boxer who wins the championship through the strength of their fists, the same principle applies. As for rules and order, life exhibits similar phenomena. God’s Sovereignty extends not only over the government of the people, but just as decisively over the exploits in the field of science and the creations in the realm of art. In both of these territories, there are laws ordained by Him, and the coryphæi who produce the results in connection with these laws are often the ones who control a piece of history. It is no different in the technical field. God alone has the power to withhold an important discovery for centuries and then bring it forth at the time He wills; and yet it is not uncommon for such technical inventions to govern the fate of States. Just think of the printing press...

§3. The Transfer of God’s Sovereignty.
In addition to this first distinction, a second one also arises. For it is incorrect to assume that the transfer of divine sovereignty is aimed solely at the government of nations and peoples. The sovereign power that belongs to God over us humans is, in the act of transfer, entrusted to a human being in many different ways for exercise. God’s sovereign power also extends over all inanimate nature, and how great is not the portion of that power that has been placed at the disposal of humanity. Our power over nature by no means covers the entire power of God over the inanimate creation. It is limited mainly to our little planet, and even on this little planet there remains so much for us to investigate. But however this may be, humanity is nonetheless invested with sovereign power over nature to no small degree. And the same holds true for virtually every sphere of life. We are constantly amazed at the majesty of so many actions that now emanate from human beings, and this with the near-certainty that even mightier wonders await us. On the oceans we have become lords and masters. One ascends the Jungfrau with a locomotive. Even the airspace has come under our human sovereignty through aviation. The entire domain of hunting and agriculture, of trade and industry, is unlocking whole new regions for the radiance of human sovereign power. And then there are those peculiar corporations of our human life that require the establishment of statutes, rules, and laws

The sovereignty of these associations is not merely theoretical, but practical. They appoint their own leaders, and within the circle they form, they find the strength for a completely independent administration. Corporations that formally have an authority above them in the law of associations, yet operate entirely within the walls of their own institution. Even the state body is controlled to no small degree by such sovereign associations. In almost every country, one now knows how to exercise such sovereign power in electoral associations. This sovereignty, distinct from the higher authorities, manifests itself even more strongly in the home and the family circle, and most strongly of all in the church of Christ. Dr. Eugene Ehrlich, professor at Czernowitz, recently pointed out in his excellent work on the sociological character of law, how even the political community, if it does not want to disintegrate into atomism, must continually draw from and out of the family. In the family, the sovereign father himself is still clothed with the highest sovereignty, and from the sovereignty of the family, all political sovereignty has ultimately developed in the course of history. When the jus vitae ac necis was still effectively in force in the family, one found in the family the two most powerful expressions of sovereignty—the right to bring forth life, and the right to take it away—united, while the authorities had to limit themselves to the right of destruction. The term jus vitae ac necis, that is, the double right to procreate a child on the one hand (the jus vitae), and to deprive the child of life on the other (the jus necis), was therefore so significant. And what holds true spiritually, but is no less decisive, is the sovereignty in its own circle in the church of Christ. In the struggle over the theory of the “two swords,” the very concept of state sovereignty first came to full development. And although it cannot be denied that the church has not always limited itself to advocating sovereignty in its own circle, and that it has all too often also sought to secure power outside that circle, even through the exercise of judicial authority, it still remains firm for the believer that no earthly authority has been granted the competence to disturb the church in its free following of Christ’s commandments.

§4. Sphere Sovereignty. With utmost seriousness and force, we must therefore not only protest against the alleged omnipotence of the State, but also offer resistance. This claimed omnipotence of the State is the most unbearable tyranny imaginable. A group of men with a lust for power can then, through flattery and seduction, through the promise of benefits, rally the support of the masses behind them, place themselves on God’s throne, and play the Almighty in order to give free rein to their own despotism and greed with the utmost brutality. The Jacobins did no different. And even now, though in a different guise, as the doctrine of the all-powerful State is again gaining ground, we are once more facing the very serious danger that, under the fair-sounding name of state sovereignty, ever more encroachment is being made on our personal and social freedom. It must therefore remain firmly established for us that true sovereignty resides only in God, and that this Sovereignty in God contains and encompasses all, in the most absolute sense, and can be limited or pushed back by nothing. This general, all-encompassing concept of the Sovereignty of God expresses itself in the individual conscience as the right of conscience. For everyone who feels that anything demanded or imposed on them by anyone on earth falls short of God’s honor or majesty, senses that they may not yield to any earthly sovereign who demands this of them, and that they can never subordinate God’s original Sovereignty to the sovereignty that has been transferred to some human being, so that their conscience thereby compels them to refuse obedience. Secondly, the transfer of sovereignty can never take place except in a limited sense, and this transfer never implies that God relinquishes it so that man may exercise it on his own authority. Even in the case of transfer, sovereignty remains vested in God, and man acts only as His servant or instrument. But thirdly, the sovereignty exercised by men through transfer is also divided. It spreads out over various and distinct domains, and takes on a different character in each of these domains. There is the domain of the Family, there is the domain of the Church, there is the

§5. The Domain of Science. This shines so clearly in the domain of the sciences, that no principled opposition to our position is to be feared from that quarter. Science, in its research and its mental operations, obeys laws over which the Government has no say. Logic does not recognize its scepter. In itself, science is absolutely sovereign, indeed with a sovereignty limited and confined to its own circle, but still in such a sense, that the strict demand of its vital law makes itself felt in every domain of human society. This does not in the least take away, however, that the Government has it in its power to intervene in a very sensitive way in the domain of science. What science needs is absolute

As far as this terrain is concerned, the government then has it in its power, through the schools it has founded, regulated and maintained, to ensure, by the appointment of scholars who belong to the direction favorable to it, a propaganda for this direction, with which almost no competition is possible. Where the budgets of the public universities run into the millions every year, private competition becomes almost unthinkable, and the less so as those who would want to introduce a university-level scientific study and training in a divergent direction would first be forced by the government to contribute to the government universities. Especially in our country, this competitive struggle between the free and the official science has developed quite sharply. §6. The aesthetic domain.
The same applies in the aesthetic domain. Beauty forms its own world and can, in its manifestation, be bound only by itself. Hence, in this field too, its own sovereignty must have free course. It must be admitted that the law prevailing here does not have the same strict character as that in the field of science. This is explained by the fact that the world of thought exists in itself, while the world of Beauty does not allow itself to be thought of independently of the perception of the Subject. This even led to the so-called proposition of: de gustibus non est disputandum, expressed more briefly among us as: “tastes differ.” What is only in this, that in lower aesthetic formation the subjective perception is too indefinite to rise to the full awareness of a higher, tone-giving element. However false someone’s taste may be, it is often followed. In higher realms, on the other hand, this purely subjective increasingly recedes, and one recognizes, even in serious disagreement, the sovereignty of harmonies that lie above our reach and are not subject to our taste, but rather rule over us or at least should rule over us. This increasingly leads to the recognition that in the realm of Beauty too there is a law ordained and produced by God, and that no one has the right to present himself as a priest in the realm of Beauty who does not know how to grasp this law of the harmonies of Beauty and unconditionally submit to it. In no domain does the weak man then consciously stand up as autonomous against the government, as in this domain of these divine harmonies. It is even unthinkable that an artist by the Grace of God, who has shown his genius in creations of the first order, would ever yield to a magisterial command.

Under Thorbecke, art was rightly not considered a government affair among us, something that was intended to honor its sovereignty. §7. The religious domain.
Even in the religious domain, this applies, at least in part. Whoever is unprejudiced recognizes unconditionally that the government has to worship in the church and not to rule. Above all, on the sacred domain, the sovereignty in its own sphere must apply unconditionally. In the Church of Christ, Christ alone is King. To say that in the Church the government would have a right in the sense that its magisterial supremacy would also continue in the Church, is the senseless thought that also arose among us after 1619 from the side of the state, but which every Roman Catholic, every Calvinist, and every Lutheran has always rejected as a violation of the sovereignty of the Church.

§8. The domain of the Family. The same applies, albeit in a somewhat more limited sense, to the life of the Family, including the elementary school system and education. The English saying: “My home is my castle,” is the expression of a fundamental truth on which the whole social structure rests. Human rights, neighborhood interests and so much more must also be respected in family life, but family life itself obeys its own law, and indeed a law that God has established over family life, not the authorities. Our civil code may have laid down articles-wise regulations regarding the relationship between spouses, the relationship between parents and children, the regulation of inheritance and so much more, yet it is clear that many of these provisions do not emanate from the authorities, and can no less be maintained by the authorities.

§9. The interference of the Authorities. Yet this does not take away that the authorities can also exert a very extensive influence on family life, even if it is only by prohibiting polygamy and regulating inheritance rights. And the same goes for the whole of life. Agriculture obeys its own laws, which are not determined by the authorities, but by nature, and in that nature God has determined. An authority that would want to force agriculture into the framework of exotic regulations against the sovereign fundamental rules of nature, would ruin the fields and only cause damage. It is no different with the use of flowing water, with all industry and all trade. All these manifestations of our human life must, if they are to thrive, correspond to the rules that are ordained in the nature of the thing for each of these parts of life. No authority has the power or authority to intervene independently here. It has to obey here and not to command. All these domains of human life are not of human invention, but of divine creation, and by that divine creation have been placed in a completely separate framework. It is not man, nor the authorities, who can wield the scepter of sovereignty on each of these domains. That scepter is in the family, in agriculture, in industry, in trade, in shipping, in fishing, God’s and God’s alone, and therefore the “sovereignty in its own sphere” can never be overlooked here. Insofar as all this now proceeds of itself and from its own impulse in Family and Society in conformity with those laws of God, and its sovereignty is respected by all stakeholders, the authorities can keep themselves.

§10. The Rights of Freedom. This principled distinction between the two ways in which absolute sovereignty, which is God’s alone, has found expression in our human life, i.e. on the one hand in the own sphere of each part of our life, and on the other hand in the collective sphere that binds all under one and the same Government, must therefore be emphasized so decisively, because the rights of freedom of the people vis-à-vis the authority of the Government can only be defended on the basis of this distinction. Whoever neglects the background, or if one wishes, the foundation of Divine Sovereignty, and, as it is then said, opposes the binding of Politics to the Supreme Power of God, closes off the only way that can lead to a happy harmony between Government and People in the political field. For if one assumes that the sovereignty of the Government is not derived from God, but rests in itself, then the absolutism of Despots and Tyrants threatens, and the People’s freedom finds no more room for the hollow of its foot. Or also, if one takes the opposite standpoint and derives the highest authority from the will of the people, then the ordering power of the Government completely disappears and a majority from the people, a majority in number or in power, intrudes into the position of the Government, and what was called freedom, under the waving of the Freedom flag, degenerates into the most unbearable tyranny. The contrast between the unity of the whole and the self-will in the individual must be resolved in harmony, and this harmony can never, neither in ideals nor in reality, be grasped, unless both, the individual and the whole, submit to Him who called both into life to glorify His Majesty in both.

§11. No Theocracy. Not long ago, Léon Duguit, the well-known professor of Constitutional Law in Bordeaux, in his “Traité de Droit Constitutionnel” Paris 1913, volume I pp. 24 ff., has designated this system as: Doctrine théocratique and has contrasted it with the liberal system professed by him as Doctrine démocratique (p. 28). In itself, there are no overwhelming objections on our part against such a designation of what we plead for with the name of theocracy. For our conviction is that all αὐθεντία, i.e. all rule that has no other rule above it or tolerates, rests in God and in God alone, and that in every domain of human life, and so also in the political domain, no man can ever be anything other than an instrument of this sovereignty resting in God. This does not take away, however, that it is considered an unhistorical judgment, as our opponents still brand our system with this theocratic explanation today.

—- Page 290 —-

The designation “theocracy” is entirely misleading and attempts to shed false light on the matter. For the name “theocracy” has a history, and in this history it has never meant anything other than that the gods, through signs, oracles, or priests, made known their will in the affairs of the State, and demanded obedience to this revealed will. This conception lived in part in the ancient kingdoms of the East, insofar as in all these kingdoms a certain authority in matters of state was granted to the priests, even if this was not infrequently limited to cases of uncertainty, when the rulers themselves invoked the pronouncement of the gods, through the aid of the priests or augurs. But in essence, the name of theocracy has been used historically exclusively for the regime that stood under the direct leadership of God Himself in Israel. From the calling of Abraham to the destruction of the Temple on Zion by Nebuchadnezzar, and later by Titus, a government has existed in the lineage of Abraham that has occurred nowhere else. This government had a supernatural character, both through the direct revelation of God to the patriarchs and to Moses, as well as through the Urim and Thummim, and the dwelling of God in the midst of His people in the Holy of Holies, soon further strengthened by prophecy. A government like this has never again been exercised. Not even in Israel does it exist anymore. And the great mistake, through which so much misunderstanding and confusion has also arisen among us, lies in this, that one repeatedly applied, in a completely erroneous manner, what was narrated in the Old Testament with regard to that theocratic government, to the government of peoples in general. This is a blunder of which, unfortunately, even Calvinists have not seldom made themselves guilty, and for that very reason we can in no case allow ourselves to be called “theocratic.”

THE ROMAN CATHOLIC CONCEPTION According to Thomas Aquinas, sovereignty is very decisively located in God. Romans 13:1 is not departed from. But, says Thomas, a distinction must be made between three things, namely, between the principle, the form, and the use. And although the principle of magisterial authority is to be derived from God as the Source, yet it stands equally firm that the form which that authority assumes, and the manner in which it operates, originate from the human factor, namely, from the multitude, that is, from the people as a whole. This view is explained on the one hand by the undeniable fact that Thomas was still all too much in the leading-strings of Aristotle, and on the other hand by the tendency which existed in this period of the Middle Ages on the ecclesiastical side to weaken the sovereignty of the Princes for the benefit of Papal authority; and how could this be done more thoroughly than by representing the Princes, in a certain sense, as creatures of the people? Bellarmine and Suarez moved along the same line. “Certainly,” says Bellarmine, “all authority rests in God, but that authority is not laid by God upon a single person, but is deposited in the multitude of the people.” Suarez expresses himself even more decidedly. “Sovereignty rests immediately in the community by the nature of things. If, therefore, the sovereignty is to pass into the hands of a particular person, it is necessary that this person should have received it through the approval of the people.” The revolutionary group of Roman Catholic politicians, who until quite recently had their organ in Le Sillon, and who at bottom merely renewed the ideas of Suarez, has, on the other hand, been sharply condemned by Pope Pius X in his letter of August 25, 1910, and on this ground: “Le Sillon places the public authority originally in the multitude, and allows it to pass from there to the magistrate, in such a way, however, that it always remains fundamentally rooted in the people.” But although the conception of sovereignty undergoes repeated and not insignificant modifications even among Roman Catholic politicians, yet it remains fixed for the bon catholique that the authority of God descends, not merely once and for all to remain either in the people or in a princely house, but continually in every moment, either in the people or in a princely house. Here we are dealing with a problem that has such a strongly pronounced inner contradiction that human thought has still vainly tried to reconcile the contradictory elements, and although the Reformed confession has not hesitated for a moment to express itself more freely and boldly, the Calvinists too did not have the key to this mystery in their hands. §17. In all God’s ordinance.
This is not only so in the matter of government, but it is so in all things. The appointment of a government goes on as humanly as the conclusion of a marriage. The formula “by the grace of God” ought therefore to be applied to all things in our life. He who believes, prays for everything and gives thanks for everything, and also fully recognizes that his life on the one hand lies under his own responsibility, and on the other hand is entirely governed by God’s ordinance. These are the two sides of life, which are distinct at every point, and yet form a unity, only in a way that is inexplicable to us. But although the matter of government does not form an exception, it is perfectly understandable that, in order to designate the highest point of its manifestation, we use the sacred formula with regard to it, which does not otherwise flow from our pen. He who hires a clerk in his office can, by an imprudent choice, completely ruin his business, and yet it is God who has brought that clerk to him. Yet we do not speak of a clerk that he came to us by the grace of God, not because it was not so, but because the use of formulas of that nature is determined by the importance of the matter for which they hold good. Naturally, it is a question of sovereignty whether a midwife shall come to the lying-in chamber and, if so, who she shall be, but of the lying-in woman we do not use such a high word as of sovereignty. Not everything is equal in value and significance, and it is this inequality in the valuation of things that brings with it that we bind ourselves to solemn expressions for the higher, the use of which would be ridiculous on a lower terrain. We therefore continue to adhere to the use of the formula “by the grace of God” where we attach importance to it, and it remains established for us that also in the appointment of the person or persons who are invested with the supreme authority, the divine will’s disposition is to be recognized.

§18. Popular sovereignty.
Above all, one must beware of being misled by a mistaken conception of the concept of popular sovereignty. For if by popular sovereignty is meant a sovereignty that would rest by nature in the people, then the thus conceived popular sovereignty must be combated with all one’s might as the fundamental error of the revolutionary system. If, on the other hand, one wishes to indicate that in a legally constituted commonwealth the sovereignty of Almighty God is instrumentally exercised by the people, then one speaks perfectly correctly, and there is nothing whatsoever to be objected to. The people in America, who during the War of Independence appointed their own magistrates, were the bearer of God’s sovereignty as His organ and instrument, and although it may now lead to confusion to speak in such a case of “popular sovereignty,” yet it is so that even now in the United States of America the divine sovereignty is asserted by the people as its organ.

POPULAR SOVEREIGNTY. To reject, as an offense against God’s Majesty, the notion that popular sovereignty forms no opposition to the sovereignty of princes, but stands on the same level, is a well-conceived view. Sovereignty resides in God and in God alone, but He can take either a prince or the people itself as the organ and instrument for the exercise of this His supreme authority. Whichever choice is made on God’s part, it always remains a delegated and not an original sovereignty. The prince is not himself sovereign, but an organ of God’s sovereignty. And so also, where the people exercises the supreme authority, it is not the people itself that is sovereign, but it merely exercises this authority in God’s Name and on God’s behalf. But this is precisely what the spirit of the Revolution denies and denies again. From 1789 onward, it has constantly been maintained on that side that sovereignty, by nature, in essence and origin, rests in the people. There is then no longer any question of God’s Almighty power. Sovereignty then has nothing to do with God’s Almighty power: not God, but man rules. The cry “No God, no master” tolerates no exception. The people are then their own lord and master, and whoever they appoint to govern is not a ruler over them, but a servant of the people, accountable to the people. This is the deeply sinful conception of popular sovereignty, minted for the overthrow of God’s authority and the exclusion of the Almighty from the entire life of the State. Quite to the contrary, the anathema that rests upon this falsified popular sovereignty must also be pronounced over what is found in a country like the United States, and against this one cannot protest strongly enough. Just as it pleases God to invest a prince, and his successors, with delegated sovereignty, so also God can do the same and has done the same with a people. Not as if in a republican state-form the people possessed sovereignty in and of itself, and now delegated it to the magistrate. Even in a republic, the original Sovereignty remains vested in God and in God alone, but the people are now chosen as the organ and instrument to exercise it, and the magistrate now no longer stands as one who rules by the grace of God over the people, but the people itself possesses the supreme authority to appoint a magistrate, and it is the law that then regulates the fixed relationships between the people and the magistrate.
