§1. Further Elucidation Necessary.
The elucidation of the Program of Principles of the Antirevolutionary Party, which appeared in March 1879 under the title: “OUR PROGRAM,” published in Amsterdam by J.H. Kruyt, was perhaps not entirely unsuitable at the time for meeting a need that had arisen spontaneously, though it was still very limited then. That need arose through the death on May 19, 1876, of the Founder of our party, Master Guillaume Groen van Prinsterer. As long as Groen van Prinsterer remained with us, he was, as the incarnation of the political existence and intention of the Antirevolutionary Party, the natural and life-giving center for our entire group. But when he was taken from us, that group, lacking a bond, would have all too easily fallen apart, and in order to hold it together, Groen’s life-thoughts necessarily had to be translated into a programmatic formula and thereby interpreted for a wider circle. For me, as a non-Jurist, the undertaking of serving as a guide to our Calvinistic people in this matter was—I readily admit it—no small risk. Nevertheless, since no better hand was extended at the time, and since I was able to resume the editorship of *De Standaard* in 1878 after a long illness, I felt called to this task, however difficult. §2. The Concept of Law. Our starting point must be the establishment of Law. Not as if, since Rudolph Gneist advanced the concept of the “Rule of Law State” (*Rechtstaat*), the life of the state could be confined within the legal sphere—and even though criticism of this representation remains a duty—yet, whoever on his part wishes to give direction to the political life among his own people cannot escape the demand to explain the meaning and significance of Law. The derivation of the word provides no definitive indication here. What Ahrens, in his *Droit naturel* I p. 107, cites from the Digests No. 2: “Juri operam daturum nosse oportet, unde nomen Juris descendit,” that is: For sound legal knowledge, it is necessary to understand the origin of the word: Law—does not hold water. Even the attempt by Rudolph von Jhering in his *Zweck des Rechts*, II pp. 12–74, to understand the concept of “morality” from the word is, however ingenious, not to be considered conclusive. And this applies even less to the word Law, inasmuch as the Germanic root: *recht*, the Greek: *dik*, the Latin: *jur*, the Scandinavian: *pravo*, the Gothic: *to//zsas*, and the Hebrew: *mishphath*, diverge too widely not only in form but also in meaning to lead to a unity of thought in the formation or choice of the word for Law. Right is the outflow of a power that establishes or restores a certain order, and its antithesis lies in what is crooked, in what is left, in what is confused, in what is chaotic. Yet the concept of Right did not arise merely as an antithesis. It is entirely autonomous in its origin, and by virtue of that autonomous origin, it created its own antithesis for itself. The concept of Right stands in the same relation as the equally autonomous concepts of the Good, the Beautiful, and the True. The Good, as a concept, indicates the character of the moral sphere; the Beautiful, the character of the aesthetic sphere; and the True, that of conformity with the real; and thus, in the concept of Right, is expressed the existence of what ought to be, as opposed to what may not be. Right, just like the Good, the Beautiful, and the True, is a distinct category of being. Just as the category of the Good itself creates the antithesis of the non-good, that is, of Evil; just as the category of the Beautiful calls into existence the antithesis of the Ugly; and just as the category of the True is unthinkable without the antithesis of the Lie; so too does the category of that which is Right, by its own motion, place Unright opposite itself as the negation that must be destroyed. The Good aims at intention, the Beautiful at form, the True at the identity between seeming and essence; Right, however, derives its significance from the relationships, both between God and the creature, and between the creatures mutually. §3. The Sense of Right. All this would pass outside our consciousness if the spiritual feeling for the antithesis between Right and Unright were not given in our mentality. Just as we received a sense for taste, for smell, for sight, for hearing, etc., so too is there in our mentality a sensation, a consciousness, that reacts to the distinction between Truth and Lie, to that between Beauty or Ugliness, and to what is beneficial or offensive in a moral sense. And in an entirely similar sense, separated from the question of what is substantively right or unright, there is in our mentality a spiritual sense that reacts to the Category of Right or Unright. This is our innate or implanted consciousness of Right or Unright, which, among all four mentioned, manifests itself most strongly in reaction to our perceptions. To what is good or bad, the consciousness of many sometimes reacts very weakly; to what is truth or lie, already much stronger; but the reaction is strongest when one feels that unright is occurring. This cannot be tolerated. Against this, almost everyone rises up spontaneously, and often with tempestuous spiritual fervor. Thus it appears that, entirely apart from any specific content of the Right, we are susceptible to a very strong sensation of right or unright. This sensation as such is an innate property of our human nature. It is inherent to man, apart from all content, and thus also apart from the question of whether we are actually right. §4. The Source of Right. If by knowledge of the Right is meant the knowledge of what is considered valid law in a specific country, in a specific year, with respect to a specific case, then the direct source from which this knowledge flows is the written law, as known from the Constitution, statute, decree, or ordinance; at least in part, not infrequently in connection with a certain *customary law* (gewoonte-recht). However, even this positive law cannot always be known from its sources in a manner impervious to contradiction. The pleading of the bar always attempts to establish and resolve this uncertainty. The decision then rests with the Judiciary in its various degrees, yet this decision by no means always follows the same method. That method can anxiously bind itself to the letter of the statute or decree. It can also, as many now especially desire, detach itself as much as possible from what is written. Often, a middle path is followed. But even in this latter case, a free supplement almost always attaches itself to the written law, which can be derived from very diverse sources. We already pointed to the *customary law* or *convention* (adat). Added to this are, specifically, earlier judicial pronouncements, reference to foreign law, to moral motives, or to the common popular consciousness. Yet even so, the judge’s main task remains not the discovery of the law, but the most accurate interpretation and application of the law already found. The situation is entirely different, however, for the political power which sees itself charged with the task of creating positive law. This legislative and ordinance-issuing power does not find the Right *in* the law, but must bring it *into* the law from elsewhere, and thus also test what has hitherto been considered Right against a touchstone derived from elsewhere. §5. The Right from God. The attempt to locate the source of Right in man—that is, in man’s nature, in the historical development of humanity, and in man’s philosophical, comprehensive thought—an attempt which, since the Renaissance, broke the continuity of the medieval conception of Right and became increasingly dominant in the so-called “modern” development of jurisprudence, is to be explained by nothing other than a self-deception that constantly shifts the problem. He who, with Proudhon, and even more so with Bakunin and Kropotkin, with Netjaschefs, Most, Mühlberger, and Reinsdorf, casts himself into Anarchism and Nihilism, can indeed maintain the proposition: Not from God, but from man! For then not only the ruling power, but also all binding connection, falls away; and nothing remains but the absolutely self-sufficient, solitary individual, who recognizes no right other than the right of his absolutely free will. But then there is absolutely no longer any question of a structure of Right, and there can be no organic cohesion.

§6. Right and Morality. Right and Morality abut one another, and consequently do not always escape mutual confusion. Earlier laws often drew into the administration of justice matters that have now been removed therefrom and referred to the sphere of the moral life; such as, notably, various transgressions in the sexual sphere. With respect to piety, the same phenomenon presents itself. Especially in the Old Testament, various matters were demanded by God as Right, which among us have been referred to the voluntary tribute of piety of spirit. Hence, in the Holy Scripture, matters that, once detached from the bond of Right, diverge into morality and piety, could fall under the single concept of Right—a distinction that then encompasses under morality all that pertains to our relationship to ourselves and our fellow creatures, and honors as piety our correct relationship to God. Morality and piety therefore stand as distinct, yet belonging together in unity, set against the Right among men. This is explained by the fact that Right establishes the framework of our life, separate from our intention, while conversely, morality and piety demonstrate how we shall interact within that framework of Right with a good conscience. The law among men can do nothing other than regulate the external distinct relationships; it therefore limits itself to the establishment of those relationships, insofar as they are *externally* regulable and controllable; and it systematically refrains from all judgment of intention. Whether the law is intended to regulate the mutual relationships from person to person (private law), or to regulate the relationships in the public society of the people under a Government (public law), is not strongly relevant here. Law is always fundamentally distinguished from morality and piety in this: that law never asks for anything other than the discharge of the debt note, entirely indifferent whether one does it wholeheartedly, or indifferently, or even with aversion, whereas all that can be called moral or pious depends, in principle, always precisely upon the intention. The entirely same fulfillment of duty can therefore simultaneously bear a legal character and be moral. He who in Israel brought the tithe of his land, including provisions for widows and orphans, into the gate every third year, and did this without human compassion, solely because the law thus required it, acted exclusively in the realm of law. He who, conversely, did it with gratitude in his heart that he was able to do so, and thus helped the widows out of distress, performed simultaneously a moral deed. By God’s ordinance, the sphere of Law encompasses all that concerning which relationships susceptible to consciousness exist, since it regulates all of them without any exception. Only, that remaining terrain does not fall under the administration of justice maintained by the Magistracy. Over that broad terrain, the administration of justice remains with God—partially now already in the conscience and in the providential connection of suffering to sin—but to come into full scope only at the Last Judgment, insofar as it has not already found its completion through the Justification that faith brings. The human administration of justice, conversely, continues to bear its own distinct type, marked firstly by its very limited scope, secondly by its external form, and thirdly by its coercive character.
