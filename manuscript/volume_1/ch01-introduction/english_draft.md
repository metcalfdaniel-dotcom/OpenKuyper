FOREWORD,
In the autodidact, there is always a streak of the daredevil; and since I never attended a single legal lecture during these six years of my Leiden studies, I already hear the “*Quousque tandem?*” which will likely be shouted at me from the corner of classical jurists as: “What presumption is this!” Yet there is no question of presumption here. It is my unforced life path, which has compelled, rather than merely tempted, me to write this work, by an urge that could not be resisted. My studies had initially moved in a single respect toward the political side. Insofar as scientific participation attracted me, history—and specifically Church History within the historical field—had my love from the start. Professor Dr. M. de Vries, who already, as a lecturer at the Leiden Gymnasium, often gave me inspiring advice, always pointed me toward Church History as the study subject most suited for me, and Professor Dr. J. H. Scholten guided me in the same direction. Especially from Scholten’s side, a certain guidance emanated for me here. If I compared the modern Scholten with the orthodox professors in Utrecht, the interesting phenomenon presented itself that Scholten’s writings offered an overflowing abundance of Calvinistic literature, while scarcity prevailed among the orthodox professors. Scholten moved among us along the same lines as Dr. Alexander Schweizer in Zurich. Schweizer, in his *Glaubenslehre* [Doctrine of Faith], which saw the light in 1844 published by Orell, Füssli & Co. in Zurich, had followed the method of suggesting the historical connection of his own modernism with the ancient Reformed theology by using citations from Calvin, Bullinger, Hyperius, Peter Martyr, Alsted, et al. for every chapter. For predestinarianism, determinism was then substituted, and the identity was found. Since I myself at that time felt most strongly attracted to modernism, and yet cherished warm sympathy from home for that which

The elucidation of the Program of Principles of the Antirevolutionary Party, which appeared in March 1879 under the title: “OUR PROGRAM,” published in Amsterdam by J.H. As long as Groen van Prinsterer remained with us, he was, as the incarnation of the political existence and intention of the Antirevolutionary Party, the natural and life-giving center for our entire group. §2. The Concept of Law. Our starting point must be the establishment of Law. Not as if, since Rudolph Gneist advanced the concept of the “Rule of Law State” (*Rechtstaat*), the life of the state could be confined within the legal sphere—and even though criticism of this representation remains a duty—yet, whoever on his part wishes to give direction to the political life among his own people cannot escape the demand to explain the meaning and significance of Law. The derivation of the word provides no definitive indication here. What Ahrens, in his *Droit naturel* I p. 107, cites from the Digests No. 2: “Juri operam daturum nosse oportet, unde nomen Juris descendit,” that is: For sound legal knowledge, it is necessary to understand the origin of the word: Law—does not hold water. Even the attempt by Rudolph von Jhering in his *Zweck des Rechts*, II pp. 12–74, to understand the concept of “morality” from the word is, however ingenious, not to be considered conclusive. And this applies even less to the word Law, inasmuch as the Germanic root: *recht*, the Greek: *dik*, the Latin: *jur*, the Scandinavian: *pravo*, the Gothic: *to//zsas*, and the Hebrew: *mishphath*, diverge too widely not only in form but also in meaning to lead to a unity of thought in the formation or choice of the word for Law. Ahrens’ well-known view, as if our word for Law were derived from the fact that the straight line is the shortest distance between two points, has therefore found favor with almost no one. “Law” is

Weigand, in his *Deutsches Lexicon* II p. 447, brings us much closer to the truth by pointing out that our *Recht* is connected to the Latin *Rectus*, derived from *regere*, and that it thus contains the idea of ruling—that is, ordering, regulating, and governing; an interpretation confirmed by the word ‘right hand,’ as the opposite of that which is left, something which likewise aims at dominion, e.g., in the saying that Christ is seated “at the right hand of His Father.” The derivation of the word ‘Judge’ also points to a similar significance. Right is the outflow of a power that establishes or restores a certain order, and its antithesis lies in what is crooked, in what is left, in what is confused, in what is chaotic. Yet the concept of Right did not arise merely as an antithesis. It is entirely autonomous in its origin, and by virtue of that autonomous origin, it created its own antithesis for itself. The concept of Right stands in the same relation as the equally autonomous concepts of the Good, the Beautiful, and the True. The Good, as a concept, indicates the character of the moral sphere; the Beautiful, the character of the aesthetic sphere; and the True, that of conformity with the real; and thus, in the concept of Right, is expressed the existence of what ought to be, as opposed to what may not be. Right, just like the Good, the Beautiful, and the True, is a distinct category of being. Just as the category of the Good itself creates the antithesis of the non-good, that is, of Evil; just as the category of the Beautiful calls into existence the antithesis of the Ugly; and just as the category of the True is unthinkable without the antithesis of the Lie; so too does the category of that which is Right, by its own motion, place Unright opposite itself as the negation that must be destroyed. The Good aims at intention, the Beautiful at form, the True at the identity between seeming and essence; Right, however, derives its significance from the relationships, both between God and the creature, and between the creatures mutually. If such relationships do not exist, there is nothing but chaos; but if they do exist, the question automatically arises whether they are the play of chance, or whether they have been instituted and ordered by a power standing above all creatures. And if the latter is true, then the problem comes up for discussion whether the thinking which ordered these relationships, supported by the will that established them, in that establishment

§3. The Sense of Right. All this would pass outside our consciousness if the spiritual feeling for the antithesis between Right and Unright were not given in our mentality. Just as we received a sense for taste, for smell, for sight, for hearing, etc., so too is there in our mentality a sensation, a consciousness, that reacts to the distinction between Truth and Lie, to that between Beauty or Ugliness, and to what is beneficial or offensive in a moral sense. And in an entirely similar sense, separated from the question of what is substantively right or unright, there is in our mentality a spiritual sense that reacts to the Category of Right or Unright. This is our innate or implanted consciousness of Right or Unright, which, among all four mentioned, manifests itself most strongly in reaction to our perceptions. To what is good or bad, the consciousness of many sometimes reacts very weakly; to what is truth or lie, already much stronger; but the reaction is strongest when one feels that unright is occurring. This cannot be tolerated. Against this, almost everyone rises up spontaneously, and often with tempestuous spiritual fervor. Thus it appears that, entirely apart from any specific content of the Right, we are susceptible to a very strong sensation of right or unright. This sensation as such is an innate property of our human nature. It is inherent to man, apart from all content, and thus also apart from the question of whether we are actually right. Someone who is completely mistaken in the conception of his right, and regards as unright what justly befalls him, often reacts to this false sensation of un

§4. The Source of Right. If by knowledge of the Right is meant the knowledge of what is considered valid law in a specific country, in a specific year, with respect to a specific case, then the direct source from which this knowledge flows is the written law, as known from the Constitution, statute, decree, or ordinance; at least in part, not infrequently in connection with a certain *customary law* (gewoonte-recht). However, even this positive law cannot always be known from its sources in a manner impervious to contradiction. The pleading of the bar always attempts to establish and resolve this uncertainty. The decision then rests with the Judiciary in its various degrees, yet this decision by no means always follows the same method. That method can anxiously bind itself to the letter of the statute or decree. It can also, as many now especially desire, detach itself as much as possible from what is written. Often, a middle path is followed. But even in this latter case, a free supplement almost always attaches itself to the written law, which can be derived from very diverse sources. We already pointed to the *customary law* or *convention* (adat). Added to this are, specifically, earlier judicial pronouncements, reference to foreign law, to moral motives, or to the common popular consciousness. Yet even so, the judge’s main task remains not the discovery of the law, but the most accurate interpretation and application of the law already found. The situation is entirely different, however, for the political power which sees itself charged with the task of creating positive law. This legislative and ordinance-issuing power does not find the Right *in* the law, but must bring it *into* the law from elsewhere, and thus also test what has hitherto been considered Right against a touchstone derived from elsewhere. The task thus became, firstly, to know and unlock the sources from which that higher knowledge of the law could flow to us; secondly, to cause the law to flow forth from those sources; and thirdly, to cast this discovered law into the precise form required for the specific people and the particular condition for which it was intended to apply. That this discoverable law is derived from a higher power lies in the nature of the matter and persists, even if the existence of a Higher Being, as the source of all law, is absolutely denied. He who believes and kneels in adoration before a holy God cannot conceive of the ultimate source of Law otherwise than in the order established by this sovereign Supreme Being, an order which transcends all creaturely imagination. He, however, who lacks the joy of such an all-pervasive faith, can place the source of Law nowhere else than in the creature. For him, then, above the obscure reasoning of the solitary thinker stands thinking humanity in its historical development; and above the thinking of ordinary humanity stands the thinking of those who proved gifted with legal genius. If the scientific thought made possible thereby finds data for the Law in human nature; if history gives further definition to these natural data; and if philosophy succeeds in placing these further defined data in such a connection with the entire manifestation of life, and with our knowledge thereof, that the whole makes the impression of a well-integrated structure, or higher still, of a purely functioning organism—then one believes to have found herein that which compensates for the lack of faith, i.e., a higher power which indicates what is right or wrong, and from which the ruler of the people derives the right to subject the people to himself through coercion and the threat of punishment. §5. The Right from God. The attempt to locate the source of Right in man—that is, in man’s nature, in the historical development of humanity, and in man’s philosophical, comprehensive thought—an attempt which, since the Renaissance, broke the continuity of the medieval conception of Right and became increasingly dominant in the so-called “modern” development of jurisprudence, is to be explained by nothing other than a self-deception that constantly shifts the problem. He who, with Proudhon, and even more so with Bakunin and Kropotkin, with Netjaschefs, Most, Mühlberger, and Reinsdorf, casts himself into Anarchism and Nihilism, can indeed maintain the proposition: Not from God, but from man! For then not only the ruling power, but also all binding connection, falls away; and nothing remains but the absolutely self-sufficient, solitary individual, who recognizes no right other than the right of his absolutely free will. But then there is absolutely no longer any question of a structure of Right, and there can be no organic cohesion.

Morality signifies a manner of acting which, given similar circumstances, spontaneously repeats itself and naturally establishes and solidifies itself. Were this only the case within ourselves, then morality would exclusively find its basis in some personal peculiarity and consequently remain limited to that single individual. If, however, it becomes evident through regular interaction with others that a similar manner of acting establishes itself among those others, then that manner of acting acquires something communal. Even he who personally, and of himself, might be less strongly inclined toward it, soon feels moved by the impulse to imitate. Hence, such a manner of acting becomes something into which everyone grows from childhood onward: something from which every deviation is conspicuous, draws the attention of many, and provokes a “finding it strange,” indeed, a condemnation. And in this way, what was initially an involuntary expression of life becomes fixed morality. Hence, this morality, however strictly dominant within its own sphere, can differ significantly from the morality in other spheres. §8. Customs and Social Forms. Alongside these two spontaneous regulations that guide the common life of the people, there now stands the entirely different category of Customs, Manners, and Modes of Intercourse, which in themselves can bear neither a legal nor an ethical character. For these forms of life and interaction do not touch the essence, but only the form of society, even though they may have originally arisen from the necessities of life. The custom of greeting one another on the road—and in that greeting making certain accepted movements with the hand—had its origin in the fear that the approaching person could be a robber or a bandit; the failure to greet could then betray this in time to prepare for defense. Yet after all such danger had passed, these usages remained prevalent, especially in the East; and to some extent they are still maintained among us. The uncovering of the head, the bowing of the face, the greeting with the hand—in other lands even the hand-kiss or the embrace and the kiss on the cheek—all served to give an ordered character to interaction and intercourse. Even with certain animals, one sometimes observes such forms of greeting at their encounters; just think of the cooing with deep neck-bowing among pigeons. And further, the mutual greeting on birthdays or name-days, the announcement of an intended marriage, at a birth or a death, the visits to the newborn or the bereaved, the accompaniment at a funeral, the regular visiting of one another, the offering of small gifts, the leaving of calling cards, the appearing in festive attire at high celebrations, the wishing of a new year, the rising when a woman enters, the going before her, the honoring of good customs in decent and disciplined clothing, the not interrupting someone before they have finished speaking, the referring to oneself as a willing servant, the addressing in writing with an ascribed title, the use of standardized terms of address on the envelope—the rising and stepping aside of the younger—all these are customs and social forms that, without legal or ethical character, nevertheless shape the outward appearance of our social life.
