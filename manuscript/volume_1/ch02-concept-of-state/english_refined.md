§2. In the Middle Ages. When in the Middle Ages the “Holy Roman Empire” sought to introduce a new imperial unity for the entire civilized world, the tendency naturally arose to regard the various Christianized lands as dependent on this Imperial Empire. But soon resistance arose, especially from the side of France and England, and the centuries-long struggle between the Guelfs and Ghibellines began to strengthen this resistance, although there was as yet no question of a fully equal standing alongside the Holy Roman Empire. The idea of the unity of Christendom could not yet be abandoned, even after the defection of the Eastern Church. There was as yet no thought of the idea that Europe would consist of a broader series of equal kingdoms, and above all, the pressing advance of Islam, and especially of the Turks, was the reason why one refused to dissolve the unity into a multiplicity of kingdoms. Not least the Crusades upheld the idea of unity, so that the need for a common term for the political association of the various independent peoples was still lacking. It was only in Italy that the unity gradually crumbled as a result of the conflict between Emperor and Pope, and no less did the rise of urban city-states under the influence of the Renaissance favor this new state of affairs. Pisa, Genoa, Venice, and many other cities sought to realize a condition similar to that which had once existed in Athens and elsewhere, with the city as the center of a kind of dependent political territory. In Latin, this was then called, as of old, a respublica, or also a civitas, and only when Latin began to lose its monopoly as the official language and the language of scholarship did one take recourse, in order to express what was common to all these Italian republics, to the word état.

In the sense in which we now speak of “the States of Europe,” and even of “the States of the world,” the name “State” was hardly used at all among us, except under the direct influence of Italian or French writers. Alongside the States General, the name of the Provincial States still lives among us, and the Provincial States are again distinguished from the Deputed States as a moderamen. These now characterized expressions all point back to the ancient meaning of the word State, which was connected with status. It then referred to the distinct groups in the population that held an independent position of power, and the name of the state passed over to the person who represented the position of power of the group. The person was thus not a State in his own right, but by virtue of a right of representation or mandate. Something that must always be kept in mind is that the Latin Status, from which our state is derived in all these distinct meanings, by no means meant a territory or estate, but originally nothing other than condition. One still speaks of the state of death, of a defective state, of the state of marriage, etc., in exactly the same sense. These groups in the population that held a higher and more or less independent state within the whole of the people, then formed a distinct or separate state, and this expression of state passed over by itself to him who represented this position, this higher state, this state and the relative power of his group. There was certainly a concept of partial independence expressed, almost always in relation to equal powers of other estates or groups. The power arose from the partial independence of the distinct groups, estates, cities or provinces, but could only be exercised in the mutual bond of the joint States. Now that later the unity of Christendom, as it had existed and flourished under the Popes and the Emperor, had fallen apart, it was quite natural that the distinct peoples of Europe were each intent on acquiring their own national position, and that in this name of State the bond was expressed in which these “states” as forming the European population belonged together and were designated for international mutual relations.
