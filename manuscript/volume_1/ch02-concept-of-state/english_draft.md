In the age of chivalry, most of these forms became firmly established, and among the military they retained their old formality. General civilization tends to ever more widely and deeply extend these forms of civic right. Yet they do not always do this to the fullest extent. The purely external character of these forms often leads to the fact that there is no inner disposition corresponding to them. Hence, they often appear to be without substance, verging on palpable falsehood. All kinds of forms of showing respect go hand in hand with rancor and bitterness in the heart. An endless stream of compliments for the woman, but with lust in the eye and betrayal of the first principles of morality in the soul. Praise and honor bestowed as long as you are honored, only to be smeared and besmirched behind your back as soon as you turn around. And yet, though one clearly sees these dark sides, the good side of these noble forms must not be overlooked. In rougher circles, the same envy and rivalry, the same cunning machination, and the same sensual passion prevail, but envy expresses itself in outbursts, machination in open attacks on the foundations, and passion in shameful assaults even in public. And precisely for this reason, one must not overlook how the noble forms of social intercourse enhance personal safety, exorcise coarseness, develop the sense of justice, and awaken the moral consciousness. Stepping aside for an older person, or a person in a higher position, deepens your own and the other’s right, leads to honoring the woman in her distinctive forms, refines the social life between the sexes, and offers a safeguard against what is low and common by elevating one’s own sense of honor. And even though the damage done to sincerity by these forms cannot be denied in many respects, the gains that stand against it are by no means nullified by that damage, and a comparison of country with country proves sufficiently that rougher forms just as decisively corrupt the national character as nobler forms often morally strengthen it. Just compare Belgium with the negro life in the Congo State, life in England with that among the Zulus in South Africa. In refined forms of social intercourse there speaks a more mature human existence, and it is precisely to this maturation that the gradual adoption of a higher standpoint is connected. Of course, this would stand out even more clearly if here too the after-effects of our fall into sin did not creep in. Then indeed the forms of custom and convention would be just as varied as they are now, but would constantly spring from the root of our common human nature in a pure way, and the good in the forms of social intercourse would be just as safeguarded against adulteration by mere appearance. But even though under many flaws and many temptations to sin, the Common Grace has also here worked to avert corruption, and has enriched us in the course of history with a culture that ever more nobly unfolds.
