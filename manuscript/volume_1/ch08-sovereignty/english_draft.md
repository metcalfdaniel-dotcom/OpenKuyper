§6. Four meanings of “people.”
It is also not open to dispute that the word “people” has gradually acquired three distinct meanings. To be complete, we would even have to say four, but the “people” as opposed to the aristocracy can remain outside the discussion here. The Roman used in this sense the word plebs, which, as we saw, is of the same root as populus. Even the title of the social-democratic organ: “The People,” reminds one of this conception. Then it can first of all mean: that part of humanity which, by origin, character and language, forms a completely separate group within the human race, as when we speak of the Chinese, the Negroes, the Mongols, etc. Secondly, “people” can mean a group of similar character, but which nevertheless forms only a part of what the word “people” denoted in the first-mentioned sense, as is very strongly the case with that group of Germans who live in Germany and yet by no means make up the whole of Germandom. And in the third place, the use of the word “people” then comes into play when referring to the inhabitants of one and the same State, entirely apart from their origin. So the King of Belgium in a proclamation will address the combined inhabitants of his country, or if one wishes, his combined subjects, as “my people” or “mon peuple,” even though it is also firmly established that the Belgians are of two different origins. For “nation,” as we have already remarked above, derived from nascor, natus, points much more strongly than the word “peuple” to unity of origin. Without fear of contradiction, it must in any case be established that the use of the word “people” for the combined subjects of an entirely diverse origin is metaphorical, and that, on the other hand, the only correct and original application of it refers to the division of our human race into physically cohesive groups or sections; so that it has only depended on the historical course of events whether such a section has remained united as a whole or has gradually become divided into distinct sub-divisions, in order then to give the name of “people” once again, though in a modified sense, to these separate sub-divisions of such a physical main division of our race. With regard to their origin, the Scandinavians form one people, but in the course of history they have continually split up into two, and now again into three, peoples.

Then, all too quickly, such a profound difference in consciousness, in worldview, manifests itself, that the race can no longer remain united and must diverge in the variations of peoples. It is precisely the richness of social life that necessitates this. Now if this spreading in variations had been able to take its regular and undisturbed course, then our entire human race would have fitted itself into the divisions of family, clan, tribe, and people, and this distribution would have taken place independently but yet in a similar manner among each of the races. Family, clan, tribe, people—it would all have remained pure and unmixed, just as one still finds it in some high Alpine valleys, where for centuries, almost entirely separated from the world, a single pastoral or agricultural family has settled and remained isolated as a family in itself. But precisely this orderly distribution of variations has been disturbed in all kinds of ways, and thereby the genealogical unity of the peoples has suffered damage, both through admixture with foreign elements and through the splitting of what was originally one.

§9. Language.
The unity of a people expresses itself most clearly in the unity of its language; and above all, when that language upholds the people, then it comes to the aid of its significance for world life. The Englishman, the Frenchman, the German does not need to call upon Esperanto as a world language to feel at home in the great world traffic. Above all, the Englishman smiles when asked why he does not speak foreign languages. Belgium, however small, feels just as much at home in the massive world movement, because practically every Belgian who leaves his country speaks French fluently and easily, and thanks to this linguistic advantage can get by everywhere. So you can also see that in all European tasks, of an ethnological, sociological or statistical nature, little Belgium always counts just as much as the great states, just as Switzerland does, while we, with the Scandinavian peoples, are hardly ever taken into account.

We owe to the small domain of our language undoubtedly great national strength, but in the community of nations, it often limits our position in a painful way. Yet, precisely because of this, the fact that we are one distinct people and that this is undoubtedly established by our origins and our past, stands firm. It is admitted that the state structure in which we live protects and strengthens the peculiarities and the independent character of our people. But the possession of our own language alone provides the decisive and conclusive proof that we, even apart from our state connection, still form a distinct people, with our own worldview and our own conceptual world, alongside the other peoples of Western Europe. “The language is the whole people!” may not be expressed too strongly for the thoughtful observer. At least Austria and Belgium show it differently. But it cannot be denied that the possession of our own language, which makes all fellow countrymen understandable to us and us to them, points to a unity of inner being that binds us together in a much stronger life connection than the narrowest state connection could ever do. Of course, this does not prevent that in our community, party after party also sets itself against each other in the field of language, and the sharpest differences are delineated. The rose is a plant of a completely independent nature, something which, however, does not prevent this plant from displaying the splendor of its inner life in a luxuriant wealth of variations. It is the peculiarity that matters, and precisely on the possession of this peculiarity in the character of the people, our national existence may still be proud. Language is the conscious expression of the unconscious life. An own language always shows that the group that speaks it, in its inner consciousness, is distinct from what lives in other groups, and that the ability to give expression to that particular language to the inner being, is the common possession of all who belong to our people and make up our people.
