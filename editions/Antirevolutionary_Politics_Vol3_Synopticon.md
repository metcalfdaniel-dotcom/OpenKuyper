# Part IV: The Synopticon (Great Ideas)

> This Synopticon maps the 102 Great Ideas of the Western World (Adler) to the text of *Antirevolutionary Politics*.

## ANGEL

### The Heavenly Host

Kuyper affirms the reality of the angelic realm as part of the cosmos created by God. In his political theology, angels represent the unseen spiritual order that parallels the earthly administration. He occasionally references the 'angel of the nations' (Daniel) to suggest that national destinies are intertwined with spiritual conflict, though he warns against speculative angelology that distracts from the Word.

## ANIMAL

### Creation's Lower Tier

Animals, for Kuyper, are fellow creatures under the dominion of man, but distinct in dignity. The state has no direct jurisdiction over the animal kingdom, but it has a duty to suppress cruelty (vivisection controversies) as a violation of the moral order. He often uses animal metaphors (the 'organic' life of the beehive) to illustrate social cohesion.

## ARISTOCRACY

### The Rule of the Best

Kuyper is critical of hereditary aristocracy when it implies a caste system that obscures the equality of all men before God. However, he speaks of a 'spiritual aristocracy'—the elect or the gifted—who are called to lead not by right of blood but by right of service. He contrasts the fossilized European nobility with the dynamic leadership required in a Calvinistic democracy.

## ART

### The Aesthetic Sphere

Kuyper famously championed the liberation of Art from the Church. In his view, Art has its own 'Sphere Sovereignty' and direct relationship to God (Common Grace). It doesn't need to be 'ecclesiastical' to be pious. The State must respect the freedom of the Arts, neither censoring them (unless for public morality) nor co-opting them for propaganda.

## BEING

### Ontology of Grace

### The Prime Mover

All secondary causes ultimately rest in the First Cause, which is the Will of God. In politics, this means no historical event is accidental. The 'Revolution' was not a mistake but a judgment. ## CHANCE

### No Accident

In a Calvinistic worldview, 'Chance' does not exist. Every lot cast into the lap is from the Lord. Paradoxically, this fatalism does not lead to passivity but to action, as the statesman knows he is an instrument of a sovereign plan, not a victim of random fortune.

## CHANGE

### Evolution vs. Unfolding

Kuyper distinguishes between 'mechanical' change (revolution) and 'organic' change (evolution/unfolding). True progress is the gradual unfolding of the germ of life planted by God. Revolution is a rupture; Reformation is a restoration of the proper line of change.

## CITIZEN

Citizenship is not merely a right but a high 'office' (ambt). In a constitutional state, the citizen shares in the sovereignty. ## CONSTITUTION

### The Covenant of the Nation

A Constitution is the codified conscience of the nation. Kuyper revered the Dutch Constitution but believed it must historically evolve. He opposed the 'Paper Constitution' mentality of the French Revolution, where laws are invented *ex nihilo*. A true constitution reflects the historic rights and organic character of the people.

## COURAGE

### The Calvinist Virtue

Political courage springs from the fear of God, which extinguishes the fear of man. Kuyper exemplifies this in his 'stump' speeches—daring to defy the liberal elite. He calls for a 'holy boldness' in the public square, refusing to be intimidated by the spirit of the age.

## CUSTOM AND CONVENTION

### The Law of Habits

Custom is the 'common law' of social life. Kuyper respects custom as the deposit of historical wisdom (Antirevolutionary), yet he subjects all custom to the critique of Scripture. 'Custom' can never legitimize sin; however, the State should modify custom only slowly and organically, not by decree.

## DEMOCRACY

### Organic vs. Arithmetic

Kuyper is the father of Christian Democracy in Europe but a fierce critic of 'Jacobin' democracy. He rejects the 'arithmetic' view (50% + 1 determines truth) in favor of 'organic' democracy, where the people's voice is heard through their natural corporations and estates. He supports universal suffrage not because the masses are sovereign, but because sovereignty resides in the 'people' under God, not in the King alone.

## DESIRE

### The Motor of Action

Desire is neutral; its direction determines its morality. The Revolution unleashes the 'desires' of the flesh (materialism). The Christian life redirects desire toward the Kingdom. Politics must account for human desire (self-interest) and check it, rather than presuming a utopian altruism.

## EMOTION

### Heart vs. Head

Kuyper distrusts the 'cold rationalism' of the Enlightenment. He appeals to the 'mysticism' of the heart. In politics, he knew how to stir the emotions of the masses, but insisted that emotion must be disciplined by Principle (Beginsel). Unbridled political emotion leads to the Terror.

This eschatological horizon prevents the State from becoming an idol (Totalitarianism) and gives the believer patience in defeat.

## GOOD AND EVIL

### The Moral Antithesis
